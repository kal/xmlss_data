monument_desc A Norman building, interpreted as a church, was identified by archaeological excavation in advance of redevelopment of the site of the church of St. Augustine the Less in 1982. The elements recorded were fragmentary but it was possible to estimate the size of the building which measured approximately 22 metres east to west by 8 metres north to south and that it was had two-bays. The line of the walls was represented by stone-robbing trenches but the door jambs survived to a height of 0.6 metres above the level of the foundations. The building was constructed of Brandon Hill Grit with stones forming the edges of the walls and a rubble construction in the core. The remains of ceramic jars were found inside the church and these were interpreted as acoustic jars set in the floor. The building was later incorporated into the new structure when the church was substantially rebuilt in the late medieval period and the remaining elements were badly damaged by rebuilding of the later church and by the insertion of burial vaults.
Dickinson (1976) in his reinterpretation of the documentary history of the foundation of St. Augustine's Abbey c.1140 suggested that a small church was sited at St. Augustine the Less to house the regular clergy of the monastery until the larger church had been built.
The monument was completely destroyed by the construction of the extension to the Swallow Royal Hotel in the late 1980s.
