monument_desc A round barrow on Kingsweston Hill. The monument is 36 feet in diameter by 18 inches high with no ditch. It was excavated in 1924 by Tratman. A child's tooth was the only human remains. Two small bronze flakes, possibly part of a blade-like implement was taken by Tratman to indicate a Bronze age date for the original interment, but in his general conclusions he states that this and other barrows (SMR 761; 783; 784) are Iron Age in date. A few potsherds found in the barrow were dated by the British Museum as transitional Bronze Age/Iron Age. Other finds include a Roman blue glass bead, part of a Saxon bronze fitting, dated to about 600 AD. All finds are in the University of Bristol Spelaeological Society Museum. Though inconclusive, the excavations suggest a late Bronze Age date for the barrow, with other material in a secondary position. Green briefly reviewed the dating evidence available for the barrows at Kingsweston Hill in considering a cairn he had excavated at Tickenham. On the basis of a re-examination of Tratman's pottery finds, which appeared to be from collared urns, Green concluded that "in the absence of any structural grounds for considering the Kings Weston Hill barrows to be of Iron Age date" the Kingsweston barrows were of Early Bronze Age origin (Green 1972-73, 43-44). Tratman replied to Green's reinterpretation, arguing that the interpretation of the pottery as Iron Age was questionable, the monuments had not necessarily been disturbed to any great extent in antiquity and that the argument that absence of structural grounds for an Iron Age date leads to the inference of a date in the Early Bronze Age was "highly specious". Tratman concluded that there was no good reason to redate the Kingsweston barrows until fresh evidence became available (Tratman 1975, 56-57).
