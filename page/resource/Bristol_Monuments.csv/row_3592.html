monument_desc Horfield Prison, located to the west of Gloucester Road. Construction of the prison began in 1875 but work was suspended in 1877 after the boundary wall had been completed in anticipation of the passage of the Prisons Act which transferred the administration of all prisons to a new central authority, the Prison Commission. Construction was resumed in 1882, apparently to a new design by the Surveyor of Prisons, Alexander McHardy. The prison is thought to have opened in 1883 although building continued until 1889 (Brodie et al., 2002 150).
The prison held both male and female prisoners until 1921 and had accommodation for 188 men and 60 women. The sexes were separated and the women's access to the chapel, for example, was via a covered passage. There was a four-storey women's wing (now F Wing) and the female hospital and reception block and the laundry (now the "Time" workshop) were attached to this. The male wing (now A/G Wing) was a four-storey brick structure with the administration block and chapel at its east end. This is a two-storey structure in an Italianate style with a central arched entrance reached by steps and a square tower at the south-eastern corner (Brodie et al., 2002 150-157). The prison appears to have been extended at its eastern end at some time after the First World War. The National Monuments Record holds a file on Horfield Prison (NMR File No. 93741).
