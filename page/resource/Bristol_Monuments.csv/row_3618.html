monument_desc The memorial to the 79th Regiment, Clifton Down. The war memorial, built of Portland stone, was erected in 1766 by Lieutenant-General Sir William Draper (1721-1787) in the garden of Manilla Hall, Draper's home, to commemorate the role of the regiment he commanded in the campaigns of the Seven Years War (1759-1762), including an expedition which captured Manila from the Spanish in October 1762 (Dreaper 1998, 1; 8-9).
The monument is a sarcophagus with scrolled brackets at the corners surmounted by an urn with a flame and set on a rectangular plinth with angle buttresses. It is constructed of Portland stone with purple slate plaque set in the south-east face. A Latin inscription on the monument was drafted by the poet Christopher Anstey (1724-1805), a friend of Draper (Dreaper 1998, 11). The memorial was removed to its present position in 1882, after the intervention of Dr. John Beddoe (1826-1911), former President of the Anthropological Institute and a local antiquary, when the construction of Manilla Road threatened its destruction (Merritt 2002, 18-19; Parker 1914, 8).
The memorial has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building No. 901-1/7/755) and is described in the listing as "a very early war memorial."
