monument_desc St. James Church. The church is the former church of the Benedictine priory of St. James, founded in 1129 as a cell of Tewkesbury Abbey.
Late C12 five-bay nave of stout columns with 4 half-round minor shafts; wide, scalloped capitals, and square-section semicircular arches and lozenge hoodmoulding which runs on into the east wall; ornamented string course below deeply splayed clerestory windows
C12 west end has clasping buttresses to the lower rubble section and a much restored segmental-arched doorway within a round arch; above is a sill band to the ashlar upper part, which contains a fine arcade of interlacing arches pierced by 3 round-arched windows matching those at the east end, the middle one wider; between the tops of the buttresses is a string, and a c.1160 wheel window of plate tracery forming 8 circles around a central one within a zigzag ring, but the interlacing moulding has mostly disappeared; above is an upper string and an arrow slit; wide, gabled north aisle with a semicircular-arched window to the south gable.

After the dissolution of St. James, the advowson of the rectory was acquired by Henry Brayne as part of his purchase of the St. James property in 1544.
The 5-bay C19 north aisle is 2 bays wide: an arcade of red granite columns, probably following the line of the old aisle, and respond shafts in the north wall, wide capitals with undercut foliage, with shallow pointed arches, and half-arches to the nave.
The south aisle has fine painted corbels to a timber pitched roof. Restored west window.
Large late C19 pulpit; C19 Norman-style font with zigzag moulded column base; wrought-iron altar railing, choir stalls with poppy heads, plain pews with doors.
