monument_desc Redlandhill House, located on the southern side of Redland Hill. The house was constructed as a pair of semi-detached villas in the eighteenth century. The listing gives James Bridges as the architect of the houses and 1761 as the date of construction. This has been disputed by Evans (2000) who suggests that the house was in fact constructed by a Bristol mason, Philip West, in late 1767 or 1768.
The buildings were of three-storeys. Photographs in Bristol Reference Library of the Redland Hill (north-west facing) elevation of the southern of the two buildings (B21205) and the entire south-west facing elevation (B21205) records that the structure was rendered with a three-sided bay at the south end. Immediately to the north of this was a projecting entrance porch of classical design. Above the central doorway was a segmental arch supported by pilasters with windows between them. In the parapet above the arch there was a pediment.. A string course dividing the ground from the first floor extended around the whole building. All of the windows were 3 by 4 pane sashes, those in the second floor being slightly smaller. The window openings on the north-east facing elevation were surrounded by mouldings while those in the rear, south-west facing elevation were had ashlar rustication. A tall parapet surrounded, and obscured the roof but there was a large chimney stack at its centre.
The buildings were extant at the time of the Ordnance Survey first edition 1:500 plan (Bristol Sheet LXXI.12.17), surveyed in 1881. The southern of the houses was demolished in 1933 while the northern is extant, having been purchased by Bristol City Council in 1964 to provide accommodation for Redland College. The building subsequently became part of Bristol Polytechnic. It appears from the Ordnance Survey Landline plan of 2000 that part of the rear wall of the southern building may survive on the site, having perhaps been reused as a retaining wall.
The surviving half of the building has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/34/1725).
