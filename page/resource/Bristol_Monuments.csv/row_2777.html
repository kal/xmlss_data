monument_desc No.35 Prince Street, located on the west side of Prince Street. The building was a three-storey, five-bay house constructed in 1740 by altering and extending the earlier "Great House" belonging to John Day. This was purchased by Richard Bayly in 1740 who obtained a new lease from the Bristol Corporation in June 1741. Ison suggested that the house may have been designed by the architect William Halfpenny (the professional name used by Michael Hoare) (Ison, 1952 172; Gomme et al., 1979 141). The main, east-facing, elevation was classical in style. The ground floor was rusticated with a central entrance doorway with a segmental pediment supported by semi-circular pilasters with bands of vermiculation. On either side of the doorway there were two segmental-headed window openings which contained four by six pane sashes. A plat band divided ground- from first-floor, and above were five tall sashes under alternate segmental and pediments. The window openings at second floor level were surrounded by moulded stonework and contained four by three pane sashes. Above there was a cornice, and a tall stone parapet with five panels. There were chimneystacks at the centre of the north and south ends of the roof.
Bayly died soon after completion of the building and it was sold in July 1742 when it was described as a "Great House - with a Freestone Front, Marble Chimneypieces, wainscotted and very handsomly furnished, with convenient Cellarage, Coach House, both sorts of Water, &c." (quoted in Ison, 1952 172).
The original internal organisation of space is not currently certain but by the early 1920s there were two linked reception rooms occupying the full width of the front of the building, overlooking Prince Street. Parts of the original panelling and chimneypieces also survived (Winstone, 1983 pl.173).
The building had been renumbered to No.40 Prince Street and was occupied by the Port of Bristol Sanitary Office by the early 1920s (Dening, 1923 pl.31). It was recorded by Dening in the early 1920s (BUAD 4105; Dening, 1923 99-101).
The building was destroyed by bombing in 1940.
