monument_desc Castle Sugar Refinery, Queen Street. The refiner was located on the west side of Queen Street and had frontages on the Avon and the surviving uncovered stretch of the castle moat. The refinery was bought by Wills, Young, and Co. in July 1870 after the failure of the joint-stock company which had perviously owned it. The new owners invested in the business, installing additional boilers and charcoal filters.
The process by which the sugar was refined was described in an article published in the Bristol Times and Mirror and reprinted in 1883 (Anon, 1883). The entrance to the works was on Queen Street and the raw sugar was bought into the first floor melting room, on the north side of the works by Queen Street bridge, where it was dissolved in water and boiled in "blow up" pans. The syrup was then released through a pipe into the room below where it was cleaned and decoloured through charcoal filters. The syrup was then pumped into vacuum pans for further heating and then cooled and the various grades of sugar crystals produced by spinning in centrifuges at 800 rpm. The site is now occupied by an office block built in the 1980s which is semi-basemented in part. No elements of the refinery are known to survive above ground and the archaeological stratification of the site is likely to have suffered at least some disturbance during the construction of the office block.
