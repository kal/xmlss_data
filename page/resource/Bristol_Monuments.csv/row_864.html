monument_desc No.40 High Street. The building was in existence by 1261 and by 1306 was described as comprising a messuage and cellar (Leech, 1997 80). It formed part of Cooks' Row by the mid-fourteenth century and was acquired by Thomas Stamford in 1414. Stamford granted it to Agnes Knight in 1440, the cellar being described as newly rebuilt and annexed (ibid.). By the mid-fifteenth century it was owned by Agnes Fylor, who also held the lease of No.39 High Street. She bequeathed the property to her son Thomas, a London mercer, in her will of November 1467. Under the terms of her will the property passed to her daughter Joan at her son's death, and at Joan's death to the vicar and churchwardens of All Saints (Burgess, 2004 15-16). Nos.39 and 40 together appear to have formed The Rose inn from 1467 and the All Saints churchwardens leased No.40 to John Hooper, a vintner, in 1525. Hooper retained the property until 1564 when it was in the possession of John Banting. In 1577 it was granted in fee farm to Philipp Langle, grocer.
The building retained an arch-brace roof with plain windbraces in the 1820s (BRSMG M2388) which was still extant in 1850 (Pryce, 1850 221-222).
