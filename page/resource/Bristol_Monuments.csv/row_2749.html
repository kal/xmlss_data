monument_desc Jewish Cemetery located on the western side of Oakdene Road (formerly Cemetery Road). The cemetery was established in 1898 in a triangular plot of land covering an area of 860 square metres. There was a cemetery chapel, measuring 5.35 metres from south-west to north-east by 4 metres wide, at the centre of the site (ST 6197175231), and was enclosed by a 1.5 metre high Pennant sandstone rubble boundary wall. Human remains and headstones from the burial ground at Rose Street, Temple Meads were reinterred at the cemetery in the 1920s when the Rose Street burial ground was cleared.
An adjacent rectangular parcel of land of 800 square metres on the north side of the cemetery was acquired and was incorporated into it in 1933. The original cemetery chapel was demolished and a new chapel was built between January and May 1933. This is a single-storey rendered building with a clay tile hipped roof. There are double timber panelled doors in the north-east and south-west faces of the chapel. Foundation stones were laid at the north and south corners of the north-east facing elevation by Lewis Aaron Melcher and Abel Mass. A stone plaque on the south side of the western doors commemorates the opening of the chapel and the extension to the cemetery on 28th May 1933:
"THIS MEMORIAL HALL AND / CEMETERY EXTENSION WAS OPENED BY / JOSEPH STEINBERG ESQ PRESID[EN]T / OF THE BRISTOL JEWISH BURIAL SOCIETY / AND CONSECRATED BY / RABBI HARRIS SWIFT / MINISTER OF THE BRISTOL HEBREW CONGREG[ATIO]N / 28TH MAY1933 - 3RD SIVEN 5693." Above the entrance there is a plaque bearing an extract from Chapter 1 Verse 21 of the Old Testament Book of Job ("THE LORD HATH GIVEN AND THE / LORD HATH TAKEN AWAY: BLESSED / BE THE NAME OF THE LORD") in Hebrew and in English.
A new entrance was established to the north of the original entrance and wrought iron gates were donated in memory of Gita Miel by her husband and children.
The cemetery has a section reseerved for Cohanim (a direct male descendant of Aaron, brother of Moses, with a distinct personal status within Judaism) and another for suicides. Inside the chapel there is a memorial plaque for Jewish soldiers who were killed during the First and Second World Wars. The cemetery is owned by the Bristol Jewish Burial Society (Chevrah Chadisha).
