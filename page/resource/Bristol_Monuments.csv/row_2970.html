monument_desc The Carmelite friary in St. Augustine's Place. The friary was founded in 1256 and was a walled precinct with a rectangular plot on the north side of St. Augustine's Place (now Colston Street), probably defined by Pipe Lane, Host Street and Steep Street.
In the absence of archaeological excavation, the internal arrangement of the site remains uncertain but the church and burial ground seem to have been at the south-western end and may have been orientated parallel with Pipe Lane. Evidence for the form of the church itself is equally limited. William Worcestre, writing in 1480, gives two, slightly different, sets of measurements for the nave, 24 and nearly 29 metres long by 13.4 or 14.5 metres wide (Neale 2000, 50-51; 278-279). The choir was 24 metres long (Neale 2000, 50-51). By the early sixteenth century there was "a chapell and an yle off the Church" (Weare 1893, 74). The chapel may have been that dedicated to St. George, known to have stood in the church by 1412 (Wadley 1886, 89), although there was also a chapel dedicated to St. Mary by 1393 (Wadley 1886, 51). The tower of the church was just under 2.7 metres wide and, with the spire, Worcestre says, was 200 feet high (Neale 2000, 100-101). There were three large tracery windows at first-floor level on the Colston Street frontage (BRSMG M2531; BRSMG M2532).One architectural feature of the church to have survived the 1530s may be the screen bequeathed by Thomas White in 1542 to Bristol Cathedral. Warren identified the remnants of this as the heavily-restored late-medieval screen now set in the fourth bay of the south choir aisle (Warren 1904-08; Warren 1904).
The spatial organisation of the precinct is also uncertain. Worcestre recorded the cloister as 21.4 metres square (Neale 2000, 50-51) although its position within the precinct is unclear. The north-eastern end presumably consisted of ancillary buildings and orchard and gardens. The friary obtained its water supply from the St. John's conduit which ran along Pipe Lane and then Host Street. Worcestre also mentions a statue of St. Mary in a shrine in the precinct wall near the corner of Host Street and Steep Street (Neale 2000, 18-19). The friary appears to have had gardens extending westward towards Park Row.
The friary was surrendered on 28 July 1538 and the site was bought by the Corporation. The buildings were demolished and provided stone for rebuilding altars in several churches. A Great House (BUAD 420M) was subsequently built on the site by Sir John Young.
There have been numerous discoveries of human remains around Pipe Lane, the earliest in April 1788 (BUAD 3666; Gentleman's Magazine 1788, 455). In August and September 1904 John Pritchard recorded (BUAD 46) several sections of what he believed were medieval walls as well as a number of burials (Beddoe 1906; Pritchard 1906, 133-141). In November 1932 several more skeletons and a pavement of medieval floor tiles were found during excavation in Trenchard Street (BUAD 3308) and disarticulated human remains were discovered inside No.2 Trenchard Street in 1999 (BUAD 3464). In 1989 John Bryant recorded a window of late-medieval date about 1800 millimetres high internally in the front wall of No.2 Trenchard Street (BUAD 622).
