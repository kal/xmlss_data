monument_desc A Romano-British settlement at Filwood Park. The site was discovered during levelling of the ground in Filwood Park in 1982 and the exposed remains were subject to archaeological excavation that year by R. G. J. Williams (Williams 1983) of Bristol and Avon Archaeological Research Group (BAARG) (SMR 20253). The excavation recorded a rectangular enclosure bounded on two sides by ditches. A two-room rectangular building, with a probable hearth, stood at the north end of the enclosure and a gully ran north-south through the enclosure, which was apparently cobbled. To the east of the enclosed area was a small metalled area adjacent to a ditch aligned north-south. Iron and lead ores, coal, and "the waste products of metal working" (Williams 1983, 14) were recorded here. Parallel ditches ran south from the southern side of the enclosure towards a small stone building with "substantial faced stone walls" (ibid. 15) and an area of cobbling and flagstones roughly 75 metres from the main enclosure. It was suggested by the excavator that the cobbling and flagstones may have served as the base for a further building. To the west of this was "an area of hardpacked burnt clay floor" with "a line of laid stones on the south side indicating a furnace, possibly a corn dryer" (Williams 1983). A dump of pottery was found some 50 metres to the east of the enclosure. On the available evidence the excavator concluded that the site was probably a farmstead, occupied from the 1st-century A.D. until possibly the latter part of the 4th-century A.D., where some metalworking was also taking place (Williams 1983, 19).
Subsequent evaluation and excavation in advance of development in the area of the park (SMR 20254; 20349) and at Inns Court Community Centre (SMR 20054; 20348) indicated that the settlement was more extensive than had been previously thought. Work at Inns Court in particular suggested that there was a substantial stone building of at least two ranges immediately west of the community centre. This building was apparently of higher status than those previously recorded in the area and surviving areas of internal stone flooring were found. A wide ditch separated this building from another stone building to the south, and this too had stone floors (Williams 1997, 77-79). The finds evidence may also indicate that the site may have had an origin in the Late Iron Age.
The areal extent of the settlement is currently undefined, although the features so far identified extend for a distance of over 400 metres from east to west.
