monument_desc Milking Parlour on the south side of Kennel Lodge Road. Built in the mid nineteenth century the building was first cartographically depicted on an estate plan dated 1865 (BRO AC/PL/126), although the term Milking Parlour was not in use until the twentieth century and the building may have originally been a 'cowhouse' (Bryant and Ducker, 2009, 4).
The building is a long and narrow structure (23.5m by 5m) that utilised an existing Brandon Hill Grit rubble wall for its northern wall. The remaining three walls were constructed in squared pennant sandstone rubble with freestone dressings bonded with off-white mortar. The south facing façade was built in a neo-Tudor style with 4-bays, each bay comprising a central doorway with a square-headed window to each side. Internal walls created four connected rooms. These internal walls were constructed in red brick with bullnose bricks to all openings. The flooring was bunched Pennant flagstones. At each end of the building was a gable containing a ventilation slit and the roof was originally slate.
At some point a small room at the east end of the structure was partitioned off, possibly to create a dairy, and a small single room extension with pan-tiled pitched roof added to the west end of the building. The small extension was constructed with squared Pennant sandstone with brick dressings bonded with black mortar.
The building was recorded in 2009 prior to conversion for accomodation.
