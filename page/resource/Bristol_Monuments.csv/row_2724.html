monument_desc St. James-the-Less, Upper Maudlin Street. The church was built in 1867 by a committee of the Bristol Female Penitentiary to serve the penitentiary (Ralph & Cobb 1991, 27). Arrowsmith's Dictionary of Bristol reports that the church was consecrated by the Bishop of Bristol on 30th December 1867 (Spear & Arrowsmith 1884 56). It was designed by Pope and Bindon and built of Pennant Sandstone with freestone dressings. The entrance from Upper Maudlin Street was at the west end with a stair turret in the centre of the north wall giving access to an upper gallery and suggesting that the church was aisled at least on its north side. A transept crossed the apsaidal east end.
The history of the building is at present unclear, but it seems to have been built for the penitentiary and later become a chapel-at-ease to St. James, possibly after 1873 when it was consecrated, and the gallery appears to have been reserved for the exclusive use of the inmates of the penitentiary next door.
A "Religious Census" carried out by the Western Daily Press on 30 October 1881 recorded 167 worshippers at Morning Service and 158 at Evening Service.
In late summer 1932 the building was purchased by the Christian Lads' Brigade, founded in 1893, as a new headquarters and converted for their use. The architect was W. H. Watkins and the scheme involved the insertion of a reinforced concrete mezzanine floor. This floor was used for the gymnasium and lectures and concerts while the ground floor was subdivided with folding partitions to provide classrooms and an office. The building works were undertaken by Messrs W. Cowlin.
The opening ceremony was held on Saturday 22 October 1932 and was presided over by the Bishop of Malmesbury. A Guard of Honour in Upper Maudlin Street was inspected by Field Marshal Sir Claud Jacob (1863-1948), Commandant of the Church Lads' Brigade (Colonel of the Worcestershire Regiment and until recently Military Secretary at the India Office) and the building was formally opened by Mrs Yda Roberts, who had donated £1000 towards the conversion works.
The church survived into the early 1960s but was subsequently demolished.
