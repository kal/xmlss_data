monument_desc Blaise Castle, a Gothick sham castle located at Blaise Hill which stands wthin the Iron Age hillfort (SMR 777). The structure was commissioned by the Bristol merchant Thomas Farr, who then owned the manor of Henbury, and was designed by the Scottish architect Robert Mylne (1734-1811) who was employed between 1763 and 1772 to remodel the interior of Kings Weston House (SMR 755). The design has a wide, two-storey crenellated central tower with a flat roof, and three narrower but slightly taller towers attached at equal intervals around its circumference. The windows and other detailing is in a Gothick style, and each of the towers has window openings with shallow arched cinquefoil heads set in rectangular frames. There are blind cross arrow slits at the ground- and second-floor levels. The main entrance is at the centre of the south-east facing elevation. The building is constructed of Lias limestone rubble with limestone ashlar dressings. The three-tower form of the Castle was apparently inspired by Fort Belvedere, built in the early 1750s for Prince William Augustus, the Duke of Cumberland, at Shrubs Hill in Windsor Great Park (Pevsner 1958, 468). Unusually for such structures, the Castle was habitable and had a kitchen and drawing room. It was used as a summer house for entertaining.
When Farr became bankrupt in the late 1770s he sold the Henbury manor to Dr. Denham Skeet, a Bath lawyer. The Castle and its setting were recorded in a number of drawings by Samuel Hieronymus Grimm made in the late 1780s (British Library Add. Mss. 15540 f.112-f.115), and Grimm's 1788 drawing of the building from south-west records a fence enclosing the immediate area around the building. In 1789 Skeet sold Blaise to John Scandrett Harford, a Bristol banker. Humphry Repton was commissioned by Harford to landscape the Blaise estate and in his Red Book of 1798 recommended raising one turret in order to "give it more the character of a real castle at a distance" (Red Book). The proposal was not, however, carried out. The site became a popular destination for day-trips by visitors to Bath at around this time (Daniel 1999, 230) and Blaise Castle is mentioned in Jane Austen's novella Northanger Abbey (written in 1798).
The Castle suffered neglect in the twentieth century and the structure deteriorated. Remedial works were undertaken in 1957 and the interior was largely removed at this time. In 2006 a new internal timber floor was inserted into the building.
The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/20/1361).
