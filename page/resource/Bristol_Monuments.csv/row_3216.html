monument_desc The Custom House located on Welsh Back. Two tenements stood on the site by 1583, one being used as the Custom House at that date (Leech 1997, 169). A new building was constructed on the site of these in 1666 and acted as the Custom House through the later seveneteenth century, although it is not clear whether the structure was purpose-built as a Custom House. This was a square three-bay building of four storeys and attic with a hipped pantile roof. At the time of its construction the building had a cellar measuring 23 1/2 feet wide and extending 32 feet west from Welsh Back. The main, east-facing, elevation fronted on to Welsh Back. By 1825 (BRSMG M2238; BUAD 1360) the ground floor was a shopfront with entrance doorways at the north end and centre. The northern doorway served a narrow property which was in the occupation of Timothy McDonell in 1850 (BRO Corporation Plan Book C fo.98a). The middle doorway led into a passage to the "Three Cups" public house, built in 1820 at the rear of the building. On either side of this middle doorway were large shop windows with stall risers beneath. The first-floor fenestration consisted of three large sash windows while at second floor there was a large pedimented coat of arms in the place of the central window. On either side there were smaller cross-windows and the fenestration at third-floor consisted of three cross-windows. At the centre of the east face of the roof there was a small dormer with a cross window. A brick "turret" on the rear, west side, of the roof my have given access to a flat roof in the centre. On the north side of building there are a number of tall chimney stacks and towards the centre of the elevation at third-storey level, apparently built into one of the flues, is a feature which may have been a window. A nest of stairs was attached to the north side, some serving an adjacent building. There was also a chimney stack on the south wall. Much of the rear, west-facing, wall had been built against by the early nineteenth century but the elevation had at least two mullion and transom windows, and a three-storey square block with a pyramidal pantile roof had by then been added to its north end. This had single windows at each floor in its west-facing elevation.
In 1666 the building appears to have been owned by John Goninge and his mother Elizabeth who leased it to John Bowen and Richard Thrupp. A new Custom House was built in Queen Square in 1710, and the cellar beneath the building subsequently passed into the ownership of the Corporation which leased it to Francis Brown in 1719 and to Thomas Lewis in 1786 (Leech 1997, 169).
The structure seems to have survived into the mid-nineteenth century. The site of the building is now beneath the eastern part of the site of Telephone House.
