monument_desc Kings Weston Down Camp, a univallate hillfort, defended on the north and east sides by steep slopes, on the south by a low rampart and ditch, and on the west, the most vulnerable side, by a larger bank and ditch. Entrances cross the levelled ditch through the main rampart at the NW and SW corners, but neither need be ancient. Excavations by Rahtz in 1957 revealed an occupation layer within the south rampart of the fort and showed the ditch of the west rampart to have been 5 feet 5 inches deep and over 15 feet wide. Pottery indicates primary occupation in a late phase of the IA "A" period (but 13 sling stones in secondary fill of the circle's ditch (SMR 782, a circular earthwork within the fort) suggest IA "B" occupation. The main destruction of the upper rampart of the fort seems to have occurred in the mid-C2, while some Roman occupation is indicated by the quantity of pottery found here. Further west at ST55307805-ST55357797 is a cross-ridge dyke similar in size and character to main rampart of the hillfort, of N. The outer defence or cross ridge dyke was found to have a ditch similar to that of the inner work, but it was only 2 metres wide and 1.5 - 2 metres deep. It contained rubble from the rampart deposited in the mid-C2 or later. A probable hut site was also noted close to the east end of the main enclosure, around 7 metres in diameter. Another F4339-42, and in reserve collection.<3> On the eastern extremity of King's Weston Hill, 3 1/2 miles NW of Bristol, near Blaise Castle Camp there is a small but clearly defined earthwork across the E end of the hill in half moon shape with a trackway running up to a west entrance, then ends of the banks resting on the escarpment of the hill at either side. Area enclosed c 5 acres. Further along the hilltop to the west there is another slight ditch & mound running across the hill from N-S. The hill overlooks Roman town of Abona and whole of lower Severn estuary, particularly the Roman road marked on Ordnance Survey map as extending in a direct line from Lawrence Weston to the Severn. Immediately opposite this road are the remains of a beacon tower which tradition allots to the Roman period.<7> Site is reasonably well looked after, but used by a large number of the public who cause some erosion of the banks. The west bank is 3 metres above the bottom of ditch and c 1 metre above the interior of the enclosures. The interior of the enclosure is covered with short grass, as is the west bank and ditch, which are constructed across the flat top of hill. The other sides of the enclosure are bounded by steep wooded slopes, in which a bank is visible on the south side.
The hillfort is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (AV 53).
