monument_desc A heavy anti-aircraft battery to the west of Rockingham Farm. The battery lay a short distance inland from the coast and occupied a roughly rectangular site of just over 4.8 hectares, between the north-west side of St. Andrews Road and the railway. The site was operational from February 1940 until the end of the Second World War and formed part of the Bristol Gun-Defended Area (GDA). The site was initially manned by 206th Heavy Anti Aircraft Regiment, Royal Artillery which had been replaced by 98th Heavy Anti Aircraft Regiment (Territorial) by November 1939.
The battery was laid out with four octagonal gun emplacements arranged around a central command post, which was unusual in comprising three separate structures. The magazine was sited on the north-west side and stands roughly 2.5 metres high. It is partially sunken and is protected by earth banks. Internally there are five rooms served by a corridor. The ancillary buildings - barracks, sick bay, stores etc. - lay just to the south-east, on the north-east and south-west sides of the access road which led into the site from St. Andrews Road to the east. The 98 HAA Regiment war diary for 9 November indicates flooding problems from heavy rain, recording that the mud on the site was nine inches deep. "Drainage ditches are full to overflowing as the drainage system connecting with the sea sluices has fallen into bad repair."
The battery was initially equipped with four 3.7 inch Mark I static guns. However it had been re-equipped with four 4.5 inch guns by May 1941 (John Hellis pers. comm.) and had GL Mark II radar (which came into service in Spring 1941 and could detect approaching aircraft at a distance of around 30 miles) by June 1942. A short account together with a photograph of the emplacement in action was published in 1943 (Anon 1943, 44). The latter enhancement may have been the result of a Prime Ministerial directive of 27 February 1941 which gave priority to the defence of shipping during the Battle of the Atlantic (Dobinson 2001, 292). The radar mat appears to have been circular rather than octagonal and smaller than the usual diameter of 130 yards (Dobinson 2001). An earthwork visible on a 1946 vertical air photograph (CPE-UK-1869 3016) which probably represents the location of the mat (at ST 52342 80707) measured only 46 yards (42.2 metres) across. By May 1944 the 4.5 inch guns had been replaced with 3.7 inch Mark II guns. The latest reference found to the site found by Dobinson dates to January 1946, suggesting that the battery was decommissioned later in that year (Dobinson 1996, 329).
Most of the ancillary buildings appear to have been demolished by the beginning of December 1946 but the gun emplacements, the command post and the magazine are extant. The site is overgrown and there is some damage to the emplacements and the magazine (SMR 21552). The site is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM number AV 28885).
