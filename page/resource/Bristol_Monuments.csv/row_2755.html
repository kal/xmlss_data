monument_desc Steevens' Almshouse, located on the north side of Old Market Street. The almshouses were founded by Thomas Stevens, a former mayor of Bristol, under the terms of his will of 6 April 1679 which required a group of his "very good friends" to purchase a parcel of land in the parish of St. Philip and Jacob and then construct an almshouse "fit and convenient for twelve poor men and women" (Manchee 1831, 167). The trustees bought a parcel of land which consisted of a "tenement . converted into three small houses or tenements, and a stable and garden ground" from Robert Yate, this "being the only place they could find in the said parish of St. Philip" (Manchee 1831, 168). The form of the seventeenth century almshouses is not known, but the references in Manchee suggest that they may initially have been converted tenements.
The almshouses were rebuilt in 1725. The 1725 structures consisted of two ranges of buildings parallel to one another on either side of a yard, and were behind a narrow row of shops. The entrance to the almshouses was in the centre of this row. The two almshouses were two-storey, four-bay buildings with pitched roofs. An account published in 1831 described the almshouse as "containing sixteen rooms, one room being assigned to each poor person, with a fire-place in it" (Manchee 1831, 174).
The building was badly damaged by bombing in 1940 and restoration work was begun in 1957.
