monument_desc Temple Meads Joint Railway Station. The building, designed by Sir Matthew Digby Wyatt, was erected between 1865 and 1878 in a joint venture by the Great Western Railway and Midland Railway. It was originally called Bristol Joint Station and had a steep French Empire roof to the tower, which was destroyed in the Second World War, and crockets to the turret tops. The listing argues that "the later Temple Meads station uniquely shows, with the Bristol Old Station at Temple Meads, the growth of a major terminus over more than a century". Additional platforms, designed by P. E. Culverhouse, were put in between 1930 and 1935 and the original footbridge linking the platforms replaced with an underpass.
The fabric of the building is rusticated conglomerate with limestone dressings.
The Booking office has forward projecting screens to the train sheds. It is in a Tudor Revival style.
The station has two-storeys, and 3-window range, with single storey. There is a nineteen-window range to right and 17-window range to left. The Booking office has a symmetrical crenellated front with lower angled side blocks and a central two-stage tower, and octagonal turrets to the corners; the ground-floor four-centred arches have banded Purbeck marble shafts, a label mould with quatrefoil spandrels, and twentieth-century doors. The first floor has six-light square-headed windows with transoms and cinquefoil heads, stilted labels over panels with quatrefoils over the middle window; there is a half-quatrefoil arcade below the parapet, with blind lancets to the merlons; the turrets have two crenellated courses below pyramidal tops.
The tower has an arcade of engaged shafts which pass through the drip to pointed arches, under a large square panel and clock with a trefoil-headed blind arcade above. The shed screens have mullion and transom windows separated by octagonal buttresses, with a glazed cast-iron canopy all around the frontage. The main train shed has a two-centred trussed roof with traceried arch braces on octagonal corbel shafts and black diaper work under the eaves.
The building has a Grade I listing. Brunel's station has a separate Grade I listing.
