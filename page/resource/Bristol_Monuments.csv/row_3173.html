monument_desc Princes Theatre, sometimes known as the New Theatre Royal, located on the south side of Park Row. The theatre was built in 1867 to a design by C. J. Phipps. The building was of four storeys and the main elevation was of ashlar. Externally, the ground floor comprised an arcade and the main entrance was at the centre between shops. There was a public house near the western end, beyond which was a side access to the theatre. The stage was 107 feet (32.7 metres) wide and the proscenium arch 30 feet (9.18 metres) by 25 feet 6 inches (7.8 metres). The line of the gallery and the proscenium formed a perfect circle (Carleton 1983, 2). The theatre could accommodate an audience of 2800.
The theatre opened on 14 October 1867 with a performance of The Tempest. The theatre was the scene of a serious accident on Boxing Day 1869, when eighteen of the audience were trampled to death.
In 1902 there were extensive alterations to the theatre by Frank Matcham. Externally these included the creation of a glazed entrance porch with a cast iron frame to enclose the main entrance and the addition of four statues (Tragedy, Comedy, Music and Dance) were added to the parapet. Inside the theatre the floors were angled to make the seating raked, the boxes were remodelled, foyers, smoking rooms and lounges added and the decorative scheme altered to cream and gold. Electric lighting was also added throughout the building (Carleton 1983, 16). The theatre was refurbished again in 1931 when the architect A. E. Oaten oversaw the installation of new lighting and heating as well as structural alterations.
The theatre was destroyed by bombing in 1940. Fire gutted the building, leaving only the shell standing (Warne 1943, 17).
