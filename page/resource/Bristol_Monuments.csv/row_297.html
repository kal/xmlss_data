monument_desc Merchants' Dock. The dock was built as a wet or floating dock prior to the floating of the harbour by William Champion between 1762 and 1768. It was a rectangular basin, aligned north-south, with a southern entrance lock. Two other docks, a graving dock and another wet dock, were built on its east side. Champion lost money in the venture and the docks were sold privately in 1770 to a small group of members of the Society of Merchant Venturers from whom the Society corporately bought the docks complex. The Society renamed the complex Merchants' Dock and gained an Act of Parliament in 1776 requiring ships to unload flammable materials, pitch, tar etc., there. The dock was also enlarged to accommodate sixteen vessels (McGrath 1975, 155-156).
The docks were periodically let, including to the shipbuilder George Hilhouse at the beginning of the nineteenth century and at this time a Bonding Yard (BUAD 1356M) was created to the west of the dock. The entrance lock to Merchants' Dock was truncated when Junction Lock was cut between the harbour and Cumberland Basin. By 1883 Merchants Road had been created through the Bonding Yard and cattle pens stood on the west side of the dock. The western and northern sides of the dock were infilled by the Great Western Railway as part of the works to lay a line to Canons Marsh in the early twentieth century. The remainder was filled to allow development of housing at Rownham Mead in the 1970s.
