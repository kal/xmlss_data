monument_desc The Cooper's Arms, No.101, Redcliff Street. A house of probable seventeenth century date on the west side of Redcliff Street. The tenement plot was laid out in the medieval period as part of the general development of Redcliffe. During the late-medieval period a Pennant sandstone rubble building occupied the site and part of its south wall has survived. The building appears to have been at least partially reconstructed during the seventeenth or early-eighteenth century. A plan of 1894 suggests that the ground floor comprised a room on the street front with a parlour behind. A yard separated the main building from a detached kitchen and a passage ran along the south side of the building. The building was of three storeys according to the Goad Fire Insurance plan of January 1887.
In 1687 the tenement was leased by Benjamin Adams and the rate books indicate that the building was known as The Coopers' Arms by 1695 when Christopher Legg was assessed for the property. Henry Bevan, glazier, seems to have been in possession of the building between 1707 and the late 1740s, though there appear to have been a series of occupants. It was owned by the Society of Hoopers in the 1740s according to a plan by De Wilstar (BRO 04479(1) fo.26-27). Abuttals from deeds relating to The Golden Lion record the property as "a house formerly of David Harris" (BRO 35447 Box 2 Bdl.VII) and by 1775 James Emery, victualler, was the occupier as tenant to Maria Sheard. The rate books indicate that there was also a stable on the site at that date. In the nineteenth century the building was housed J. & M. B. Warry, watchmakers, and later became the premises of Thomas Hall, a metalworker. The building had apparently been demolished by 1934 (Winstone 1957, pl.51) and the site redeveloped as a motor body repair works by 1966.
As part of a programme of archaeological work associated with development the site was partially excavated (BUAD 3629), and the extant south wall of the late-medieval building recorded, in 2000 (BUAD 3552; Lowe 2001) The surviving fabric of the wall was found to include part of an arch. The wall was retained in the subsequent redevelopment of the site.
