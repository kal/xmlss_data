monument_desc A bastion or semi-circular tower on the outer or second town wall, located some 35 metres to the south-east of the Bridewell. The bastion is recorded by a plan of 1739 (BRO Corporation Plan Book A fo.24-25) which indicates that it was a semi-circular structure at that date. There was an entrance on the north side of the bastion which was reached via a walled passageway. The bastion was recorded in two watercolour drawings of 1820 by Hugh O'Neill in the Braikenridge Collection of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery (BRSMG M2447 and BRSMG M2448) and was also visited by Samuel Seyer in 1821. By this time the bastion had been converted to a three-storey circular tower with a pantile roof. Seyer (1821 270-271) described the tower as being 22 feet in external diameter while the wall at ground floor level was 8 or 9 feet thick. A stone staircase at the back of the small, square, ground floor room led to the first storey. The ceiling of the ground floor room was arched and groined without ribs. The roof when Seyer observed it was of tile with common eaves. The drawings show a shallow polygonal or pyramidal roof. The fabric of the tower was of rubble stone and evidence of a change in construction roughly in the middle of the tower is visible, indicating where the tower had been extended. There were doors at the centre of the structure at ground- and first-floor levels, and at second floor there was a small window opening.. The tower is recorded by the plan of the city produced by Plumley and Ashmead in 1828 and Stone (1909) records that the tower was demolished in 1879. The site of the bastion is now partly beneath a corner of the rear part of the Crown Court building but the medieval element mainly lies within yard of the Central Police Station. The line of the town wall to the north of the bastion also runs beneath the yard. The state of preservation of the bastion and wall is currently unknown.
