monument_desc No.9 Pipe Lane, demolished for road widening at the end of 1937 and now beneath the Pipe Lane/ Frogmore Street junction.
The house had six bays and three storeys, two on the east side beneath the first dormer gable. A chimney stack projected from the roof behind the second, central dormer. Beneath this central dormer gable was a doorway with a broken pediment above. The remaining three bays were to the west of this. There were, however, only two windows in the ground floor and these did not align with the three above. A prominent string line divided the lower floors from the attic level. The roof was pantiled, gabled to east and west, with 3 small gables with lower ridges on the north elevation, and two large chimney stacks projected from either end of it. A cellar entrance was located in the west wall of the building. There was also a small square window at ground floor level. On the same side of the building at attic level was a larger blocked square window. The windows at first and second floor in the north frontage were sashes; the remainder were casements.
The ground sloped sharply downhill from the front to the rear of the property.
A niche, approximately 1 metre high, in the north-west corner of the Pipe Lane frontage may have been for a statue or, it has been suggested, a figure of the Virgin Mary during the medieval period. It is also reported that there was a winged lion near the niche, suggesting a link with St.Mark's Hospital. The niche was removed to the City Museum when the building was demolished.
