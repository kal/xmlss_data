monument_desc No.52 Wine Street, located in the southern side of Wine Street slightly to the east of the junction with Pithay. A building stood on the site from at least the mid sixteenth century when it was the tenement of Richard Mallett (Leech 1997, 197). The building as recorded in 1823 was a gabled, jettied house of three storeys and attic which was of seventeenth-century date. The main, north-east facing, elevation of the building was almost entirely panelled. The ground floor consisted of a shopfront with a central entrance doorway and a bay window on either side, and the door of a cellar access shute was located below the western window. The first floor had full-width transomed glazing with bay windows at the eastern and western ends of the elevation. The second floor had a decorative strip at the base and there was transomed glazing across the full-width. At the centre of the elevation there was an oriel window, with six lights to the front, and two to the ends. There were five decorative brackets below the sills, including one in the oriel. There was also an oriel at attic level, containing four lights to the front and two to the ends. There was a bracket carved into a figure supporting the sill. The eaves were supported by decorative brackets and had carved bargeboards. The glazing to the first and second floors extended on to both returns by a light. There was a tall chimneystack in the western wall of the building.
The section of the side, west facing, elevation exposed where the building projected forward of those on either side was rendered and had no windows or other openings. The north-east facing elevation had panelling at ground-, first- and second-floor. To the rear of the building there was a tall, five-storey, structure. This had a lean-to pantile roof with a doorway at the eastern end and above there were small leaded casement windows. At the east end there was a stair tower leading on to a flat roof with a rail or balustrade on its northern side. The function of this is not clear, although it may have been a small viewing platform. Adoining the south side of this structure there was a small tower-like structure with a pitched roof. Beneath the eaves of this on the east side there was a small window, possibly a casement.
In 1823 the building was occupied by Crisps Boot and Shoe Manufactory but George Weare Braikenridge's notes accompanying a drawing made that year record that the building was "now taking down to rebuild" (Braikenridge Notes, C103). Braikenridge obtained the carved bargeboards for use at his Cottage Lodge.
