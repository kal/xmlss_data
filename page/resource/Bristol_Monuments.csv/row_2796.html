monument_desc No.106, Temple Street, located on the western side of Temple Street. The building was a three-storey, single-bay jettied house with a gabled pantile roof. It does not appear to have had an attic storey.
The main, east-facing, elevation was timber-framed and rendered. The ground-floor contained a shopfront with the main entrance door on the north side of the elevation. The door head was supported by two carved wooden brackets (BUAD 1014A), that on the south side a grotesque mask head and scroll work at the sides while the other had leaf and scroll decoration and bore the date 1587 on the front. The first and second floors were jettied.
The original internal plan of the building is not currently clear and the interior had been significantly altered by the time of demolition.
When Victoria Street was created in the later-nineteenth century the building was renumbered as No.115, Victoria Street. The building was demolished in 1907 and was observed by John Pritchard shortly beforehand (BUAD 697). The carved brackets from the main entrance were recovered and both were donated to the City Museum.
