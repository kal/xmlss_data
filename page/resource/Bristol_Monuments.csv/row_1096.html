monument_desc The Mansion House, located at the north-east corner of Queen Square. The building had apparently been constructed by the 1730s and was a five-bay three-storey building with a hipped roof. The main entrance door was in the central bay of the elevation. The fenestration consisted of sashes. On the east side of the centre bay there were pilasters at each storey and ashlar quions at its eastern corner. On the southern slope of the roof there were three small dormers. The side, east-facing, elevation was very plain, with an arched doorway at the north end and windows adjacent and at the south end. There were smaller windows at the centre of the elevation, between ground- and first-floor and between first- and second-floor, presumably lighting a stair. Above the coping at the top of the elevation there were two tall chimney stacks (BUAD 1346) (Greenacre & Stoddard 1986, 25).
The building was bought by the Bristol Corporation in 1786 to provide the official residence of the Mayor and was known as the Mansion House from then on. The building was apparently refitted for this purpose, apparently including a "large turtle tubb" in which to keep live imported turtles before their consumption (Hudson 1993). As a symbol of the Corporation the building was the target of demonstrations in Queen Square on several occasions. It was ultimately destroyed by fire during the riots of 29-31 October 1831 apparently leaving only parts of the shell.walls standing (BUAD 1347).
