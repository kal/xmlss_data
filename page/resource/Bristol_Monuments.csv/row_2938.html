monument_desc Ashley Manor House. The manor house was of seventeenth-century date, and was constructed of rendered random stone rubble with ranges of two storeys with an attic and three storeys with an attic and cross-gable roofs. The building was cellared.
During the medieval period the site was part of St. James's Priory lands and was bought by Robert Hooke, a brewer, in the 1650s (R. H. Leech The garden house: merchant culture and identity in the early modern city. In Lawrence 2003, 82). The house was constructed soon afterwards and a sundial originally sited over the main entrance door carried the date 1656 (Pritchard 1908, 304-308). The fenestration consisted of casements with leaded lights and stone drip moulds above, while the window frames were of oak. Internally, the entrance door opened on to a hall with two rooms on the west side and a winder stair with splat balusters on the east. Within the building there were a number of carved stone chimneypieces including one with bearing the arms of the Brewers' Company and the date 1669 (ibid.).
The building passed into the hands of Andrew Hook in 1733 and accommodated the Blue Maids orphanage from 1795 until 1829 (Pritchard 1908, 304-308).
The building was demolished in 1911 but was surveyed shortly before demolition by John Pritchard (Pritchard 1908, 34-308). A door and three chimney pieces probably from the building are in the collections of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery.
