monument_desc Muller orphanage, Ashley Down Road. In 1832 George Müller (1805-1898), joint pastor of Bethesda Chapel in Great George Street raised £1,000 to furnish their own home at No.6 Wilson Street, St. Paul's to accommodate thirty orphaned girls. Subsequently another three rented houses in Wilson Street were acquired, caring for a total of 130 children. Rented houses were not a long-term solution and by 1845 the number of children had risen even further without additional accommodation. Müller decided on the building of a purpose built home to accommodate 300 children. In 1845 he entered into a contract for the purchase of the 7 acres of ground at £120 per acre. The funding for purchase of the site and construction of the buildings came entirely from donations, including a gift from the Bristol sugar refiner Conrad Finzel. On June 18 1849 300 orphans transferred to the new site.
The plan of the orphanage developed without an overall design. The earliest building is Allen House (listed Grade II: Listed Building number 901-1/32/10038) which was constructed in 1847-49 to a design by the architects John Foster & Son. The same architects also designed Brunel House in 1857 (listed Grade II; Listed Building number 901-1/32/10058), Muller House in 1862 (listed Grade II: Listed Building number 901-1/32/10059), Davy House in 1868 (901-1/32/10061) and Cabot House (listed Grade II: Listed Building number 901-1/32/10060). All of the buildings are of Pennant sandstone rubble and all, except Davy House, have ashlar freestone dressings. The swimming baths, dating to the late-nineteenth century, also have a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/32/10062). Internally the buildings were characterised by an austere scheme of decoration, the main feature of which appears to have been biblical texts and improving legends painted on the walls. By 1870 there were five houses at Ashley Down housing more than 2000 children and the site continued to operate into the twentieth century. The site subsequently became City of Bristol College and a number of alterations to the existing buildings were made and new structures built within the site as part of this use.
