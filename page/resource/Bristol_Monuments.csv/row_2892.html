monument_desc The Marsh Wall. A town wall of thirteenth century date which enclosed development outside the core of the medieval town. The wall ran from the Back (modern Welsh Back) westwards along the northern side of King Street, almost to the Key (modern Broad Quay), then turned northwards for an unknown distance, probably terminating at Viell's Great Tower near the western end of modern Baldwin Street. The wall was pierced by gates at the Back, Back Street and Marsh Street. Smith's map of Bristol, 1568, suggests that there was a ditch in front of the wall for at least the east-west section. Documentary evidence from the sixteenth and seventeenth century evidence suggests that houses and other structures were built in front of the wall, and Millerd's 1673 map tends to confirm this.
A bastion was excavated by K. J. Barton in 1960 (BUAD 441) at the rear of St. Nicholas with Burton's Almshouses (Barton 1964) which was left, and remains, exposed after the excavation and which is a Scheduled Ancient Monument. The wall was identified in a small excavation inside Nos.1-2 Broad Quay in 1972 (BUAD 3234) and a further section of the wall was excavated in 1995 during redevelopment of Olivetti House, Marsh Street (Burchill 1995) (BUAD 2456).
The wall was built of of Brandon Hill Grit with occasional pennant sandstone bonded in a red sandy, lime-flecked, mortar. A section cut through the wall in 1995 to determine how it was founded failed to identify any evidence for wooden piles beneath it (Burchill 1995, 4). The wall is not known to survive above ground but may have been at least partially incorporated into the rear walls of the properties on the north side of King Street. The line of the ditch should lie beneath King Street, and although the insertion of services is likely to have disrupted the stratification associated with its infilling.
