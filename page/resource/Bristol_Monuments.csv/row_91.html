monument_desc The Quaker's Workhouse. The building was located between the north-western end of New Street and the lane alongside the left bank of the river Frome. Rocque's map indicates an H-shaped plan in 1742, with the entrance opposite the end of New Street.

A survey of the building (HER 24673) has confirmed that three of the four ranges occupied until recently as council flats formed the body of the workhouse built in 1698-1700 by the Society of Friends (Quakers).

Weaving was carried out here until 1721, after which the building continued to serve as residential accommodation. A mission and school were established here in the 1860s but the buildings were converted into flats in or about 1929. The basic structure survives but was heavily rebuilt in the 1860s and c.1929, with further refurbishment since World War Two. Few original internal features survive and the roof has been raised. However, this is an early survival of an urban purpose-built workhouse and possibly the oldest-surviving Quaker workhouse.
