monument_desc The Lamb Inn, on the north side of West Street, Old Market. The inn was established in 1651, according to a statement on the central bay and occupied three tenement-widths, with a frontage of 45 feet.
Ground-floor. To the left, a canted bay with railings either side. In the centre, a vehicle entrance with large wooden gates "carrying shell-pattern carving in the upper panels". Spurs to either side of access. To the right, two bays with central doorway, with twin lamps above. Across the whole width above the ground-floor a pentice roof, with full-width fascia board. Above left side of the haulingway, the inn 'sign', the figure of a lamb couchant. Painted sign bearing the legend "LAMB INN BY W. H. SYMES".
First and second floors. First-floor possibly jettied. Two-storey canted bays to left and right; two-storey square bay to centre. Sashes (8/8 pane) to first-floor, narrow (2/2) to bay sides. Six-light mullioned and transomed windows to left and right at second-floor; Ipswich-type window to centre, with keystone bearing an ornamental shield. Rowbotham's illustration shows a mullion dividing the centre panel of the Ipswich window, also oculus-type openings for the upper lights either side.
Three front gables, without windows, all linked at lower level to run across fronts of the bays.
A massive staircase was the principal interior feature, carrying the date 1651 [1017A] and with a carved ground-floor newel post and carved balusters, both typically mid-seventeenth century. John Pritchard, also noted an "excellent" chimney-piece inside the building.
The inn was demolished in 1905 for road widening. It was visited beforehand by John Pritchard (BUAD 903), who photographed several of the internal features (Pritchard 1906). Pritchard reports that the staircase, large wooden doors, sign and chimneypiece were all salvaged from the building. The staircase was retained locally; but the large wooden doors, the Lamb sign and the chimney-piece all left the city.
