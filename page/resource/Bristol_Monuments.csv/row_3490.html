monument_desc No.29 Queen Square, located at the south-eastern corner of Queen Square. A lease for building was granted in 1709 and the house had been constructed by 1720 when rates were first collected on it. It is a house of three-storeys and attic with a cellar and a pantile roof. It is constructed of brick with limestone dressings.
The main, north-facing, elevation is of five bays with a central entrance door. The ground- and first-floor windows comprise sashes with alternate segmental and triangular pediments above the openings linked by a string course. Internally the entrance hall is divided by an elliptical arch leading to an open dogleg stair. The house has extensive surviving early eighteenth-century features, including panelling in the ground- and first-floor rooms. The structure which is now the Bristol Sailors Home (BUAD 1464M), to the south of the main house, formed the stables for the house, although at least some of the upper floors were probably used as warehouse space.
Between c1720 and 1750 a kitchen block was added on the west side of the courtyard and another, smaller, structure on its east side. A lean-to structure, interpreted as a "necessary" or former pantry, against exterior of the north wall built as part of the kitchen contained an oculus doorframe with an oval light above. The smaller structure on the east of the courtyard was interpreted as a possible pentice giving covered access to the stables/warehouse.
In the mid-eighteenth century, the closet wing at the south-west corner of the house was partly demolished and the line of the wall extended. A service room with a counting house to the north were built on the east side of the courtyard. On the east wall of ground-floor room of the counting house there was a fireplace with a moulded stone chimneypiece dating to the later-eighteenth century and this had a separate boiling hearth on its south side. The counting house itself was probably on the first floor and in this room panelling to dado height survives along with a contemporary cupboard in the south-west corner. There is also a chimneypiece with a moulded overmantel and shelf. The fenestration of the west-facing elevation of this block consisted of segmental-arched window openings.
During the 1850s the stable/warehouse was converted into the Bristol Sailors Home and the elevation to The Grove was reconstructed at that time (BRO 40311/B/1 fol.17; Leech 2002).
The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/42/204).
