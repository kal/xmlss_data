monument_desc The White Lion Inn, located on the north side of Broad Street. The site of the inn appears to have covered the sites of two medieval properties. The "Whyt Lyon" had been established by 1517 in the southern of these and was then leased by Lord Lisle to John Ware and his wife Emott. After his imprisonment in the Tower of London, Lord Lisle's properties were confiscated by Henry VIII. In 1544 the Bristol property, including the White Lion, was purchased from the Crown by the Bristol Corporation and was then occupied by John Spryng. In 1598 the inn was occupied by Edmund Hereford and had passed to Margaret Hereford by 1627-28. The second medieval property had apparently been incorporated into the White Lion by this date.
The sixteenth-century form of the building is currently unclear, but it was apparently refronted in the seventeenth century. It was a four-storey jettied building with a six-window range and a pitched roof. The ground floor had a plain frontage with a band of casement windows at around shoulder height. The first floor was jettied over the footway. The first and second floors contained sash windows at the north side of each bay. Above the windows were pediments which were segmental in the second and fifth bays. The sashes at third floor did not have pediments. The frontage had pargetting forming a brick pattern. Beneath each of the first floor sashes was a small square of raised plasterwork with a circle in the centre and a quartered background.
By 1740 the inn was leased by James Bowyer, innholder, and Joseph Sheppard, saddler. In the 1760s and 1770s the White Lion was owned by Thomas Lawrence, father of the portrait painter Sir Thomas Lawrence R.A. (1769-1830). The White Lion Club, active in support of the Tory party in elections in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth century, took its name from the inn where it met.
The building was demolished in the mid-1860s to allow the construction of the Grand Hotel (BUAD 1667M).
