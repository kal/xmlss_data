monument_desc Church of All Saints, located slightly to the south-west of the junction of Corn Street and High Street. The church was presumably established around the time of the foundation of Bristol c1000AD but the earliest documentary reference to the church is in a charter of William, Earl of Gloucester dating before c1160 which grants the advowson of the church to the Abbey of St. Augustine (BUAD 3873; Walker, 1998 21). The earliest form of the building is not currently clear (02/07/2002) but two pairs of round Norman piers with scalloped capitals survive at western end of the nave, suggesting that the building was substantially remodelled in the late eleventh- or early twelfth-century.
The churchwardens' accounts record several alterations and repairs to the structure in the fifteenth-century including the reconstruction of a chapel of Our Lady on the north side of the church in c1450 by Richard Haddon (Burgess 1995, 9). The eastern end of the nave and the aisles of the extant building are of fifteenth-century date and fragments of a fifteenth-century wall painting survive within the church.
The Glebe House or priest's house (BUAD 246M) was constructed in the mid- to late-fifteenth century at the south-west corner of the church and the building, which is extant, oversails part of the south aisle. The House of the Kalendaries (BUAD 990M) was constructed over the north aisle.
There was some modification to the church during the seventeenth century and two wide semi-circular-headed windows (BUAD 1019A), probably dating to the early seventeenth-century, survive in the south chancel wall. Between 1712 and 1717 the tower was reconstructed and in 1724 Edward Colston is reported to have bequeathed £250 to the church for the rebuilding of the tower and chancel in 1724 (Manchee 1831, 317). In c.1770 a new organ was built by Brice Seede. The House of the Kalendaries was reconstructed as a coffee house, No.56 Corn Street (BUAD 336M) in 1782.. The lantern of the tower was rebuilt by Luke Henwood in 1807. The chancel was extended on its south side in the mid nineteenth-century and the ridge of the roof was also raised.
The church in the 1820s is recorded by a number of watercolour drawings in the Braikenridge Collection, Bristol Museum and Art Gallery which show the east end of the church (BRSMG M2380-M2382) and, in a series by Johnson in 1828, the interior (BRSMG M2377-M2379).
Burial within the church was ended by an Order in Council of 18 February 1854 made under the Burial Act of 1852. The Brice Seede organ was rebuilt by John Smith junior of John Smith and Sons, Bristol organ builders, in 1844. The church was declared redundant in 1984.
The church has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/11/575).
