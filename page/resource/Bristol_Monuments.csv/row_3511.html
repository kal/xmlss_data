monument_desc No.52 Redcliff Street, located on the south side of the junction of Three Queens Lane and Redcliff Street.
The building was constructed in the seventeenth century, apparently as one of a pair with No.53 Redcliff Street (BUAD 1445M). The entrance to the building was on the north-facing elevation in Three Queens Lane.
The land parcel, defined by Redcliff Street, Three Queens Lane and the lawditch on the west side, belonged to the Dean and Chapter of Bristol Cathedral in 1661 and an inn or tavern known as "the signe of the George" leased to a tailor named Henry Hitchens then stood on the site (BRO DC/E/40/37/52064). Hitchens's lease was surrendered in 1672 and a new lease taken for 40 years by John Jones, sergeant-at-mace who was contractually bound to spend £100 on building works (BRO DC/E/40/37/52070). By 1714 the building had been converted to three tenements. A malthouse with three cellars beneath had also been built to the rear by 1746 along with a new dwelling, with an associated pigsty, behind the malthouse (BRO DC/E/40/37/52101). By 1798 the building had become The Plume of Feathers public house, kept by Charles Selby. A lease of 1775 indicates that the number of tenements had by then been reduced to two, and that the malthouse was occupied by Thomas Cole, victualler, while the three cellars were leased by Arthur Moreton, carpenter. The dwelling belonged to David Barron (BRO DC/E/40/37/52108). The tenant of The Plume of Feathers was John Box by 1826 when the building was recorded in a watercolour drawing by T. L. Rowbotham (BUAD 967; BRSMG M2042). This view shows the structure as a single-bay jettied, gabled house of three storeys and attic which is likely to have been the structure built by John Jones in the 1670s. The building was still apparently extant in the mid-1850s, but it had been demolished by the time of the Ordnance Survey first edition plan (Bristol Sheet LXXV.4.5) surveyed in 1882 and the site redeveloped. The site of the building now lies partly beneath the highway in Three Queens Lane and Redcliff Street, but the south-east part of the footprint is on the site of modern No.54 Redcliff Street.
