monument_desc Kingsweston Roman Villa, now located on the southern side of Long Cross. The villa was discovered in November 1947 during the construction of a housing estate at Lawrence Weston, at the foot of the northern slope of Pen Pole, on the dryland margin of the North Avon levels. When identified the northern part of the villa had already been removed by the construction of the new road. The surviving part was excavated in 1948 under the direction of George Boon and John Clevedon Brown for the City of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery (SMR 20179; Anon 1949, 108).
The villa was probably constructed towards the end of the Third century in c270AD, a date inferred from a coin of Claudius II (268-270AD) found within one of the original walls. It was constructed using locally quarried stone in a rubble construction and the external walls were covered with white or pink stucco or render. Fragments of window glass were recovered during excaavtion, implying that the building had glazed windows. The villa was orientated from east-north-east to west-south-west and in plan it a had an enclosed rectangular gravel court at the centre. On the south side of the court there was a porticus, an open flagstone walkway with a roof supported on free-standing columns, and on either side a wing of rooms. Those on the west side appear to have been living accommodation, the two surviving rooms both having mosaic floors. The eastern wing consisted of a bath suite with a hypocaust.
The villa appears to have been in use until the mid-Fourth century and may have been abandoned as a result of raiding along the Severn as Roman power declined. A number of local villas seem to have been attacked in period, especially around 367-368 AD.
Finds from the excavation included a hoard of six coins dating between 330AD and 337AD, discovered in Room IV. An Iron Age Dobunnic silver quarter stater was also found in the south wall of Room VIII. (Boon 1950). Two inhumations were excavated within the building, one with two sword cuts on the skull (Boon 1993).
The villa was conserved and is now one of the museums of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery. It is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (AV87).
