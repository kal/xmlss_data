monument_desc The High Cross located at the junction of High Street, Wine Street, Corn Street and Broad Street. The cross was erected in 1373, according to local tradition, (apparently as a replacement for an earlier cross) to celebrate the acquisition of county status and, according to Adams's Chronicle, was repainted and gilded in 1490 and again in 1573. In May 1633 the cross was remodelled, work finishing in December 1634. The Common Council was petitioned in 1733 by people living in the area who demanded its removal, arguing that:
"It hath been insinuated by some that this Cross, on account of its antiquity ought to be lookt upon as something sacred; But when we consider that we are protestants, and that popery ought effectually to be garded against in this Nation, we make this our request to you to consider. If the opening of a passage to four of the principal streets in this City ought not to outweigh anything that can be said for the keeping up a ruinous and superstitious Relick, which is at present a public nuisance" (quoted in Liversidge 1978 2).
The removal of the cross was ordered, though the stones were stored by the Corporation. In 1736, after the intervention of Alderman Price the cross was re-erected on College Green (at NGR ST 58349 72772).
A proposal to remove the cross from College Green was made in 1757 by a contractor working on the improvement of the paths on College Green, but the monument was not dismantled until August 1762. After a period in storage in the Cathedral it was given to Henry Hoare by the Dean and removed, in August 1766, to Stourhead where it now stands. Hoare considered that "the legs are not worth fetching" (Pryce 1861 559) andduring its re-erection the original base was replaced with a solid structure.
On the base rests a second stage on which four monarchs are depicted. Originally King John faced north (into Broad Street), Henry II faced east (into Wine Street), Edward III, a later addition according to Ponting, faced south (into High Street). The upper stages were added in 1633, and included four Tudor and Stuart monarchs, Henry VIII, Elizabeth I, James I and Charles I. At the top, was a spirelet which supported a copper cross. The whole structure stood 48 feet high according to Ponting who measured the cross at Stourhead. The stone used was an oolitic limestone, probably from Dundry or Bath. Liversidge argues that the lower tiers are of early fifteenth-century date (Liversidge 1978 11).
A replica of the cross (BUAD 40M), made in 1851 by John Norton, is located in Berkeley Square.
