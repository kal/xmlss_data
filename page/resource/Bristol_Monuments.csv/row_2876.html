monument_desc The Red Lodge, located on the southern side of Park Row. The building was constructed c.1588 as one of two lodges to the gardens of the Sir John Yonge's Great House (BUAD 420M) in St. Augustine's Back which extended from Trenchard Street across Stony Hill.

Brandon Hill Grit with freestone dressings, ashlar lateral stacks and a pantile hipped roof. 16th.century house remodelled in 18th.century as double-depth plan.

3 storeys and basement; 3-window range. Raised ground floor to garden front has an arcade of 3 semicircular arches, formerly an open verandah with a porch at the north-eastern end. Shallow side projections.

Interior with very fine late 16th.century joinery, plasterwork and fireplaces. Great Oak Room almost completely original; fine fireplace with paired fluted columns to a cornice; strapwork plaster ceiling with pendents.

There are attached rubble walls around the rear garden.

Altered c1730. 18th.century left-hand single-storey porch.
large 18th.century open-well stair, replacing an earlier small stair turret.
18th.century reception rooms, formed by enclosing the garden verandah, panelled with good fireplaces. Stone stair flight to street entrance. the garden verandah was enclosed and mullion windows replaced.and early 18th.century joinery.

Mary Carpenter (1807-1877) founded a reformatory for girls in the building in 1854 (Saywell 1964). The Red Lodge Reformatory gained an international reputation. Three cells from this phase of the building's use still survive in the basement (R. H. Leech pers comm). The reformatory closed in 1917.
The building was purchased and presented to the City of Bristol in 1920 and the architect C. F. W. Dening was commissioned to undertake a programme of restoration. The building was then leased to the Bristol Savages art club. The New Oak Room has panelling and fireplaces from the Museum's collection, the panelling from the Old Rectory at Lower Church Lane (possibly originally from the White Lodge, sister to this building).
