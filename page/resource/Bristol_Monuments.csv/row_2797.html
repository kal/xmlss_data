monument_desc No.12 Welsh Back, known as 'Langton's House'. The site now lies largely below an open area on the west side of the Back, immediately south of the 'Brygstowe' office block, and north-east of another at No.15-21 Queen Charlotte Street. In 1905 it was learned that many of the architectural details were to be removed to a new residence in Hampshire, designed by Edwin Lutyens.
The building was recorded internally by Philip E. W. Street in 1931, with annotated sketch plans made on March 18th. and internal photographs taken the following day. He noted that the first-floor room facing the Back had been the "Drawing-room", with, apparently, a fireplace on the north wall; behind was another, unidentified, room; behind again, a modern office with skylight; to the rear, to the north the main staircase, to the south the "South Room". This last had an "Elizabethan" window in its east wall (i.e.against the modern office), with a fireplace of similar attribution at an angle in the south-east corner. Immediately west was the "West Room", with, on its west side, a passage which turned west at its north end to reach Queen Charlotte Street. In the west wall of the room were (south) "jambs of Elizabethan window" and (north end) "Elizab. panelled door", the latter above a similar feature on the ground-floor. A fireplace had formerly been located along the north wall of the west room. In the south wall of the adjacent property to the north, No.11 Welsh Back, and against the second room of Langton's House, were several blocked-up "Elizabethan" windows.
The front was recorded by Robert Harman in 1732, when Arthur Taylor, mayor, occupied the house. His drawing was copied by H. O'Neill in 1821. As late as 1810 the left-hand entrance and two columns were still standing. In that year there was a fire in the building.
O'Neill shows a house of two bays, a 3-window range. Three storeys and attics. Entrances with semicircular heads to left and right, between single columns; low pairs of gates before the single panelled doors; wider central arched opening. Full-width parapet at first-floor, above the columns, with urns, etc., as decoration. Large central 15-light window with double transom; to either side, canted bays of 9-lights to front, 3 to each side, parapets. Second-floor possibly flush with first. Oriel to centre, single transom, 6 lights, 4 each to sides, parapet; either side 6-light windows. Balustraded parapet at attic level, with access door below central valley. A cross-window in either gable. Twin gables to front; cross gable, with stacks either end. Observatory platform in centre of roof, balustraded. Gables possibly slated.
Inside was a fine staircase, drawn by O'Neill in 1821 (M2266), carved balusters, and square newel posts surmounted by beasts. The fine full-height chimney-piece was drawn by Johnson in 1823 (M2268); Rowbotham recorded the doorway and panelling in 1826 (M2269-2271).
