monument_desc The New Dock Gates Inn, No.151 Hotwell Road, located on the southern side of Hotwell Road, on the east side of its junction with Dock Gate Lane. The building appears to be recorded by de Wilstar's map of Clifton of 1746 and Dock Gate Lane was also established by that date. By 1869 the building was a public house, the New Dock Gates Tavern, run by Mary Ann Wyatt. By 1874 street directories indicate that the ownership had changed and it was then run by Robert Jones. Mary Jones, presumably the wife of Robert Jones, had taken over as licensee by 1879 and she appears to have changed the name of the public house to the New Dock Gates Inn by 1884. Use of the building as a public house continued well into the twentieth century.
The building is a house of two storeys and attic with a pitched pantile roof. The building is constructed of Brandon Hill Grit in a random rubble construction bonded in a white lime mortar and is currently rendered with a sand-cement render. The original plan of the building is not clear but it may have been of two rooms at each floor off a central hall. The main, north-facing, elevation is of three bays with a central entrance at ground floor and single large sash windows on either side. The cellar has chimneybreasts at the eastern and western ends, with steps leading down, presumably from a cellar opening on to Dock Gate Lane, at the south-west corner. An extension had been added to the rear of the building by the time of the Ordnance Survey first edition 1:500 plan (Bristol Sheet LXXV.4.7) surveyed in 1883. Evidence within the building (BUAD 3851) also suggests that the main elevation was reconstructed in brick at first floor level during the nineteenth century and that the gables at either end of the building were also replaced. The pitch of the roof was probably also increased at this time.
By the late twentieth century the building was in use as an office and the interior walls were removed at ground floor to create an open plan office, probably in the 1980s. The floors are supported on inserted RSJs throughout the building. No original eighteenth-century features or fittings now appear to remain inside the building.
