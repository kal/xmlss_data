monument_desc 'The Rising Sun' public house, at the corner of Ellbroad and Lower Castle Streets. The main building, on the corner of the south side of Ellbroad Street but facing Lower Castle Street, was of two storeys, the upper jettied, on brackets. Rendered. Four bays; main entrance in third bay from north, with pair of seventeenth-century posts to the porch; large lamp over entrance; four sashes to first-floor. Large steep pantiled roof, gable to north end. One-bay extension along Lower Castle Street; similar steep roof; one window only, to ?ground-floor; stack at south end. Further one-bay extension of two storeys and flat roof; doorway and window to ground-floor, window above. Whole length of building with clinker plinth to ground-floor, returning on north elevation. North end gabled; 2 bays; plain gable end with stack at apex, adjacent to Lower Castle Street; eastern bay with cellar access below ground-floor window. One common pentice roof between the ground and first floors of the eastern bay and Nos.2 & 4 Ellbroad Street.
Pritchard (1907, 160-162) describes the accommodation as small, the rooms very low. In the bar-parlour, on the right-hand on entering, was a stone chimney-piece depicting Adam and Eve on a central panel, with a smaller panel of an eagle. Moulded plaster ceiling, divided by a large moulded beam; moulded plaster cornice above panelling; leaded panes to window; an "unusually wide" window sill. In the back kitchen, a three-quarter height chimney-piece with flat four-centred arch; decorated frieze above fireplace, with scroll work, two roses and a central shield bearing the date 1606 and the initials ' T M A'.
A front bedroom at the corner on the first-floor was largely panelled in oak, with a "handsome moulded plaster cornice, representing griffin-like monsters, divided by mask-head brackets". Below was a decorated oak frieze including several shields bearing the same initials as in the kitchen.
The building was demolished in August and September 1906, along with two other historic buildings in Ellbroad Street (BUAD 152M). and rebuilt to a design by Paull and James. The new public house was to incorporate features recovered from the old, but their fate when the Edwardian building was demolished in 1954 is unknown.
