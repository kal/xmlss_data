monument_desc St. John's Almshouse, located on the south-east side of St. John's Steep. The almshouse is reputed to have been at the end of the fifteenth century (possibly in 1491). The form of the medieval building is unknown but the original building had fallen into a "ruinous" state by 1721 (Manchee, 1831 3). This was demolished and a new building constructed on the same site. There were thirteen poor female parishioners in the building at that date and the replacement building seems to have been designed with the same accommodation (Manchee, 1831 3-4).
The new building was sited along the south-east side of St. John's Steep, and measured 23 metres from south-west to north-east and was 4.6 metres wide. The building was set back slightly from St. John's Steep and was surrounded by a wall with a central gateway. It was of two storeys with a hipped roof. String course between storeys. Cross-windows to first-floor. Arched entrance at extreme right-hand end, rusticted quoins above it. Entrance with flat porch to its left. Tall brick stacks. Front boundary wall with ball-finial decoration. The almshouse was recorded in a drawing by Samuel Loxton in the early twentieth-century (Bristol Reference Library X1704).
The eighteenth-century building was demolished to allow construction of a new factory building for Fry's in 1910.
