monument_desc Ridleys Almshouse, also known as the Batchelors and Maids Almshouse, located on the western side of the junction of Milk Street and Old King Street. The almshouse was founded by Sarah Ridley through a bequest of £2,200 in her will of 10 November 1716 to provide for "the maintenance from time to time of five old poor decayed batchelors, and five old poor decayed maidens" (Manchee, 1831 192-193). Sarah Ridley died in 1726 and the site was purchased in 1735. Five small houses had previously ocupied the site but were then reported to be "decayed and in great part fallen down" (Manchee, 1831 195). Building commenced in 1738 and was completed August 1740, and a total £860 was spent to establish the almshouse (Manchee, 1831 195).
The almshouse measured 20.5 metres from east to west and was 13.5 metres north-south and was of two storeys with a hipped slate roof. The Milk Street elevation had a five-window range and at either end there were a pair of slightly projecting side wings, each with a one-window range.
At first-floor, a round-headed recess contained a plaque which, in accordance with the terms of Sarah Ridley's will, carried the legend: "In Memory of Mr Thomas Ridley and Sarah Ridley Being Brother and Sister Never Married Erected Anno Dom 1739." otherwise cross-windows, as at both storeys of wings, also in side elevations and insides of wings. The Old King Street elevation had a three-window range with a projecting centre bay.
The building was unusual in having casement windows at so late a date.
There were some additions of perhaps 1741.
The almshouse was damaged by bombing during the Second World War, was subsequently designated as a Scheduled Ancient Monument. It was, however, removed to make way for the development of the new Broadmead shopping centre. The building was recorded by the Ministry of Works prior to its demolition (BUAD 920; O'Neill, 1951). The plaque commemorating the founder and her brother was removed when the building was demolished and is now in the collections of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery.
A drinking fountain was located in an arched recess, in a larger gabled structure, in the boundary wall against Old King Street. Until early in the twentieth century there was a 'Charley Box' or watchman's box at the junction of the two streets.
