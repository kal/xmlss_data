monument_desc Prisoner of War Camp, also known as the French Prison, located on the north side of Manor Road. The camp was built by the Admiralty in 1779 to hold those captured during the American War of Independence (Vintner 1956). The earliest record of the presence of the building, though not its form, is a plan, titled "Division 8th", which dates to c1779-1783 (BRO AC/PL59). The original form of the prison is recorded in an engraving by J. P. Malcolm published in May 1814 (SMR 20996). This indicates that the prison buildings were originally surrounded by high stone outer walls with four pairs of elevated sentry boxes at intervals along each side. These sentry boxes were set on a platform (probably wooden), immediately below the level of the parapet, supported by a narrow column at each corner and each was reached by a stair on its southern side. Those at the ends appear to have been exceptions since the stair runs along the adjoining wall rather than being freestanding, suggesting that the stairs were supported by cross-members set into the prison walls. There was a gate at the centre of the south wall.
The cell blocks were of three storeys with a pitched roof and were constructed of stone rubble with ashlar detailing. The blocks had an axial, E-shaped plan and a symmetrical front with a three-window central projecting block. Each main elevation had sixteen bays, each containing a plain barred window at first-floor level. There were small brick chimney stacks along the ridge of the roof and also a number of narrow rooflights.
The prison presumably stood empty until it was leased by the Bristol Guardians of the Poor as a workhouse c1831 and it was probably at this time that the outer walls were demolished. The western of the two prison blocks was demolished between 1861 and 1865 to make way for new workhouse buildings. The eastern block is extant but the interior is reported by the listing to have been remodelled in the twentieth century. It is not clear what other structures associated with the prison remain (Leech 2001).
The surviving building has a Grade II listing (901-1/30/1274). The listing details note that it is an early and important example. The only other known prison of this period and type is the Norman Cross camp at Yaxley in Cambridgeshire which operated between 1796 and 1816. The site is a Scheduled Ancient Monument survives mainly as earthworks, although the officer's quarters and part of the boundary wall are extant.
