monument_desc Brook Farm, located on the northern side of Whitchurch Lane, Bishopsworth. The farm was in existence by the eighteenth century and was owned in 1827 by William Gore-Langton (BRO AC/PL 107/1 & 2). The Bedminster tithe survey of 1841 indicated that the farm consisted of a farmhouse, yard and garden with outbuildings. The tithe map indicates that the farmhouse was a long building aligned north-east to south-west positioned at the centre of the farm site with outbuildings to either side. Some 5 metres to the east of the farmhouse was a pound (SMR 21199), which was also in existence by the late eighteenth century. The farm was then owned, along with much other land in the immediate area, by William Gore-Langton and was rented by John Smith and Elizabeth Sellett.. The Ordnance Survey first edition 1:2500 plan (Somerset Sheet VI.14) published in 1885 names the farm as Brook Farm and indicates that while most of the buildings recorded by the tithe map were still extant, an extension at the south-east corner of the farmhouse had been demolished. The plan also records a well just to the south of the farmhouse (at ST 57391 68587). Several of the farm outbuildings survived into the 1950s but most were demolished in the 1960s when a garage was constructed to the north-west of the farmhouse.
The farmhouse itself is extant and is now a two-storey rectangular building, measuring 9.5 metres from north-east to south-west by 8 metres wide, with a single-storey extension on the eastern side. The building is constructed of coursed stone rubble. The main, south-west facing elevation is of three bays with a main entrance door at the centre of the elevation. All of the openings have plain ashlar mouldings. Originally there were chimneystacks at the eastern and western ends of the building. These were apparently extant in the 1970s (Bantock, 1996) but have subsequently been removed. The pantile roof is hipped. The single-storey extension is also of coursed stone rubble and has a pitched pantile roof. There is an entrance door at the western end of its south-west facing elevation (Winstone, 1977 pl.201).
