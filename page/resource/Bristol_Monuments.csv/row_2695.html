monument_desc No.12 West Street, Old Market. The building is a timber-framed single-bay house of three storeys and attic with a rendered façade. It is of late seventeenth-or early eighteenth-century date and appears to have been built as the western of a pair with No.14 West Street. The building was probably constructed with a gabled roof. A surviving passage, paved with Pennant Sandstone flags, served both buildings leading from West Street to the rear of the property. The entrance door to the upper floors was off this passage.
The building was constructed of Pennant sandstone rubble bonded in a red sandy mortar with white lime inclusions. These walls are extant in the rear two thirds of the structure. The rear elevation, however, has seen a number of phases of alteration.
The upper floors oversail the passage, supported by several large oak beams and structural beams survive at all levels within the building. The internal plan has been significantly altered over time and the original organisation of the internal spaces has not yet been established. However, particularly at ground-floor the beams show evidence of mortices where internal timber partitions are likely to have been removed.
Both buildings were refronted in the nineteenth century. The ground-floor is currently occupied by a shopfront with a central entrance doorway. There are three doors in the eastern wall of No.12 West Street and the main entrance to the property is the first, northern, of these. This opens into a narrow stairwell with a moulded timber jamb on its western side. This was inserted into the building in the eighteenth- or early nineteenth-century and was an octagonal brick structure. Above the ground floor the party wall with No.14 West Street appears to have been reconstructed at a similar date. Timbers were nailed to the knees of the cross timbers to extend across the shared passage and is the timber framed party wall above rests on these extensions.
Internally no original fittings or features are known to survive, apart from a possible alcove for a cupboard in the western party wall at second floor. A number of historic timbers are extant in the ceilings, however. The roof and part of the parapet was replaced in the late-nineteenth or early-twentieth century.
At the rear, southern, end of the passage there was probably a detached kitchen constructed of Pennant sandstone rubble. Much of the shell of this appears to survive but part of the eastern and northern wall have been removed, and the original roof replaced with a lean-to pantile roof at a lower level.
The building has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/12/1661).
