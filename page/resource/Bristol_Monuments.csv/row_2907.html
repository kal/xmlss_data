monument_desc No.8, Castle Street. The building was a gabled of three storeys with a pantile roof and lay on the north side of Castle Street. It was almost certainly constructed as part of the redevelopment of the site of Bristol Castle after its demolition in the late 1650s, probably in 1663.
The building was painted by T. L. S. Rowbotham in 1829 (BUAD 1832; BRSMG Mb6398) when it was occupied by a druggist. The ground floor of the main elevation was a shopfront with a central doorway. The first-floor fenestration comprised a canted bay, with a large central sash and small sashes on each side. On either side of the bay there was pargetting or moulded plasterwork. The pargetting on either side of the bay at first floor had central elliptical panels, the western containing the letters "ID" and the eastern the date "1663". The plasterwork was very similar in style to that of No.7, suggesting that the work on the two buildings was executed at the same time and for the same owners. The second floor extends into the roof space and has a central twin sash window in the same plane as the rest of the gable end. The decorative plasterwork did not extend above first-floor. There was a chimney stack on the east side of the roof.
The building survived into the early twentieth century. Its site now lies in Castle Park on the east side of the path from Broad Weir past the end of St. Peter's Church.
