monument_desc The House of the Kalendaries, located on the south-eastern side of Corn Street. The guild was an association of secular clergy and laity constituted with the purpose of meeting on the first day of the month to celebrate mass and intercede on behalf of the living and for the souls of the dead. The Bristol Kalendars were one of only a handful of similar guilds in the country, the others being at Exeter and Winchester. Its origins are obscure (Neale 2000, 210-211), but it had apparently been established by the early-thirteenth century (Orme 1978, 35).
The house at All Saints seems to have been built in the fourteenth century and oversailed the north aisle of All Saint's Church. Little is currently known in detail about its medieval form. The guild was wealthy enough to maintain a prior and three chantry priests in the building, and in the mid-fifteenth century created a public theological library there. The initiative seems to have come from the Bishop of Worcester with the aim of creating a centre of religious orthodoxy to counter the influence of Lollardy, and the library was open for private study for four hours each weekday (Orme 1978, 40-43). A deed of 1548 suggests that the library was housed in an extension to the main building, recording that the building had "a chamber annexed ... vulgarly called the Librarye" (Leech 1997, 66).
The Guild of Kalendars was dissolved at Easter 1548 and unlike many other medieval guilds it did not reconstitute itself but instead ceased to exist and the Crown sold off its property (Orme 1978, 45). The house seems to have been bought by Miles Partridge and Hugh Partridge and was immediately leased by them to Robert Wudwarde (Leech 1997, 66). The site had passed into the ownership of All Saints parish by 1616 when a faculty was granted for the creation of a doorway through the north wall of the church (Leech 1997, 66-67).
James Millerd's 1673 prospect of Bristol includes a vignette which shows All Saint's Church, and this records the building as a sixteenth- or seventeenth-century house of three storeys and attics with a cross-gabled roof, presenting two gables to Corn Street. There was a possible stair turret at the east end of the north elevation. There were mullioned windows on all floors. All Saints Conduit (BUAD 682M) was built into the ground-floor. By the early eighteenth century the building had apparently become the coffee house of George Cooke and on the basis of the property records Leech suggests that the house may have been rebuilt in c.1740 by John Worrall, though how extensive the rebuilding was is unknown. Extensive rebuilding did, however, take place when the present No.56 Corn Street (BUAD 336M) was constructed in 1782, but elements of earlier fabric appear to have survived in the cellar and the south wall of the property.
