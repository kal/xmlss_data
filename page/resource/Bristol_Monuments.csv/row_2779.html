monument_desc Parish church of St. Stephen. It originally stood beside the quays by the River Frome, excavated 1240-1247.
Built in the fourteenth-century, rebuilt c1470. The tower and east window were paid for by John Shipward, Mayor of Bristol in the 1480s.
The clerestory repaired after a storm in 1703, aisle and east windows restored 1873. Bath stone ashlar and Pennant rubble south aisle.
In plan, aisled, clerestoreyed nave and chancel, south-west tower, south porch and south-east vestry. Perpendicular Gothic with a Somerset-type tower.
A tall five-light transomed east window. A five-bay north aisle of three-light windows, with a crenellated parapet; the south aisle similar; clerestorey of four-light Tudor-arched windows, again with a crenellated parapet. The south porch has a Tudor arch hung with open cusps. Inside, it has a fan vault with pendants. Nineteenth-century south-east crenellated octagonal vestry with 3-light mullion windows with cinquefoil heads.
Large west nave window has a flat Tudor arch and six-light transomed lights. There is a four-stage tower with north-west octagonal stair tower and an open traceried castellated parapet.
Inside, a panelled reredos by Hansom. A seven-bay nave, the chancel not structurally differentiated but the two east bays have raised floor; door in north aisle to a rood loft stair. Fifteenth-century arch-braced tie beam roof with gilded bosses. View of nave, looking east, by Johnson, 1828 (BRSMG M2492), showing the old pulpit with tester.
An ornate large marble pulpit; panelled choir stalls and pews with carved bench ends; wrought-iron sword rest by William or Simon Edney, early eighteenth century, with considerable foliage; ornate font of 1882, a square base with an octagonal basin. Royal Arms of Charles II over south door. North aisle: 3 ogee-arched chest tombs, to the west that to Edmund Blanket d.1371, a crocketed ogee arch with open cusps, containing recumbent figures of a man and wife praying, transferred from the previous church on this site. Dresser tomb to Martin Pringe d.1626, bearing a cartouche and two recumbent statues.
In south aisle a dresser tomb to Sir George Snygge d.1617 with recumbent figure resting on his elbow; probably relocated to east end of aisle in the nineteenth century (BRSMG M2493-M2494). Wall tablet to Robert Kitchin d.1594, with a kneeling couple inscribed on a brass plate (BRSMG M2495). Various late eighteenth and nineteenth-century marble wall tablets.
A new organ was built by John Smith junior of the Bristol organ builders John Smith and Sons in 1836 (the first organ he built).
Wrought-iron gates and piers, early eighteenth century by William Edney, removed from the Church of St. Nicholas after the Second World War.
Now the parish church of Bristol

Work behind the Snygge monument in 1992 revealed the blocked south aisle east window and some wall painting.
