monument_desc Crow Lane burial ground, located on the west side of Welsh Back. The inhumation cemetery was an extension burial ground belonging to St. Nicholas Parish. The plot was formerly the site of the Chapel of St. John the Evangelist (BUAD 42M) and the parish seems to have acquired it in 1548 after the dissolution of the chapel, since a deed of that year refers to the site as the cemetery of St. Nicholas parish (Leech 1997, 168). The earliest cartographic record of the cemetery is James Millerd's map of 1673. It is also recorded by watercolour paintings dating to the early 1820s in Bristol Museum and Art Gallery (BRMSG M2238 (BUAD 1360) & BRSMG M2279 (BUAD 1378)).
The cemetery was enclosed by a stone wall, and entrance was from Welsh Back via a set of steps at the south end of the wall fronting the Back. The entrance had a semi-circular arched head with a ball finial above and panelled jambs. Roque's map of 1742 shows an open-fronted, roofed structure on the outside, along the entire length of the Welsh Back frontage. A structure, possibly a form of lych gate, stood immediately inside the entrance in the nineteenth-century. This was essentially a pyramidal pantiled roof, with a ball finial in the centre, which was open on its west side. Its western corners were supported by columns and its eastern corners probably by the perimeter wall. Use of the burial ground appears from the painting of 1823 (BRSMG 2279) to have been dense. The burial ground was closed for interment by an Order in Council of 18 February 1854 made under the Burial Act of 1852.
The site was subsequently used as a children's play area until this was closed in 1902 (Latimer 1970, 4). The site was then built over. The site of the cemetery now lies mainly under Crow Lane but also extends beneath the Telephone House site. Crow Lane was widened (apparently in two stages) and the northern two-thirds of the burial ground lie beneath it. It is not known whether the inhumations have been removed from the site, but the Home Office has no record of any licence for the removal of human remains being granted (J. R. Brett pers. comm.). Articulated human remains were found in Crow Lane during observation of the excavation of telecommunications trenches in the 1980s (BUAD 293) and it is therefore likely that undisturbed burials remain on the northern part of the Telephone House site, and possibly also in Crow Lane.
