monument_desc No.7, Castle Street. The building was a gabled of three storeys and attic with a pantile roof lying on the north side of Castle Street. It was almost certainly constructed as part of the redevelopment of the site of Bristol Castle after its demolition in the late 1650s, probably in 1664.
The building was painted by T. L. S. Rowbotham in 1829 (BUAD 1832; BRSMG Mb6398) when the occupier was "I. Walsh & Co Irish Linen Warehouse." The ground floor of the main elevation was a shopfront with the main door slightly to the west of the centre of the elevation. The first- and second-floor fenestration comprised a canted bay, with a large central sash and small sashes on each side. On either side of the bay there was pargetting or moulded plasterwork. The pargetting on either side of the bay at first floor had central elliptical panels, the western containing the letters "SD" and the eastern the date "1664". The plasterwork was very similar in style to that of No.8, suggesting that the work on the two buildings was executed at the same time and for the same owners. The divisions between first and second-floor and between second-floor and attic level were marked by moulded string courses, each extending the full-width of the elevation. The attic was lit by a three-light casement at the centre of the gable and there was a chimney stack on the east side of the roof.
The building survived into the early twentieth century. Its site now lies in Castle Park on the east side of the path from Broad Weir past the end of St. Peter's Church.
