monument_desc Magdalen's or Redcliffe Almshouses, located on the southern side of Temple Gate. The almshouse is reputed to have been founded by Roger Magdalen of Nunney in Somerset during the middle ages. The building was aligned parallel with the street and measured approximately 23 metres from north-west to south-east and was 5.5 metres wide, with a garden plot to the rear.
From the Reformation the vestry of St. Mary Redcliffe managed the almshouse. The building is reported to have been rebuilt by the vestry in 1675 at a cost of £113 (Manchee 1831, 58) and on October 1700 the vestry appears to have entered into an agreement with the newly-created Corporation of the Poor. An inspection by officers of the Corporation in April 1705 found that "it was inhabited by rude disorderly people" (Manchee 1831, 57). The vestry continued to nominate poor parishioners to reside in the almshouse and to maintain the building, while the Corporation of the Poor provided an annual sum of £16 to the residents. In 1831 there were fourteen residents (Manchee 1831, 57).
