monument_desc Crabb's Well, No.105 Temple Street, located on the western side of Temple Street. The building was a rendered, jettied timber-framed house of a single bay which probably dated to the later-seventeenth or early-eighteenth century, presumably originally with a gabled roof. The Temple Street frontage measured 15 feet 6 inches [4.7 metres] wide and the plot extended around 50 feet [15 metres] backwards. The front and rear walls of the building were at least 2 feet 6 inches [0.75 metres] thick and the party walls were 4 feet [1.2 metres] thick..
The main, east-facing, elevation had an entrance door on the southern side of the ground-floor with a canted bay window to its north. The first and second floors were jettied over the ground-floor and the first floor fenestration comprised a square bay window containing a 12 over 12 pane sash. At second-floor there was a three-light window, the wider central light with a horizontal 9 over 9 pane sash.
The original internal plan is not currently clear and the interior had been significantly altered by the time of demolition. There were apparently three rooms on both the first and second floors. The tap-room, parlour and kitchens were more than a foot below pavement level. By the late-nineteenth century the building had a "square-head" attic with a flat roof, probably formed by raising the original gable roof. Part of the top of the gable appears still to have been extant in 1907.
There was a yard to the rear of the house, and a large skittle alley.
In the late-eighteenth and early-nineteenth centuries a number of carriers worked from the property. It belonged to Owen's Charity and was described in 1877 a "Dwelling houses, yard and premises" in the tenure of George Neate (Anon. 1877, 26).
When Victoria Street was created in the later-nineteenth century the building was renumbered as No.117, Victoria Street. It closed as a licensed house in the late 1880s and its condition subsequently deteriorated. The building was demolished in 1907 and was observed by John Pritchard shortly beforehand (BUAD 697). He reports that traces of original window frames were found in the main elevation during demolition, as well as moulded beams in the ceiling of the first-floor room. Pritchard believed that the inn yard was then occupied by the warehouses of Messrs. Joseph Fish & Son.
