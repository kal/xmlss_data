monument_desc Christ Church, located on the north-east side of Broad Street.
The advowson belonged to Tewkesbury Abbey which appears to have exercised its right to appoint through its cell St. James Priory (BUAD 1024M). After the dissolution of St. James, the advowson of the rectory was acquired by Henry Brayne as part of his purchase of the St. James property in 1544.
By the late-seventeenth century the church had a tower and spire at, or towards, its eastern end. Nave and two aisles. There was a covered walkway along the Broad Street frontage with a shop on north-west side; arched west doorway with twisted columns. Large traceried west window of 8 lights, one light containing a door at its base, leading to the roof over the pavement. 4-light traceried window to north aisle. Nave and north aisles gabled, crosses to apex of gables. At west end of south aisle a 5-light window with shallow segmental arch, quatrefoil in centre of tracery, above and to the right, in a decorative frame, a large clockface. At the south-west corner of the church, and reaching above the south aisle, a tall thin pillar was topped by a flaming urn. Dragon weathervane atop the spire.
A new organ was built for the church by Renatus Harris in 1708.
The church was demolished in the late eighteenth century and was rebuilt 1786-1791 to a design by William Paty. The construction work was undertaken by Thomas Paty and Sons.
The entrance was reconstructed and the interior refurbished by Henry Williams in 1883, restored 1973. Bath stone ashlar; roof not visible.
West tower and nave. Baroque style. South wall has four 3-light round-arched windows, 1883. Tower is of four stages: first stage balcony beneath a pair of Roman Quarter Jacks striking bells, and a large clockface, below a cornice and segmental pediment; above this the tower is free-standing, and all 4 faces identical. 3-stage octagonal spire on a short plinth.
The original reredos, an elliptical pediment on Corinthian pilasters with gilded plaster decoration, is now the rood screen. Four-bay nave has elliptical arches on slender Corinthian columns; aisles the same height; Adam-style ceiling decoration.
Restored 1791 octagonal timber pulpit with painted plaster cherub heads, back and and inlaid tester; the original font is now the lectern base. At the rear is the 1708 organ loft by Renatus Harris, above a balcony on 2 Corinthian columns. In the north-west corner is a segmental arch to a bay containing a small octagonal font. The Quarter Jacks came from the earlier church, 1728, by James Paty, and many of the memorials from St. Ewen's (demolished). The dragon weathervane was on the top of the spire of the old church. The Quarter Jacks were drawn by Johnson, etc. (BRSMG M2345-M2347).
Burial in the crypt of the church was ended by an Order in Council of 18 February 1854 made under the Burial Act of 1852.
The Renatus Harris organ was rebuilt in 1837 by John Smith and Sons, Bristol-based organ builders, and was refurbished later in the nineteenth century by another Bristol firm, W. G. Vowles.
