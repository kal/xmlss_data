monument_desc Ring's clay tobacco pipe factory, located on the northern side of Temple Back. The business was founded by John Ring in partnership with Frederick Cookworthy in 1802 (Price et al., 1984 264) and a factory established "opposite George's Deal Yard" on Temple Back. The factory was gutted by a fire so that, according to the Bristol Mirror of 27th July 1811, "in little more than an hour nothing more than bare walls were left standing" but was soon operating again. In 1817 Ring's partnership with Cookworthy was dissolved and Ring took over the running of the factory alone until his death in August 1818. The business and premises were auctioned and may have been bought by a William Bissix (Price et al., 1984 267). The business was taken on by John Ring's elder brother Richard Frank Ring who built a factory - the Ohio Tobacco-Pipe Manufactory - on the north side of Commercial Road at Temple Back which exported pipes to the Caribbean, the east coast of the United States, Canada and Ireland. By 1861 the business had become Richard Charles Ring & Co. although its interests diversified considerably and by 1884 the factory had apparently been sold to Hawley & Co. The factory was vacant at the time of a fire insurance survey in August 1896 and the second edition Ordnance Survey map of 1902 indicates that the factory had, by then, been demolished.
Some archaeological work has taken place on the site since the 1980s. Part of a muffle kiln from the factory was recovered during excavation of a service pipe in the mid 1980s [BUAD 3313] by Reg Jackson (Egan, 1984 318-319; Peacey, 1996 33-34). Part of the site was excavated in 1995 [BUAD 517] by Rod Burchill (Burchill, 1995; Williams, 1996 86). Large-scale office development at Temple Quay in the late 1990s destroyed most of the monument and only small elements, if anything, will remain.
