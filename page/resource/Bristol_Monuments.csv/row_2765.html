monument_desc Back Hall or Spicer's Hall, located at the northern end of Welsh Back.
Hall, aligned north-south. Rectangular plan. Arch-braced trusses to roof, some supported on corbels with carved human faces. Two tiers of cusped windbraces; two purlins to each side. Of oak. Two small windows above blind arch in north end wall. In 1826, an arched doorway at south end of west wall and square doorway at east end of south wall, this area interpreted as a former gallery. In the south-west corner near the arched doorway was a pulpit, destroyed in Mr.Attwood's absence.
The hall was a ground-floor structure. There was the surviving end of a blind arch in the north end wall. Tall traceried window with two-centred arch at north end of west wall, bricked up. Two-centred arch to doorway on ground-floor; similar but slightly wider example above, bricked up, with small opening in wall adjacent to south (with a trefoil head), blocked with stone. A large arched opening further south, with, beyond it, a chimney breast in a later rubble. Most of the west wall was in angular random rubble, all shapes and sizes from small to medium. Quoins in ashlar. Types of stone and colours of mortar are unrecorded. The outside of the wall was much altered. Areas of plaster were recorded on both faces of the wall.
At its base the south wall of the hall was noted as being of angular random rubble. Later fireplaces and flues were noted higher up. It was partly plastered over.
The south end wall of the structure immediately adjacent on the west side of the hall survived largely intact. At ground-floor level was a blind arch, with, apparently, Pennant Sandstone voussoirs. The walls either side were thick, stepping back at first-floor level, where the main room was situated. The bottom of a possible doorway was noted, blocked with rubble, slightly left of centre, with, above and to the right, a window with two-centred arch, similarly blocked. The end wall was plastered at least at this level.
The building was gutted by fire during a bombimg raid on Bristol during the Second World War. The shell of the building was recorded by B. H. St.John O'Neill in 1941, but the final drawings dated 1944. He recorded both sides of the west wall of the hall, also the western end of the north end wall. In addition he drew the south end wall of the adjacent structure, which was part of the same complex.
