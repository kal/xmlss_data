monument_desc Highbury Chapel, located on the eastern side of the junction of Cotham Road and St. Michael's Hill. The chapel was a Congregational chapel. The chapel was constructed between 1842-1843 to a design by William Butterfield and is believed to be his earliest work (Stell 1986, 64). The chapel is constructed of Pennant sandstone rubble with limestone dressings and is in the Perpendicular Gothic style. It has an aisled nave of five bays with a polygonal chancel.
By 1862 accommodation within the chapel had become very limited because of housing development in Cotham, but also because of the popularity of the ministry of Rev. David Thomas. E. W. Godwin was commissioned to expand the chapel. Godwin's scheme followed Butterfield's the style of original design closely but used a mix of Pennant sandstone rubble and freestone for the fabric of the walls. His additions included an apse, a cloister and a freestone tower. Godwin also altered the interior, designing a polygonal oak pulpit on a freestone base, an oak screen across the apse to conceal the organ. He also designed a stained glass memorial window for the west end of the chapel, which was made by Joseph Bell (Soros 1999, 138). On the wall of the north-east nave respond there is a monument to Arnold Thomas by Eric Gill. This was executed in 1924 and takes the form of a shepherd and sheep.
The chapel was bought by the Church of England c1975 and is now Cotham Parish Church. The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/4/1195). The listing notes the building to be "an exceptionally early and unusual example of the application of the Gothic Revival style to nonconformist chapel architecture."
