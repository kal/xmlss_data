monument_desc St. Philip's Poor House, located on the western side of Pennywell Road. The building was constructed c.1698-1700 according to Winstone (1971, pls.89-92) and was a two-storey structure of Pennant sandstone rubble with classical mouldings of ashlar stone around the door and window openings.
An Indenture for the poorhouse dated 1803 (BRO P.StP&J/OP/2) refers to a lease of 1730 for a "good raparious and substantial workhouse".
Jean Rocque's 1742 map of Bristol indicates that the building had a C-shaped plan, the central range set back with the side ranges reaching to Pennywell Road. The building measured 180 feet (55 metres) by 80 feet (25 metres).
Plumley & Ashmead's 1828 survey depicts the northwestern side of the poorhouse as a single rectangular block that was wider in the centre separate from the two wings on the north-east and south-west side of a yard area.
The south-western half of the building and the northern range appear to have been demolished by the late-nineteenth century and the site redeveloped. The north-eastern half of the building survived into the twentieth century and was photographed when in a dilapidated condition in the early 1920s (Winstone 1971, pls.89-92). This remaining element building was demolished on 28 December 1925 (Winstone 1971).
