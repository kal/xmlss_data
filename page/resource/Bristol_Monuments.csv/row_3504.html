monument_desc Shirehampton National Schools, located on the eastern side of Station Road, Shirehampton. The site was granted by Philip John Miles of the Kingsweston Estate in the mid-1840s for the establishment of a "National School" (BRO 11054(32)). The school seems to have been built in the mid-1840s, apparently to a design by the architect Henry Rumley. Rumley's initial scheme of 1847 (BRO 222638(36)) does not appear to have been implemented but a school was probably in existence on the site soon afterwards. As built the school was a single-storey structure of coursed sandstone rubble, with Perpendicular Gothic ashlar details and a pitched roof. There was apparently a lantern at the northern end of the roof and a projecting chimneypiece at the centre of the roof. By January 1861 a new schoolroom had been added to the building (BRO P/St.MS/Sch/2(3)). The internal plan consisted of classrooms for the mixed school on the northern and southern sides of the entrance of Station Road, the southern being a large L-shaped space. There was an infants classroom behind these rooms and two pairs of toilets attached to the exterior of the north and east sides of the building. A new extension, designed by P. J. Byrne, was constructed at the southern end of the building in 1892 (BRO P/St.MS/Sch/2(6)). This matched the scale, style and materials of the original school. The school was apparently badly damaged by the explosion of a bomb close by on 29 March 1941 (Etheridge 2006, 13). The school was closed in the second half of the twentieth century and by 1972 was listed in local street directories as the Shirehampton Social Club. A café was added to the northern end of the building and the remainder had become a snooker hall by the end of the twentieth century.
