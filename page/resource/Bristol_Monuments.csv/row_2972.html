monument_desc A rank of four shops on the west side of the Exchange Avenue. The rank was probably designed in c.1848 by R. S. Pope as part of his reworking of St. Nicholas Market (Leech, 2001) and is recorded by the Ordnance Survey first edition 1:500 plan (Bristol Sheet LXXI.16.25) and the Goad Fire Insurance plan of 1887.
The main, east-facing, elevation on Exchange Avenue was an ashlar façade of four bays with moulded segmental-headed arches, each framing a shop. At the shoulder of the arches was a deep impost-band and the arches had keystones. Above these was a plat band and parapet. Each of the shops had a frontage 2.6 metres wide on Exchange Avenue and was 3.7 metres long and had a first floor level which was hidden behind the parapet. At the south end of the elevation was a doorway leading to a small rear yard and now the entrance to the toilets. In the footway of Exchange Avenue, in front of the rank is an iron plate covering the entrance to a cellar beneath the structure. The condition of this cellar is unknown.
The northern two of the rank were demolished in the early twentieth century when the rear of the Old Post Office was rebuilt and extended to the south. The southern two shops are extant and were subject to archaeological survey by Dr. Roger Leech in October 1999 [BUAD 3518] (Leech, 1999) and some stripping of plaster on selected internal walls to determine the development of the structure in early 2001 [BUAD 3761] (Leech, 2001). The rear wall of the building was found to pre-date construction of the shops. The shops have been altered internally, a scar in the rubble wall forming the north wall of the building suggesting that the roof had been altered. A partition of late eighteenth- or early nineteenth-century date had been inserted to sub-divide the first floor. Nothing of the original interior of the shops survives.
