monument_desc The Elms, Brislington, located on the south side of a lane leading north-east from Bath Hill. The date of construction of the house is unknown, although it probably dates from the 18th century, but it is recorded by the Brislington Tithe Survey of 1843. A photograph taken around 1903 (Winstone 1985, pl.331) indicates that the house was double-fronted with a central doorway with an open-bed pediment, and semi-circular bay windows on either side. A gravel drive led around the house to the south east entrance, but there do not appear to have been formal gardens. A number of outbuildings, of unknown function, lay to the west of the house. The house was demolished in 1939, and the site had been redeveloped by 1960. The extent of surviving archaeological deposits associated with the house and its outbuildings is unknown.
Rebuilt on site of earlier house (Rowe 1985, 13) dating from the seventeenth century when part of an estate granted to Thomas Daniel. In 1660, Rowland Lacy, Lord of the Manor, enfranchised copyhold lands in exchange for small fee farm rents and so Daniel received enfranchisement. The site included the subsequent properties of "The Elms"which was formerly called "Upper Oakenhill", and a second property formerly known as "Lower Oakenhill". The estate remained in the Daniel family until 1775 when it was sold to William Hall. (Ireland Manuscripts). Rowe (1985, 13) states the house was rebuilt in the eighteenth century. In 1780 the estate was sold to Nehemiah Bartley, distiller from Bristol, and as one of the nouveau riche merchants may well have been responsible for the rebuilding of the house. There were several owners in rapid succession until 1796 when the house became part of the Clayfield-Ireland estate. In the early 19th century, the house was let to the Rev. Thomas Browne Simpson, Vicar of Keynsham, from 1804-1818. Mrs Susanna Ricketts rented the property in 1846 after leaving Brislington Hall following the death of her husband, Jacob Ricketts. After her death in 1870 there followed a number of tenants with Egbert Spear being the last occupier in 1912. After his death in 1936, the house remained unoccupied and was badly damaged by fire in 1938, and was demolished a year later. A Scots Fir tree, one of several which grew on the edge of the lawn, was recorded as surviving still in 1985.
