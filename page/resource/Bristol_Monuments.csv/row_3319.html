monument_desc British School, located on the southern side of Redcross Street, Old Market. A school appears to have opened on the site in 1819. This was run by The Society for Promoting the Lancasterian System for the Education of the Poor according to the principles set out by Joseph Lancaster. Lancaster was a member of the Society of Friends who sought to create a network of schools for the children of what he called the "industrious classes" (Lancaster 1803). Lancaster's system employed monitors, a select group of older pupils who were taught by the master, who, in turn, taught the remaining children. In 1814 the Society was renamed the British and Foreign School Society (BFSS) and the school had become known as the British School by the 1840s. The school initially accepted only boys but a girls school was established in 1813. At 31 December 1841 there were 170 boys and 105 girls on the school roll (Anon 1841).
The building was built as a three-storey structure of Pennant sandstone rubble bonded in a lime mortar and with a pitched roof. The phasing of construction has not been established but minor alterations including a new playground, had been made by 1846 and in 1850 William Armstrong, an architect based in Brunswick Square, designed a new classroom extension with raked seating at the southern end.
The school was closed in 1889 because it did not meet the Board School standards and was converted to industrial use. The building was subsequently largely gutted during the twentieth century and the roof removed and a flat roof created. It was occupied by a wrestling club by 2003.
