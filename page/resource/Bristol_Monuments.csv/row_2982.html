monument_desc Building located on the east side of the north-west leg of Greyfriars, at the bottom of the steps leading up into Blackfriars. The building was probably one of the conventual buildings of the Franciscan Friary. It appears to have been located amongst the buildings on the north side of the cloister and was likely to have been close to the eastern end of the refectory. The function of the building is unclear but a survey by T. S. Pope in 1892 recorded that the building measured 31 feet 6 inches from north to south and 11 feet 6 inches from east to west and the height from ground floor to the ridge of the roof was 29 feet. The elements of the shell walls, including a buttress at first-floor level were of medieval date and at the rear there was an "outer hall or lobby" which gave access "from the ground floor to the land on the higher level" via a flight of steps (Weare 1893, 33-34). Internally the building appeared originally to have comprised a hall at both ground and first floor. Parts of two Gothic round trefoil windows were extant in the west-facing, Greyfriars, elevation until c.1921. Pope's drawings of these windows were published in Weare (1893).
By the early twentieth century the building had been converted into two dwellings. The building was of two storeys and two bays and a pitched pantile roof. In the south facing elevation there were central sashes at first-floor and attic level. The dwellings had adjacent entrances in the west-facing elevation. A tall square brick chimney stack projected from the centre of the eaves above the western elevation. Immediately to the north of this there was a low single-light dormer window.
By 1931 (BUAD 2265) the building was ruined. Its site now lies beneath the northern part of the western range of the Whitefriars office block.
