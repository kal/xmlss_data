monument_desc Barstaple's Almshouse or Trinity Hospital, on the south side of Old Market Street, on the east corner of Jacob Street. The almshouse appears to have been founded by John Barstaple (died 1411) in the early-fifteenth century during the reign of Henry IV and was confirmed by a grant of Henry V in February 1417 (Manchee 1831, 499-500). It appears to have occupied sites on both sides of Old Market Street from its foundation. The fraternity of the Holy Trinity and St. George ran the almshouse and William Worcestre, writing in 1480, notes that John Barstable had appointed Nicholas Barstable as "master of the priests of the chapel of the Holy Trinity" (Neale 2000, 82-83).
The original form of the almshouse is uncertain and the main evidence comes from watercolour drawings (BRSMG M2764, BRSMG M2772 and M2773) dating to 1822 and 1825 in the Braikenridge Collection, Bristol Museum and Art Gallery. The buildings appear to have been arranged around a courtyard. The accommodation buildings were of two-storeys with traceried windows of late-medieval date. At first-floor there was gallery or corridor which projected from the main range and was supported on corbels. The entrance doors seem to have been set within ashlar mouldings with four-centred arched heads.
The chapel is reported to have been rebuilt in 1716, the work being funded by the Bristol Corporation at a cost of £463 4s. 3d (Manchee 1831, 80). It was apparently reworked again in the early 1790s in a Gothick style (Leighton 1933) and some of the rooms in the almshouse were rebuilt in 1814 at an expense of £140 (Manchee 1831, 79).
The entire almshouse was rebuilt between the mid-1850s and the early 1880s to designs in a Tudor Revival style by Foster and Wood (who also designed the rebuilt Foster's Almshouses in Colston Street ). All of the almshouse buildings are in squared Pennant sandstone rubble with ashlar detailing. The north-east block dates to 1857-1858, while the chapel and the north-west and south-west blocks were reconstructed in 1867. The south-east block dates to 1881-1883. These later blocks show a Burgundian Gothic influence.
The chapel is a single-storey building. The main entrance to the chapel is off Jacob Street, and consists of a doorway with an ogee hood in the centre of the elevation. The three-light east window has Perpendicular tracery and an ogee hood, with crocketed niches on either side and also above it. The Old Market Street elevation has a three-window range, with two-light arched windows. The roof is gabled on the eastern side and hipped on the western, with small square bell tower on the northern side of the ridge.
The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/12/1633).
