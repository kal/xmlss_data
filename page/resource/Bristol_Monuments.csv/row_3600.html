monument_desc Old Council House, located on the north-western side of Corn Street. The building was constructed as a Council chamber and treasurer's office between 1824 and 1827 to a design by Sir Robert Smirke. The sculpture on the building was by E. H. Baily. Extended 1828 by R. S. Pope and G. Dymock. Limestone ashlar with Pennant ashlar plinth, roof not visible. Neoclassical style. 2 storeys, attic and basement; 5-window range, with a 2-storey, 3-window left-hand extension.
The Council House is symmetrical with a plinth to a moulded band, articulated above by giant pilasters to an entablature, the end window sections set back, and the centre recessed between giant fluted Ionic columns; a mid C20 attic storey behind outer balustrades, tall panelled parapets and recessed centre with a statue of Justice.
Steps up to a large doorway, 2-leaf doors with 6 flush panels with roundels; windows with 6/6-pane sashes. Parapet sections flanking the centre have raised heraldic panels, and 2 attic windows flank the statue. The right return has a symmetrical 5-window range with end sections set forward between pilasters, 4 low basement windows have 8/8-pane sashes.
To the left in Corn Street, the former Treasurer's Office is symmetrical with a plinth and cornice. Distyle Greek Doric columns to the entrance, with an entablature with triglyphs and cornice to a recessed doorway, now a window with ashlar panel beneath the cill; windows have 6/6-pane sashes. Low basement windows have grilles.
Inside, wide entrance and stair hall with dogleg stair; first-floor rear former Council Chamber; basement with semicircular-arched tunnel vaults.
Housed the Council House and offices until relocation to College Green after the Second World War, and after used as crown courts. The Treasurer's Office was added when the House was found to provide insufficient space.
