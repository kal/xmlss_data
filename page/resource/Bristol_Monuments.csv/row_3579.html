monument_desc Twyford House, located on the western side of High Street, Shirehampton. The building appears to have been constructed in the first half of the eighteenth century as a three-storey, three-bay stone rubble house with pitched roof. There are chimneystacks at the apex of the gables at either end of the roof. The main entrance door is at the centre of the north--west facing elevation. There is a single sash window at first and second floor above the door.
At ground floor the internal plan of the building appears originally to have comprised a narrow central hall with large rooms to the north and south. The stair appears to date to the first half of the eighteenth century and the door and doorcase to the northern room are of similar date. At first- and second-floor the plan was similar and the doors and doorframes to the principal rooms are also of early-eighteenth century date, although at second-floor the openings are smaller than those of the floors below.
The house was first recorded by Taylor's plan of the area of 1777. In the early-nineteenth century the building was refronted with ashlar stone. As part of this work full-height bow windows were added, the northern and southern bays each containing three sash windows on each floor. At the same time sash windows were inserted into the south wall of the southern room of the first- and second-floors in a similar position to that on the floor below, and presumably at the same date. A large kitchen was added to the north-west corner of the original building. Single-storey outbuildings of stone rubble bonded in a white lime mortar also appear to have been constructed to the west of the house at this date. In the late nineteenth century the chimneypiece, cornice and skirting of the northern room were replaced.
In the 1908 a larger, two-storey extension, a "smoke room," was added to the south-west corner of the house (BRO Building Plans Vol.55 fo.11). Part of the rear wall of the original house had been removed to double the size of the southern room.
By 1935 the building was occupied by the Christian Science movement. In the later 1970s or the 1980s it passed into the ownership of Avon County Council which appears to have made a number of alterations, including the insertion of steel girders to support the floors. The pitched slate roof was also covered with bitumen.
The building has a Grade II listing (Listed Building 901-1/52/1751).
