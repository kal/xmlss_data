monument_desc Sea Mills Dock was the third wet dock (or floating harbour) in Britain. It was constructed in 1712 by John Padmore for Joshua Francklyn, a Bristol merchant. The dock was unpopular because of its distance from the city centre and fell into disuse after 1766. A plan of the Southwell estate of 1772 records the dock as essentially rectangular, with a long wall on the north side and north-west to south-east walls forming the entrance and eastern sides. There was apparently no dock wall on the southern side and the Trym flowed into the Dock at the eastern corner.
The upper part of the Dock was disturbed by the construction of the bridge carrying the Portway in the 1920s. The only structural elements now visible are the two piers at the entrance. That on the north is the best preserved, and contains remnants of two sluice gates (Buchanan & Cossons 1969, 37-38).
