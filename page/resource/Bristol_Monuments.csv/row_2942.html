monument_desc No.26 Small Street, located on the east side of Small Street. The building appears to have had a medieval origin, with a house recorded on the site from 1350. It was rebuilt in the fifteenth century as a timber-framed two-bay structure with a hall. In 1499 the building was granted to Matilda Baker, widow, by Thomas Hardyng. It subsequently became part of the endowment of Forthey's chantry and was recorded as belonging to St. Philip's parish in 1540. From 1607 until 1623 Elizabeth Warden resided in the house and in 1649 it was purchased by Alderman Jackson and remained in the possession of the family for some years. Significant alterations to the building, apparently in the second quarter of the seventeenth century, including refronting and the insertion of a floor into the hall to create a new room. The decorative scheme for this upper room included a moulded plaster ceiling and a carved stone chimney piece. The ground floor room also had a stone chimney piece, moulded plaster ceiling and panelled walls. Externally the building was a three-storey jettied house with a gabled pantile roof. The first and second floors were jettied.
The building was demolished in the autumn of 1921. W. F. Read visited the building before demolition (BUAD 3040) but found that "it had been repaired so many times externally that probably no part of the original structure remained" (Pritchard 1922, 94). The chimney pieces were extant at the time of demolition, though that in the ground floor room was damaged, as were the plaster ceilings (Pritchard 1922, 94-97).
