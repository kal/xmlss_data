monument_desc The Assembly Room, located on the west side of Prince Street, to the north the junction of Prince Street with Assembly Rooms Lane. The Corporation made a grant of land for the erection of the building in 1753, subject to the right to occupy the hall for up to six days per year. Funds were raised for construction by a tontine subscription with 120 members each taking a £30 share in 1754. The building was erected to a design probably by the architect William Halfpenny (the professional name used by Michael Hoare) and was completed in 1755 (Ison, 1952 109).

The main elevation faced Prince Street. Attached to the north and west. Approached by steps which led into the ground-floor, which was rusticated to the Prince Street frontage. Central bay set forward; one segmental-headed window to each side of entrance. Pediment supported by two pairs of Corinthian columns above the rusticated storey. Central Venetian window, with, above, an entablature bearing the inscription "Curas Cithara tollit" ("Music dispels care"). To the each side a tall sash window with pediment. The ball-room was lofty, 90 feet long by 45 wide, with plaster decorations; two doorways led to the retiring rooms; lit by rows of segmental-headed windows on each side, with rusticated jambs and voussoirs. Carved and panelled ceiling to ball-room, with cornice; gallery at east end. Interior illustrated in TBGAS,29,opp.p.24. Jackson's 1825 illustration (M2508) shows a canopy over the front steps.
Elevation to the lane of a 7-window range. Right-hand entrance with pediment; 4 windows, entrance, and window, all with semicircular heads with rusticated voussoirs. Similar windows to first-floor, with 7 recessed panels above. Plain parapet.

In 1811 the building was converted to theatrical use by its owner William Jenkins, becoming the Regency Theatre. The proprietors of the Theatre Royal took action to close the theatre, arguing that the performances there were "extremely injurious to their Interests" (Barker, 1974 75-76). The building was demolished in 1956 (Gomme et al., 1979 155).
