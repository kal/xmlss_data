monument_desc Seven Stars public house, located on the south side of Thomas Lane. An inn known as the "Starrs" or the "Seven Stars," on or close to the site of the present building, appears to have existed in Thomas Lane by the 1670s. In 1671 an annuity of 30 shillings was granted to the Feoffees of St. Thomas by Richard Pope on the issues of the house known by the sign of the "Starrs," adjacent to St. Thomas Church. Of the annuity, 10 shillings was to be used for an annual sermon on the Sunday after All Saints Day and 20 shillings was to be distributed on the same day among poor parishioners who did not receive weekly alms.
The present building is believed to have been constructed in the second half of the eighteenth century and is a rendered building of two-storeys, attic and cellar with a pantile mansard roof. The main, north-west facing, elevation has a three-window range with the main entrance at the centre. It is not clear whether any earlier fabric survives within the building from any earlier structure.
In 1787 the anti-slavery campaigner Thomas Clarkson (1760-1846), in Bristol to collect evidence on the slave trade for the London-based Committee for Effecting the Abolition of the Slave Trade, met the then landlord of the Seven Stars, a Mr Thompson, who put him in contact with several sailors with experience of slaving voyages.
The building continued in use as a public house through the nineteenth century and by 1882 the landlord was James Clements.
The building has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/42/297).
