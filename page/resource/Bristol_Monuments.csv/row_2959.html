monument_desc The Dominican Friary, or Blackfriars. The friary was founded in 1227/8 by Maurice de Gaunt (R. Graham The religious houses. In Page 1907). Its layout has not yet been confirmed but the friary church (BUAD 1359M) appears to have been located on the north side of the site close to Rosemary Lane. On its south side was the cloister, with the church lying along its northern edge, the frater to its west, and the chapter house, sacristy, etc., on the eastern side. Cutler's Hall, is likely to have been the dorter and to the south of this was the lesser cloister. The building now known as Bakers Hall may have been the infirmary.
The house was surrendered on 10th September 1538. The site was purchased by the former mayor William Chester in June 1540 for £37 10s and was apparently used as a private dwelling with many of the buildings of the friary apparently being put to new use with the minimum of new building. The church would appear to have been incorporated into other buildings, although some of the stone was used to rebuild an altar and Easter sepulchre in St. Werburgh's church in 1552-53 (Skeeters 1993, 82). In 1933 tracery windows dating to the fourteenth century was discovered within one of the walls of a garage (BUAD 2378). The larger of these was damaged but survived to its full height, suggesting that demolition of the friary church after the dissolution was partial and at least some of its fabric was incorporated into other buildings. The site was acquired by the wealthy grocer Dennis Hollister in the mid seventeenth century for his residence and when he joined the fledgling Quaker movement in the city in 1654 open air meetings were held there. The Society of Friends meeting house (BUAD 241M) was later added to the rear of the linking block, New Hall.
Two of the conventual buildings, later known as the Bakers Hall (BUAD 239M) and Cutlers Hall (BUAD 240M), are extant along with part of a linking block. The surviving buildings are of Pennant sandstone rubble with limestone dressings, and pantile roof. The present New Hall is an 1869 rebuilding of a thirteenth-century hall, and was restored in 1970. Bakers Hall and Cutlers Hall are Scheduled Ancient Monuments (AV 102) and also have Grade II* listings (Baker's Hall is Listed Building number 901-1/40/489 and Cutler's Hall is Listed Building number 901-1/40/490). New Hall has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/40/493).
