monument_desc Temple Back pottery, located to the east of Temple Church, on the far side of Petticoat Lane. Richard Frank left his pottery in Redcliff for this new location and seems to have amalgamated his business with the next door Water Lane pottery [BUAD 87M]. Joseph Ring took over the business in after marrying Frank's daughter Elizabeth and, with the help of Anthony Hassells, the pottery began to produce "Queen's Ware," a cream -coloured earthenware, in 1786. Ring died in April 1788 and the business was taken over by his brother Robert Ring and Henry Carter. Joseph Ring II and John Decimus Pountney came into the partnership in 1813 and the firm traded as Pountney and Carter until Pountney's death in 1852. In 1822 the pottery consisted of ranges of buildings arranged around four kilns, two adjacent gloss kilns and two biscuit kilns. This arrangement was retained into the early 1880s when it was recorded by the 1:500 Ordnance Survey first edition plan. The throwing room was on the west side of the kilns while the Dripping and Placing Room, the Hot House and the Enamel kiln were on their north side. A yard separated these buildings from the Plate Makers, Dish Makers shops and the Gig Room. The Mill Houses and steam engine were along the south-east boundary of the site. The pottery closed in 1886 and the centre of the site had been redeveloped by August 1896 as a Day Industrial School. The site is now occupied by The Crescent Centre.
