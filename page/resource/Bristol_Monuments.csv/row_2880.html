monument_desc The Rose and Crown public house, No.38 Broadmead, located on the west side of the junction of Broadmead and Merchant Street. The building was of probable seventeenth century date and its footprint measured 13.9m from north-east to south-west and 5.5 metres east-west.
The building was of three storeys and attic and was of probable seventeenth-century date. The north-facing elevation was of three storeys and attic with a two-window range. By the early twentieth century the ground-floor had a central entrance with public house windows, all forward of the main elevation (perhaps reflecting a hidden jetty behind). The fenestration at first and second floors comprised pairs of sashes.
Parapet, behind which a roof turning from a gable end against the next building (west) to the ridge roof above the east elevation; single dormer window. The east-facing elevation was of three storeys and the first-floor was jettied. The fenestration of the northern and the adjacent bay comprised a single sash at both first and second floors.
The elevation had three gables, the right-hand joining a short return of the parapet on the north-facing elevation. There were no attic windows. There was a chimney stack at the southern end of the building.
The building had been demolished by 1935. The site of the building is now beneath the south-western quadrant of The Podium.
