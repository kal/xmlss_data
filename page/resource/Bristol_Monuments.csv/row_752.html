monument_desc The Redcliff Wharf glass house. The glass house was extant by 1700 when it was owned by Thomas Ewens who seems to have retained it until 1717. It is shown by Millerd's map of c.1715 which indicates that the glasshouse then consisted of two small rectangular buildings. It was then acquired by a Captain Wheeler, who ran it until 1723, and then by John Jones who held it until his death in 1740. His widow sold the works to Crosse and Berrow in 1750 and it was acquired by Little and Longman in 1761. Robert Vigor joined the business in 1767 and by 1775 it had become Vigor, Stevens and Hill. In 1802 the company amalgamated with Wadham Ricketts and Co. and documentary evidence indicates that in the early nineteenth century the works was producing glass bottles, some of which were being shipped to New York; a Braikenridge illustration of the 1820s shows the glass furnace with a wooden superstructure. The glasshouse appears to have closed down in the later 1820s.
A single archaeological evaluation trench dug in 1989 (BUAD 446) found possible evidence of the glass works, including several stone walls as well as a brick floor covered with a blue, glassy substance. Some disturbance to the deposits was noted. The extent of the monument is currently unknown.
