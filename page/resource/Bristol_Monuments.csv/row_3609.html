monument_desc Church of St. Mary the Virgin, Henbury located at the south end of Church Close, off Henbury Road. A church is believed to have been built on the site in the Saxon period. No fabric pre-dating the Norman Conquest of 1066 is believed to remain within the structure, and it was apparently substantially rebuilt in the second half of the twelfth century when a new nave was created. The chancel was rebuilt in the 1280s. The nave retains wome evidence for fire damage but this is undated and it is not clear if it was related to the late-thirteenth century building. The fabric of the tower has been dated to the sixteenth- and seventeenth centuries and it contains a peal of eight bells, the bells having been cast between 1717 and 1897.
The church was altered by the architectural practice of Thomas Rickman (1776-1841) and Henry Hutchinson (1800-1831) in the 1830s (who had designed several churches nationally and also St. John's College, Cambridge). Their changes were not considered successful, however, and the architect George E. Street (1824-1881) carried out further restoration work between 1874 and 1877. Several of the windows were reglazed with stained glass in the later nineteenth century, though John Harford donated some Flemish glazing dating to the sixteenth century, which is now in the vestry. A new organ was built between 1876 and 1878 by the Bristol-based firm of W. G. Vowles. Within the church there is a brass plaque commemorating the parishioners who were killed during the Second World War (1939-1945).
The church had chapels-at-ease within the parish at Northwick, Aust and Compton Greenfield.
The church has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building Number 901-1/17/1323).
