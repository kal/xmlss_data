monument_desc Teast's Dock, later known as Albert Dock. The dry dock (a shipbuilding dock) was built at the south-western corner of an inlet off of the western side of St. Augustine's Reach. The dock was sited on the position of a graving dock, shown on Rocque's 1742 map, which was apparently created in the early eighteenth century when the harbour was tidal. The dock was subsequently turned into a dry dock by the addition of a pair of lock gates and, Plumley and Ashmead's 1828 map suggests, the building of stone walls. Part of the dock wall was recorded during an archaeological evaluation in 1997 (BUAD 3300) which was constructed of Pennant sandstone bonded in a off-white mortar. A layer of yellow/brown silty clay was found which the excavator suggested may have been a working surface for the dock (Cox, 1998). The dock had a number of associated structures.
The Teast family took over running of the dock between about 1790 and 1832. It later passed into the hands of the Peters family, who renamed it Albert Dock. The dock was infilled soon after 1884.
