monument_desc The Merchant Tailor's Almshouse, located on the east side of Merchant Street. The Society of Merchant Tailors' had established its almshouse in four tenements it owned on the western side of Merchant Street by 1630 (Fox, 1880 25). Its role seems to have been to provide accommodation for members of the Company in reduced circumstances, in accordance with the responsibility placed on the wardens by the charters which established the Company at the end of the fourteenth century.
The present building was erected as a replacement for the earlier almshouse in 1701, largely using the Company's own funds. The building is of two storeys with ranges arranged on three sides of a central courtyard to give a U-shaped plan. It is a rendered brick structure (one of the earliest brick buildings in Bristol) with ashlar dressings. The main entrance doorway has a bracketed shell hood and above is a large, painted panel bearing the arms of the Merchant Tailor's Guild, and flanking it the inscription "ANNODM/1701." The almshouse was designed to provide accommodation for nine people, with each range of the building having three individual dwellings.
By the late 1820s the Company of Merchant Tailors' was effectively defunct, having only one surviving member, a Mr. Isaac Amos (Manchee, 1831 205). The almshouse was inspected around the same time and was described as "a handsome and capacious building, well adapted for the accommodation and comfort of the description of persons for whom it was designed; but in its present state, and under the circumstances to which the company . is reduced, it must be considered as producing very little benefit as a charity, and as depending for its continuance on a very precarious support" (Manchee, 1831 222).
The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/40/485).
