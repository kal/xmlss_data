monument_desc Holy Trinity Church, Westbury-on-Trym. Dawson suggests that it is likely that the church stands on the site of a Saxon minster. He also notes that the plan of the nave and chancel are irregular and therefore "may reflect the survival of part of the pre-conquest building in the present structure" (Dawson 1981, 20). The south aisle dates to the thirteenth century.
The majority of the church was rebuilt in the fifteenth century, changes including the reconstruction of the tower and the insertion of a clerestorey with "unusually small windows" in the nave (Gomme et. al. 1979, 68).
An organ was built by John Smith senior of the Bristol organ builders John Smith and Sons in 1824. Under the then incumbent, Revd. William Cartwright, the church was restored in 1851 and heating ducts inserted, the boiler house being located in Bishop Carpenter's crypt beneath the chancel. In 1852-53 the architect John Norton was commissioned to design a new vestry at the north-east corner of the building and galleries for children within the tower and on its north side. The galleries were served by stairs against the north wall of the tower. With the alterations the church could accommodate 979 worshippers. There were 389 appropriated settings, 350 free sittings on the ground floor and the galleries and 199 seats for children in the galleries.
The building has a Grade I listing (Listed Building Number 901-1/26/2047).
