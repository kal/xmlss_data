monument_desc Portland Chapel, located on the south side of Portland Street, Cotham. The chapel was built in 1791-1792 to serve the growing Wesleyan Methodist congregation living on the north side of Bristol and one of the principal movers behind the establishment was Captain Thomas Webb (c.1726-1796), who had been a significant figure in the establishment of Methodism in the Americas. As a Loyalist, Webb had returned to live in England in 1778.
The chapel had five bays, with a slightly projecting centre comprising three bays with a pediment above. The external walls were rendered and the fenestration consisted of a semi-circular arched window with freestone dressings in each bay. The chapel had a pitched roof with louvred hexagonal lantern at the west end.
The interior originally measured 17 metres from east to west and was 11.9 metres wide, with a small apse at the east end. At the west end there was a gallery supported on Tuscan columns.
The seating was altered in 1864 and the chapel was extended to the west in 1871-1872, when a polygonal organ chamber was added to north wall (Stell 1986, 68). The Ordnance Survey first edition 1:500-scale town plan surveyed in 1881 (Bristol Sheet LXXI.16.9) records that the chapel then had seating for 630 worshippers and also indicates that there was a burial ground on the western side of the chapel.
The chapel closed in 1970-1971, and was demolished in the same decade. The remains of Thomas Webb and his wife were removed from the burial ground and reinterred in the courtyard of the New Room (BUAD 256M) off Broadmead. The site was subsequently redeveloped for housing.
