monument_desc No.94 St. Thomas Street, located on the west side of St. Thomas Street, to the south of Three Queens Lane. The building was a rendered timber-framed, single-bay house of two storeys and attic and probably dated to the later seventeenth- or early eighteenth-century. The design of No.93 St. Thomas Street was very similar (see the late nineteenth- or early twentieth-century photograph in the Ghey Collection), suggesting that the two were constructed as a pair, and possibly as a part of a terrace of three with the larger No.95 St. Thomas Street (BUAD 1440M).
By the end of the nineteenth century at ground-floor level the main, east-facing, elevation on St. Thomas Street comprised part of the frontage of the White Hart public house. However, it retained its original narrow ground-floor window. The first-floor was jettied and had a window opening at the centre. A projecting feature which extended across the full width of the frontages of Nos.93-95 divided second from attic storey. The opening in the attic gable was a slightly smaller and contained a central casement window. The building had a pantiled roof which was half-hipped to the front. There was a large chimney stack, apparently of stone rubble, rising from the north wall. The Goad Fire Insurance plan of January 1887 suggests that in plan the ground floor had a single large room with a yard to the rear. An opening had been created in the centre of the party wall with No.95, although it is not clear whether this alteration was associated with the use of No.95 as a public house or its earlier use as a pottery.
The exact date of construction of the building has not yet been established. The 1800 Land Tax assessment records that the property was owned by William Hopkins (Jackson 2001, 5). By the late nineteenth century the house had been incorporated into the White Hart public house. Along with No.95 St. Thomas Street (BUAD 850M) and the two properties to the south, No.94 St. Thomas Street was bought by Samuel Hannam for use as a timber yard and No.94 was subsequently demolished to make way for a new brick warehouse (Jackson 2001). The site was still being used as a timber yard by the early 1950s but the eastern part was was taken for widening of St. Thomas Street in the late 1950s. Much of the site of No.94 St. Thomas Street now lies beneath the highway.
