monument_desc No.40, Wade Street, located on the north-eastern side of Wade Street. The house was built as part of the development of Great Ann Street and Great George Street between 1700 and 1720. This large development, unusually in Bristol at that time, was intended to provide housing for artisans and craftsmen (R. H. Leech 'The processional city: some issues for historical archaeology' in Tarlow & West (eds.) 1999, 19-34).
The building was completed by 1707 and on 27 July that year was conveyed by Nathaniel Wade to a Mr C. Slade. It appears to have been a single bay, three-storey house, originally with a plan consisting of a single room to each floor, and was constructed of Pennant sandstone rubble (Young 2000).
By 1879 the England family, with a locally-established blacksmith's business, set up a smithy at No.40 Wade Street. The forge was "behind the house through a passage" (quoted in Erskine 2000) and in 1882 the business belonged to J. England. In 1921 the property was sold to a farrier named Roberts who retired in 1931. The Bristol Corporation acquired the site by Compulsory Purchase on 18 December 1936. The building was subsequently demolished, apparently demolished in the mid-1950s.
