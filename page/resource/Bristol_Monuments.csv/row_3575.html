monument_desc Kings Weston House. Sir Robert Southwell bought the Kingsweston estate in 1679 and it was inherited by Sir Edward Southwell at his father's death in 1702. Southwell undertook some changes to the garden in 1709 and decided to rebuild the gabled house, commissioning Sir John Vanbrugh to design the new building c.1710. Many of Vanbrugh's original drawings are contained within the Kingsweston Book of Drawings, held by Bristol Record Office (and also published (Downes, 1967)). The foundations of the earlier house were apparently reused and Downes suggests, from the evidence of letters from Vanbrugh, that Vanbrugh was very willing to accommodate Southwell's opinions on the design (Downes 1987, 342-345). Building was completed in 1725, after Sir Edward Southwell's death.
The house is of limestone ashlar with a seven-window range. It has a U-shaped double-depth plan and is of two-storeys with attic and basement.. At the centre of the building, above the three central bays, is a chimney arcade comprising six square stacks linked by semi-circular arches on each side. Robert Mylne carried out alterations to the building between 1764 and 1775, changing the east and north elevations and reworking the decoration of the interior (Gomme et al. 1979, 107-111). Vanbrugh also designed a number of the service buildings close to the house including the brewhouse, Loggia - probably an extension to an earlier but now demolished garden building - archaeologically recorded by Bryant (Bryant, 1997), a banqueting house known as The Echo, now surviving only as a shell and the Penpole Gate which was demolished in 1952. The ice house (SMR 3163) lies some 150 metres to the north-east of the main house.
The interior of the house was remodelled for Edward Southwell by the architect Robert Mylne between 1763 and 1772.
The stable block was designed by Mylne in the 1760s (Gomme et al. 1979, 181).The landscape park and gardens designed for Edward Southwell are presumably recorded by a view of Kingsweston of 1712 by Kip but it is not clear to what extent these were redesigned for the new house or subsequently, nor what the quality of the archaeological preservation of these buildings is likely to be.
The house has a Grade I listing (Listed Building Number 901-1/24/1742). The Loggia has a Grade I listing (Listed Building Number 901-1/24/1746), The Brewhouse has a Grade I listing (Listed Building number 901-1/24/1743), The Echo has a Grade I listing (Listed Building number 901-1/24/1744) and the gatepost close to the north-east of the main house has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/24/1745).
