monument_desc St. Peter's Vicarage, located on the south side of Peter Street some 10 metres to the west of the western end of St. Peter's Church [BUAD 513M]. The building was a jettied, single-bay structure of two-storeys plus attic with a gabled pantile roof. There was an entrance on the east side of the main elevation at ground floor and a window to its west. A full-width carved beam separated ground and first floor and this carried the date "1613". There was a central six-light window at first-floor, and evidence for other possible windows. A full-width carved beam with brackets at either end supported the second-floor jetty and there was a central two-light window at second-floor. The building had carved bargeboards with brackets to the eaves and at the apex of the gable was a decorative finial. The side, north-east facing, elevation of the vicarage was blank but a two-storey side extension with a hipped pantile roof had been added. This had an entrance on the west side of the main elevation, adjacent to that of the vicarage itself, with a shop window. The single first-floor window was a sash.
The vicarage was of early seventeenth-century date and G. W. Braikenridge reports that the carved beam at first-floor also carried the initials of Robert Aldworth who was responsible for its construction. By 1825 the building was in use as a shop and was occupied by a basket-maker.
