monument_desc The Royal Western Hotel, St. George's Road. The building was constructed as a hotel between 1838 and 1840 by Isambard Kingdom Brunel, to a design by R. S. Pope. It was intended to provide accommodation to transatlantic travellers before embarking on the onward stage of their journey. It ceased to be a hotel in 1855 and was coverted to use as a turkish baths. It continued to be used as a baths, becoming the Hydro Hotel between the First and Second World Wars. Most of the building was demolished to make way for Brunel House in the late 1970s, although part of the façade was retained.
The building was a two-storey structure with a large gateway at either end of the frontage with a large lunette above each. The centre of the facade at ground floor level was formed by a colonnade (of Doric columns) with sashes above. There was a string line above the colonnade and at the level of the roof parapet. Photographs show a number of hipped roofs.
The surviving façade of the building has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/15/241).
