monument_desc Clifton Down Camp, an Iron Age promontory fort at Clifton Down. The hillfort stood on the east side of the Avon Gorge and forms part of a cluster of three Iron Age hillforts, with Stokeleigh Camp and Burwalls on the western side of the Gorge. The ditches enclose an area of approximately three acres and Burrow (1981, 76; 235-236) argues that the "defences appear to have been bivallate with an entrance on the E[ast]." The entrance has been slightly affected by quarrying. It lies just north of present path into camp from the east and has an over-lapping counterscarp bank. In the north-west corner is a rectangular earthwork (SMR 3223). Sections cut by the Clifton Antiquarian Club through the south-east bank of this earthwork, and through the low bank leading from it towards the observatory, revealed no dating evidence, although the latter appeared almost certainly to be later than the inner vallum of the original camp. Roman coins (SMR 3224) and flints (SMR 3225) have also been found within and around the hillfort.
The hillfort was described by William Worcestre in 1480 (SMR 20588; Neale 2000, 34-35), by John Aubrey in the later seventeenth century (SMR 20659; Fowles 1980, 374-375) and a number of local antiquarians (eg. Seyer 1821, 60-61). Part of the outer earthworks of the camp are reported to have been levelled by Sir William Draper in the mid to late eighteenth century. There has been significant erosion by limestone quarrying on the west side and the construction of Suspension Bridge Road to the south also cut into the hillfort.
A windmill was built inside the hillfort in 1776 but was destroyed by fire soon after, and was rebuilt as the Observatory (SMR 1031) in 1828-29. A reservoir is also known to have been constructed inside the hillfort in the nineteenth-century. The hillfort is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (AV29).
