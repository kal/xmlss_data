monument_desc Colston's House. A large late medieval house complex, on the east side of Small Street. Including a hall, and a chapel or second hall. Main ranges running parallel to the east side of street. Incorporated a late Norman arcade, destroyed in 1961. At present the layout of the complex is not fully understood.
Front of 5 bays, all gabled to street, 2 storeys and attics. Left bay with window and right-hand entrance; 2nd.bay with left semicircular-headed entrance and right window; middle bay with central entrance, 2 leaf door, 12-pane shop windows to either side, Babers sale room in 1826; 4th.bay a square-headed haulingway entrance, leading to Withers's cooperage in 1826; right bay with left-hand entrance and segmental-arched window. At first-floors and attics, the left-hand 4 bays are all consistent. One 8-light transomed window to each first-floor; one 4-light window or opening to each attic. Right-hand bay has a 4-light first-floor window, at lower level than those adjacent, and a sash at attic level.
Rear elevation to courtyard of 2 storeys and 4 bays, Perpendicular, late C15 or early C16. Left-hand bay with square-headed entrance to haulingway to street, sash window above, cutting the string-course. Other 3 bays each of 8-light windows to ground-floor, separated by thin columns reaching full height of both storeys. Single tier of trefoil-headed panels, 4 per bay, with string course above at upper sill level. 8-light windows to first-floors, each light with trefoil head (1039A). Parapet for full 4-bay width, 2 panels to each bay.
Hall of 5 bays, exact location uncertain (1037A). Braikenridge notes (128) that most probably this was Colston's great hall, in 1821 a warehouse attached to a grocer's shop kept by Messrs.Pinnell & Co. (listed at No.21 Small Street in the directories).
Chapel or hall of at least 4 bays (1038A). Hammerbeam roof (Find Record No.903); floor possibly inserted. In use as the Mirror Printing Office in 1821. The roof is preserved in the collection of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery.
In the first-floor room lit by the windows in the rear elevation, on the opposite wall, was a 5-sided full-height chimney-piece (1040A). The room was provided with a moulded and decorated plaster ceiling.
Elaborate Classical-style chimney-piece (BUAD 1041A) in a room described as the 'Drawing Room' where Joanna Southcott's congregation assembled "for some time".
