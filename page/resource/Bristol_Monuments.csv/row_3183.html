monument_desc Clifton National School, located on the east side of Church Lane, Clifton. The school was constructed in 1835 and is recorded by the tithe survey of Clifton made in 1844 (BRO EP/A/32/12). It was a large two-storey structure with a hipped roof and occupied a footprint measuring 29 metres east-west by 13 metres north to south on an apparently specially constructed terrace. The main, south-facing, elevation was of nine bays and had tall, narrow windows to both floors, those of the first-floor having segmental-headed arches. The side east-facing, elevation had three bays. Plans in Bristol Record Office (BRO 229389(6 & 7)) indicate that the ground-floor was largely occupied by a single teaching space. At the western end of the building were two smaller rooms and a lodge projected from the building at ground floor. The stairs to the first-floor were at the south-eastern corner of the building. The first-floor was largely occupied by the girls school with a smaller teaching area divided off by partitions at the head of the stair.
By 1883 an extension had been added to the eastern end of the building. This was intended as an Infants School and the Girls school was later also moved into the building. It measured 20 metres from east to west by 10 metres north-south and was a single-storey structure with a pitched roof and basement. The side, south-facing, elevation was of five bays with tall windows with segmental-headed arches and ashlar dressings. Immediately beneath the windows there was a projecting moulded freestone ashlar string course, with a second around 0.5 metres below this. The parapets at the east and west ends of the roof also had ashlar dressings. A single-storey block linking the two school buildings contained the latrines.
The school building survived into the twentieth-century and were damaged by bombing in the early 1940s. The structure remained in ruins throughout the 1950s and the site was subsequently redeveloped. However, the gymnasium, apparently dating to the first two decades of the twentieth century, survives as do elements of the main school building in the succeeding structure (Parry, 2001).
