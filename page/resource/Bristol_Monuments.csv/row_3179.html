monument_desc No.45 High Street, located on the western side of High Street. A tenement belonging to the Guild of Kalendars stood on the site by 1470 and at that date it was occupied by John Lemster, cook, and was part of the group of buildings known as "Cokyn Rewe". The building was leased to Walter Phylyp, mercer, in 1520. Phylyp bought the property from Sir Miles Partridge in 1542 after the Dissolution of the Chantries. Dr. White, Phylyp's grandson, made the tenement part of the endowment of his almshouse in Temple Street (BUAD 283M) and it was subsequently leased to a number of individuals. The building was recorded as new built in 1700 and was known as the "Hen and Chickens," the "Seven Stars" by 1760, "The Globe" in 1775 and "The Wheatsheaf" in 1778 (Leech, 1997 82).
The building was surveyed by Dr. Roger Leech in 2000 (Leech, 2001) and much of the fabric of the building was of late-medieval in date, including the shell walls, the timber beams supporting the floors at ground-, first- and second- floor. The medieval features of the cellar are particularly well-preserved. The original form of the building appears to have been a one-unit house with a gallery linking it to a detached kitchen at the rear. At a cellar level the area between the front and rear walls was probably an open lightwell. The building was refronted in 1907.
The building has a Grade II listing (Listed Building number 901-1/11/586).
