monument_desc Colston's Fort or Colston's Mount, located on the north-west side of the road now known as Montague Place. The fort was constructed in 1642 as part of the new northern line of defences around Bristol to protect the city during the English Civil Wars. It stood between Royal Fort (BUAD 928M), to the south-west, and Priors Hill Fort (BUAD 121M), to the north-east.
It was probably built of stone and was "some 18 foote square" according to the Royalist engineer Sir Bernard de Gomme (Firth 1925, 184). A survey of the ordnance captured at the storming of Bristol made on 28 July 1643 records that there was one minion and one falcon at the fort (Roy 1975, 261). Although the fort was involved in the fighting during both major assaults on the Bristol, attacks were concentrated elsewhere on the line, particularly towards Prior's Hill Fort (BUAD 121M).
Sprigge reports that following the Parliamentarian capture of the city Cromwell ordered that the forts along the line be left "in a safe condition", presumably by partial demolition (Sprigge 1854, 132). Jean Rocque's 1742 'A Plan of the City of Bristol' records the remains of the fort, a D-shaped mound on the north-west side of the road, suggesting that the south-eastern part of the fort had been cut away by the road. The remains of the fort were also noted to be extant in 1751 (BRO P/StJ/ChW/6). The antiquary Samuel Seyer observed the remains of the fort in the early-nineteenth century, noting that much had been cut away by the road, while the remainder survived in a garden (Seyer 1823, 305). The site of the fort was subsequently developed and no elements survive above ground. An archaeological watching brief during the redevelopment of the former Montague Brewery immediately to the south recorded no evidence for the fort (BUAD 3644).
