monument_desc The Three Kings Inn, located on the east side of St. Thomas Street. The date of construction of the inn is unclear. John Pritchard believed that it had been constructed in the fifteenth century and it was licensed in 1606. The main part of the inn was arranged along the south side of a large yard, known as Three Kings Yard, with the primary building standing on the St. Thomas Street frontage, and overailing the entrance to the yard of St. Thomas Street. This building was of two storeys and attic and was timber-framed. The ground floor of the main, west-facing, elevation was occupied by a shopfront. The interior had been much altered in the later nineteenth century, removing a good deal of its character. The inn was the headquarters of many country carriers serving towns such as Devizes, Warminster, Salisbury, Southampton and Portsmouth.
The building was in use as a public house in the 1880s and in 1882 was run by Malcolm Knee. It was acquired in 1903 by the Midland Railway Company with the intention of constructing a modern hotel, but the scheme was abandoned and the building remained empty for several years. It was demolished in December 1911 and John Pritchard observed the demolition (BUAD 679), noting that much of the original timberwork was exposed (Pritchard 1904, 337).
