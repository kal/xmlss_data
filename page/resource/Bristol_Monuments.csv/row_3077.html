monument_desc The White Lodge, located some 17 metres to the south-east of Upper Maudlin Street. The building was constructed c.1588 as one of two lodges to the gardens of the Sir John Yonge's Great House (BUAD 420M) in St. Augustine's Back which extended from Trenchard Street across Stony Hill. The building was of three-storeys with a cross-gabled roof and a turret on the west side. In plan it measured 8 metres from south-west to north-east and was 6.8 metres wide. The south-east facing elevation had a two-window range with two gables. At ground floor the fenestration consisted of a four-light window. There was a three-light window at first-floor level and a two-light window in the attics. All of the windows had stone hood moulds above. The south-west facing elevation had a single gable, small windows lighting the attic and a single-flue chimneystack at the apex of the gable. The turret at the north--west corner of the building was octagonal with crenellations around the top and contained a spiral stair. There was another chimneystack at the centre of the roof. The main entrance to the building appears to have been at the western end of the north-west facing elevation. This led to into a lobby which gave on to the stair in the turret and the three ground-floor rooms, two on the south side of the building and the third at the north-east corner. In the corner of one of the rooms there was a stone chimney-piece bearing the date 1588. Above was a pair of compasses surmounted by a dove carrying an olive branch in its beak (Braikenridge Notes A32).
The antiquarian G. W. Braikenridge records that in the 1820s the building was occupied by a Mr. Willcocks, who had lived there for twenty years. The chimneypiece was extant in June 1823 although the tower had been subject to "much" alteration in the period of Willcock's residence (Braikenridge Notes A32). The building was still standing in the later 1860s (Winstone 1971, pl.27) but had been demolished by the early 1880s. No record appears to have been made of the building before its demolition although there are watercolour drawings of the 1820s by T. L. Rowbotham (BRSMG M2562) and Samuel Jackson (BRSMG M2577). It is also visible in some early photographs of this part of Bristol.
