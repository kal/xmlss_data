monument_desc The Holmes, located on the eastern side of Stoke Park Road. The site lay within the Manor of Stoke Bishop and the estate remained in the hands of the descendants of the Cann family until 1800. In 1869 the estate was sold at auction in lots. The parcel on which The Holmes was built was Lot 12. The bulk of the estate was purchased by the Birmingham Financial Company and resold as smaller lots. The Holmes was bought by Herbert Meade-King in 1870 and sold to William Edwards George in July 1876. It is not clear whether Meade-King or George built The Holmes but the house was complete by 1879 and was leased out. The building is a two-storey structure constructed of rubble stone (apparently an Old Red sandstone) with ashlar detailing.
From 1884 the lessee was W. Mills Baker, the father of the plant collector Hiatt C. Baker. Hiatt Baker is likely to have been responsible for the design of the formal garden created around the house which comprised a series of sinuous walks, a rockery and an area known as The Dell (Hicks 2004, 13). The Baker family let the property to Alfred Capper Pass, owner of a smelting works in Bedminster and later Professor emeritus of Chemistry at the University of Bristol, but retained ownership of the building until 1943 when it was acquired by the University of Bristol. The University extended the building by constructing east and west wings.
In 1944 The Holmes was requisitioned as the headquarters of General Omar N. Bradley, commander of the United States First Army, and his subordinate commanders (Generals Jacob Devers (1887-1979) of the 6th Army Group, Courtney Hodges (1887-1966) of the U.S. First Army, and George S. Patton Jr. (1885-1945) of U.S. Third Army). It served as the base for the planning of Operation OVERLORD, the invasion of Normandy, in June of that year. A note from Dwight D. Eisenhower (1890-1969), Supreme Allied Commander of the Allied Expeditionary Force (SHAEF) from 1944, and other memorabilia in the hall of the building commemorate the US Army's use of the building.
For many years the building was in use as a hall of residence.
