monument_desc The Cardiff Boat Public House, located on Welsh Back. The building occupied the south-east corner of the St. Nicholas Parish extension burial ground in Crow Lane [BUAD 43M]. A dwelling stood on the site from 1422 when a rent for it is known to have been paid to the merchant Richard Trenode. By 1579 it was occupied by Welthian Betts, a widow, when it was on the north side of a lane leading to Baste Street [modern Queen Charlotte Street] (Leech, 1997 168-169). The building is recorded by early-nineteenth century watercolours in the Braikenridge Collection of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery, notably that by T. L. S. Rowbotham made in 1825 (BRSMG M2238) which shows the east-facing elevation on Welsh Back and part of the north-facing elevation, and another by Hugh O'Neill of 1823 (BRSMG M2279) also showing the north-facing and the west-facing elevation.
These show a gabled two-bay house of two storeys plus attic with a pantile roof. It was of sixteenth- or seventeenth-century date. Its footprint measured 6.25 metres north-south and 13.3 metres from east to west. The main, east-facing, elevation fronted on to Welsh Back and the ground floor was a shopfront with the main doorway at the north end of the elevation. The fenestration to the south of the doorway consisted of a pair of three-by-four light with a stall riser beneath. There were signboards above both the door and the windows. The first and attic storeys were divided by a moulded string course and the fenestration of both storeys consisted of cross-windows. There was a small window near the apex of the gable. A tall chimney stack projected around a further storey above the eaves from the south wall of the building. At the centre of the north elevation there was a single window at ground, and at first-floor one at the centre of the elevation and one at the western end, the latter beneath a gablet. There were small two-light mullioned windows at the centre of the rear, west-facing, elevation and, immediately to the north, a projecting single-flue chimney stack. In the north side of the roof there was a skylight.
In 1850 the building was occupied by Frederick Willis when it was known as The Cardiff Boat (BRO Plan Book C fo.98a). It appears to have survived until the end of the nineteenth century and was recorded by the Ordnance Survey first edition 1:500 scale plan (Bristol Sheet LXXV.4.5) surveyed in 1882. The Goad Fire Insurance plan of January 1887 indicates that by then the building formed part of the Tanner Brothers stationery factory (along with the Old Custom House [BUAD 41M] to the south). It was presumably demolished at the same time as the redevelopment of St. Nicholas Burial Ground after 1902.
The site of the building now lies beneath the eastern side of Telephone House.
