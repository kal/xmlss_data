monument_desc No.10 Lower Park Row. Early C17, altered c1722, datestone over doorways, altered early C19, restored c1980. Rubble, limestone dressings and ashlar gable stacks, and pantile cross-gabled roof. Double-depth plan with gable fronting the street.
3 storeys, attic and basement; 1-window range. Gabled front (facing west) with 2 cross gables each side, strings to upper floors. Right-hand brick segmental-arched blocked doorway, central C19 shop window, and right-hand C20 sash. 8/8-pane first-floor sash under a relieving arch, and C20 attic casement. Left return has stacks to both gables, a central C20 casement and second-floor C16 mullion window, with good small semicircular-arched leaded windows in ashlar panels to extreme sides of upper floors. Right return a 5-window range, blind to the left of paired central semicircular-arched early C19 doorways with imposts, and restored cross windows with leaded metal casements.
The interior included a rebuilt framed dogleg stair from the entrance with moulded C17 door frames and panelled doors on each landing. There were formerly a number of mid C17 features particularly 6 excellent panelled doors and a number of fine Tudor-arched fireplaces, one in the basement with a bread oven, also moulded cornices, panelling and some small freestone arched windows. The Schedule of Listed Buildings notes it as an exceptional survival.
Possibly mid-17th.century. Once known as the White Lodge (but not the 'White Lodge' that was sister to the Red Lodge). Some of the 17th.century doors were stolen before renovation of the building. Three of the original six chimney pieces survived. A well was found during renovation work. Remains of painted plasterwork were also found in the front ground-floor fireplace at that time.
