monument_desc Possible bet tohorah (for washing the dead before burial) or mikveh (Jewish ritual bath or washing place), located on the north side of Constitution Hill, at its junction with Jacob's Well Road. The monument was discovered in 1987 (BUAD 282) and appears to comprise at least two chambers cut into the hillside which collect water from springs on the hillside. A large stone lintel above an opening into the northern of the chambers appears to carry part of an inscription in Hebrew. The interpretation of the inscription is currently uncertain but Emanuel and Ponsford (1994) suggest that is "zochalim" ("flowing"). After its re-examination Blair et al (2001-02; Hillaby & Sermon 2004) suggest that "mayim tehorim" ("pure waters") or "mayim chayim" ("living waters") are possible alternative readings.
The monument was initially interpreted as a mikveh but Blair et al. note that the "mayim chayim" reading of the inscription "refers to the type of water required to cleanse a person after touching a corpse" (Blair et al. 2001-02, 18-20). The monument is also close to the Jewish cemetery known as Jew's Acre which was located on the site of the Queen Elizabeth's Hospital building and it was therefore argued that the monument was more likely to be a bet tohorah or cleansing house where the ceremony of washing the dead was carried out before burial.
The monument went out of use with the expulsion of the Jews from England in 1290 and it was then incorporated into the conduit (BUAD 934M) supplying water to St. Augustine's Abbey and St. Mark's Hospital.
Medieval mikva'ot are rare in Europe and the only other examples known from the British Isles, are those found in London in 1986 at Guildhall House, Gresham Street and in 2001 at Nos.1-6 Milk Street (Blair et al., 2001; Hillaby & Sermon 2004). If the interpretation as a bet tohorah is correct this is the only surviving example of this class of monument currently known in the United Kingdom.
The bet tohorah is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM number AV 28881).
