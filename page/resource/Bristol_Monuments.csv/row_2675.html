monument_desc No.1 Christmas Street, later known as the 'Ship' public house. The building stood on the south-west corner of the junction of Christmas Street and Quay Street. The house was apparently of late-medieval date, although its original form as built is unclear. It may have had a first-floor hall and this was beneath an arch-brace collar-beam roof with chamfered windbraces which was of late fourteenth- or early fifteenth-century date. Pritchard thought that the room may originally have measured 24 feet [7.3 metres] long by 12 feet [3.7 metres] wide. He records that the "original half-timber work at the east end clearly showed the pitch of the roof, the position of the three windows, and the general character of the building" (Pritchard 1911, 70-72). The building may originally have presented a gabled front to Christmas Street.
The medieval roof was hidden by a plaster ceiling in the post-medieval period. The construction of an adjacent warehouse in around 1878 resulted the size of the first-floor room to 17 feet [5.2 metres] long by 16 feet [7.3 metres] wide in the removal of all but the rear two bays of the late-medieval roof.
The building was demolished on 21st November 1910 to make way for an extension to Fry's factory.
The majority of the evidence for the construction of the building was recorded by John Pritchard at the time of its demolition (BUAD 3207). One photograph of the roof seen from Christmas Street was published in Pritchard's report on the building (Pritchard 1911, 70-72).
