monument_desc No.56 Corn Street. The building was constructed in 1782 to a design by Thomas Paty. It is in a Classical style and is a three-storey structure of limestone ashlar with, rusticated quoins, cornice, parapet and a hipped roof. The upper floors project over the north aisle of All Saints Church (BUAD 247M).
The main, north-west facing, elevation to Corn Street has a three window range with the pedimented centre set forward. At ground-floor there is an arcade of keyed semicircular arches. There are pediments over the first floor window openings which contain six-over-six pane timber sash windows. The south-west facing elevation to All Saints Lane matches the style of the main elevation and has a doorway at its northern end and a blind arch at the southern end.
Internally the building has a single-depth plan with the upper floors projecting south over the north aisle of All Saint's Church. Eighteenth-century fixtures remain, including the stair above first floor and other joinery in the basement and window fittings on the upper floors. The building had previously been let to Sarah Sawyer and subsequently to John Avery, a distiller and by 1832 the building had "lately" been used as a Stamp Office. It was then leased to Frederick Sway (Leech 1997, 67). Norwich Union were still in occupation in 1883, apparently with an architect, W. H. Price, and a solicitor operating from the upper floors.
The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/11/573).
