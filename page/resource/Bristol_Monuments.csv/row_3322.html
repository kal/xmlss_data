monument_desc St. Anne's Chapel, also known as St. Anne's in the Wood (located at approximately ST62097278). The chapel was founded between 1170 and 1392, when a legacy was bequeathed to the chapel (Richardson 1898, 190). William Worcestre states that the chapel was founded by "Quidam Dominus de le Warr", presumably the same family responsible for the foundation of St. Bartholomew's Hospital in Bristol. He gives measurements for the chapel and lists the plate (Neale 2000, 56-59). St. Anne's Well (SMR 1400), which W. J. Pountney suggests was a reservoir to collect water from a spring rather than a well, also lay to the north.
At the time of the Dissolution, St. Anne's was held by the Abbey of Keynsham. In a deed dated 30 June 1538, the Abbot of Keynsham, granted the Chapel and ground of St. Anne's on a lease to a yeoman of "Bristelten" named Robert Stafford. The chapel appears to have been at least partially demolished not long after the dissolution. It had been dismantled by 1592, when it was sold by Robert Bridges.
Between 1867 and 1873 the tenant farmer at St. Anne's Farm discovered human remains near the site of the chapel. Archaeological excavation of Brislington Pottery (SMR 20221) by Pountney in 1914 (SMR 20221) revealed the foundations of the chapel and two vaults, in one of which were remains of window masonry, the rood screen and the shrine of St. Anne (Pountney 1914). Other finds included a silver coin, possibly of Edward III and English and foreign Abbey tokens. The finds were presented to Bristol Museum and Art Gallery.
Today metal studs mark the position of the chapel on the ground, and a plaque on the wall of the modern Board Mill building records the site.
