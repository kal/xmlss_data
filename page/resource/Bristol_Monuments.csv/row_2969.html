monument_desc The Great House, or Colston's Great House located on the west side of St. Augustine's Place (modern Colston Street). The building was constructed c.1568, possibly reusing elements of the Carmelite friary (BUAD 609M). The house was a large mansion with a courtyard enclosed by two wings running west-north-west away from St. Augustine's Place. The entire complex was of two storeys plus attic, with a street-level cellar on the eastern (St. Augustine's Place) side. The building presented an elevation of five gabled bays with alternate canted bays to St. Augustine's Place, and at least three gabled bays on the northern and southern sides. James Millerd's 1673 vignette show that the wings were one bay wide, with two gables on their inner sides, and the rear of the street range also possessed two gables. All of main windows were of eight lights.
The arrangment of the interior has not been researched although seventeenth-century inventories record a great hall, "little studdie" and outhouses, a coach house and stables as well as a water cistern to control supply to the main house (Hall 1949, 122-123).
The initial plan included a garden, apparently walled and raised above the level of the courtyard, on the north side of the house. Its northern boundary was Trenchard Street. Later Sir John Yonge began to acquire plots on the hillside on the opposite side of Trenchard Street to create a larger garden for the house (Roger Leech pers. comm.). The White Lodge and the Red Lodge marked the edges of this garden.
The house provided the accommodation for Queen Elizabeth during her visit to Bristol in 1574. The building became a sugar house in 1653. Water for its three pans was drawn from St. John's Conduit (apparently without permission and causing a reduction in supply elsewhere) and cottages were built in the grounds of the house to provide dwellings for the refinery workers. Inventories record not only the utensils required for sugar making but also the equipment, the vats and coolers, installed in the various rooms (Hall, 1949 141). Refining continued on the site until 1708 when a school was founded in the building by Edward Colston. By 1742 it not only occupied the main house, but also a smaller building adjacent on Host Street to the east, and others fronting Trenchard Street. The building was occupied by the school into the early nineteenth century but later became an assembly rooms and art gallery. It was demolished in 1863 in order to enable construction of the Colston Hall (BUAD 69M).
