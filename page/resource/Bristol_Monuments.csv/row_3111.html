monument_desc The Hospital of St. John the Baptist, sited on the western side of Redcliff Pit. The hospital was founded in the later-twelfth century and is known to have been in existence by 1186. There is some uncertainty over the precise location and extent of the hospital. Dr. Roger Leech argues, from post-medieval documentary and cartographic evidence, that the hospital precinct was defined by Jones Lane on the south and west sides and Redcliff Pit on the east. The land parcels of Nos.71-74 represent a post-medieval subdivision of the precinct (Leech pers comm.). Jones Lane itself may have originated as a slipway to the river for the hospital.
The community appears to have contained both sexes. Some of the early documents relating to the hospital refer to "brethren and sisters" and in 1317 Bishop Drokensford of Bath and Wells asked the master to accept the niece of Edmund de Wyntereshall as a secular sister (Scott Holmes, T. Religious Houses in Page 1969, 160).
The form of the hospital is uncertain but William Worcestre indicates that it had a church and cloister. His measurements suggest that both were of a relatively modest size (Neale 2000, 248-249). The water supply of the hospital was delivered via a feather from the Redcliffe pipe which ran across Redcliff Hill (Hobhouse 1887, 145) to discharge into a square pool or cistern at the centre of the cloister (Neale 2000, 248-249). A hermitage (BUAD 627M) was established in Redrock Garden to the west of the church in the fourteenth century. The hospital also took over responsibility for the Chapel of the Holy Spirit (BUAD 925M) in the churchyard of St. Mary Redcliff in the thirteenth century. In addition it owned the advowson of the rectory of Butcome and the vicarage of Backwell (Gairdner & Brodie 1903, 286).
The hospital was at the centre of some controversy at the beginning of the fifteenth-century because of allegations of Lollardy in the Hospital and mismanagement by the then prior, John Seyntpoull. A commission was appointed in 1404 to investigate the charges (Hudson 1988, 122n) and in 1411 the goods and estates of the hospital were sequestered by the Crown (Scott Holmes 1914, lx-lxi; 80; 83-84). By 1442 there was apparently only one brother in residence (Scott Holmes, T. Religious Houses in Page 1969, 160).
The hospital was surrendered to the Crown by the master, Richard Bromefield, in early March 1544 and the site, and much of the associated property, was sold in two lots to Dr. George Owen, physician to Henry VIII and a Fellow of Merton College, Oxford, in April of that year on condition that he funded the salary and accommodation of a resident priest to preach in the city on eight occasions each year (Gairdner & Brodie 1903, 93; 286). The buildings were apparently demolished soon after Owen's acquisition of the site (Latimer 1901, 176-177).
