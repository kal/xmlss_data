monument_desc St. Mark's Hospital, otherwise known as Gaunt's Hospital, located on the east side of College Green. The hospital was founded by Maurice de Gaunt sometime before his death in 1230, with a brief to feed one hundred poor people daily. The hospital was initially administered by St. Augustine's Abbey, but was made a separate foundation by Robert de Gurnay, Maurice de Gaunt's nephew and heir, in 1232 (Ross 1959, 5). The hospital was Benedictine and the charter of 1232 specified that the community comprised the master, four chaplains and eight clerks.
The precinct extended between College Green along the line of modern Unity Street, to Pipe Lane where the corner of the boundary was marked by two plaques bearing winged lions and a small niche (presumably for a figure of the Virgin or other statue) on the exterior walls of No.9 Pipe Lane. The hospital complex consisted of a church (BUAD 93M) with a cloister on its north side. The hospital was involved in a series of disputes with St. Augustine's Abbey from the early-thirteenth century. These involved use of College Green on which the hospital had grazed its animals and buried some of its dead (Ross 1959, 7-10). In 1491 the Abbey of St. Augustine claimed that the hospital fell within its manorial jurisdiction, beginning another long dispute in which the Bristol authorities became involved.
In 1534 the hospital community had five members. Four brothers and the master, John Colman, were still present at the time of the hospital's dissolution on 9th December 1539. The church plate was sent to London in February 1540 and the Hospital and its lands were acquired by the Bristol Corporation in May 1541.
