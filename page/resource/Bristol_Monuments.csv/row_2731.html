monument_desc St. James Church House. Dated 1666 on a front plaque, altered C19, when southern half of house removed for construction of enlarged north aisle to St.James Church. Rendered Pennant rubble with limestone dressings, timber-framed right-hand wall, rubble lateral and axial stacks and slate cross gabled roof.
Single front and rear rooms divided by a stack with fireplaces each side, and right-hand C19 stair. 2 storeys, attic and basement; 2-window range. Originally 2 gables to front, with, between, a gabled 2-storey porch, open at lower level, a small first-floor room with 6-light transomed window. The right-hand ground-floor window was square-headed with drip-moulding, 3 trefoil-headed lights, with a cellar access forward and below of it. Above was an 8-light transomed window, also with drip-moulding. Late C17 keyed oculus in the top of each gable. 2 small shields over the present doorway inscribed ETA and 1666, originally in the porch. [Thomas and Ann Ellis, 1666].
Inside, a fine late C17 ground-floor full-width front room with full panelling to a cornice, a panelled overmantel to left-hand fire surround, a fine carved overmantel to the rear with a central cartouche and side sections set back with carved classical allegorical figures holding shields. A fine plaster ceiling in 2 sections with detailed panels, pomegranates and moulded bosses, separated by a panelled Jacobean-style beam with strapwork, and a frieze above the cornice with adorsal griffins; panelling flanking fireplace and overmantel in the rear ground-floor room, behind which a C17 chimney-piece, again ETA and 1666; the first-floor front room has a small Tudor-arched doorway, now to a cupboard moulded and stopped beam; diagonal-braced timber-framed wall to north side of St.James with re-used timbers.
A rebuild of part of the western claustral range of the Benedictine Priory of St. James. Incorporates the second and third (from the south) arches of the western cloister walk, infilled in the 18th/19th.centuries (967A). The infill of the northern arch encased the last remaining portion of the original tracery, in the Decorated style. The two arches are segmental pointed examples, probably from the 14th.century, and therefore presumably replacing earlier versions.
Some details were recorded by Rowbotham (BRSMG M2849). Also, the fireplace at the rear of the surviving ground-floor room (BRSMG M2850).
