monument_desc No.44, Mary-le-Port Street. A house of seventeenth-century date located on the southern side of Mary-le-Port Street. In the principal room on the first-floor were some fragments of a plaster ceiling (sketched by Samuel Loxton) which was, according to Pritchard "The semi-figure of an angel within a scroll was used at one of the four corners of the principal ceiling and fleur de lys mouldings were freely introduced into the design of another ceiling on the same level" (Pritchard 1922, 88). There was an ornately carved stone chimney-piece (BUAD 1002A) reported to be of Ham Hill stone. It measured approximately 8 feet by 6 feet and carried the arms of the Cordwainers Company of London (Pritchard 1922, 90-92).
John Pritchard visited the building before its demolition and recorded some features of the building (BUAD 674), describing the exterior as "plain and unattractive" (Pritchard 1922, 88). Pritchard also noted that the ground-floor had been subject to much alteration, although a short element of the original staircase survived "on the upper floor".
The building was demolished, along with No.45 St. Mary-le-Port Street on it western side, between April and August 1920, to make way for the construction of new premises for Messrs. Stevens Bros. Ltd., Provision Merchants. The chimneypiece was recovered and sold by the contractor to a local dealer, then to London, and finally making its way to New York.
