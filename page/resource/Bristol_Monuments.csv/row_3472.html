monument_desc No.36 High Street, located on the western side of High Street. A building stood on the site from at least the mid-fifteenth century and in 1461 comprised two shops with a share of an inn. In 1473 the inn was named "le Cok in the Hope" and at this date it was leased to Clement Wiltshire, merchant, by John Shipward. In 1566 "inner" part (presumably the rear) of the inn was conveyed by Hugh Smyth to Walter Standfast and by 1607 this element had been acquired by John Pepwall. In 1648 the property was sold to Jeremy Holwey and by 1695 the inn had been renamed the Golden Cock (Leech, 1997 79).
In the late 1930s the building occupied a 3.7 metre wide frontage on High Street and the main, north-west facing, elevation was of four-storeys and two bays with a gabled roof. The ground floor was occupied by a shop front with large plate-glass windows. The fenestration at first and second floor levels comprised evenly spaced sash windows The second and third storeys were jettied out from the lower storeys and from one another. The windows of the fourth storey, however, formed a continuous band across the frontages of Nos.35 and 36, beginning two-thirds of the way along the frontage of No.35 and running to the north side of the frontage of No.36. The ends of the two parallel gables had plain bargeboards and there were now windows in the attic gables. A double chimney stack projected from the roof on the south side of the building.
The building survived into the twentieth century but was totally destroyed by bombing during the raid on 24 November 1940. The site was subsequently redeveloped and is now beneath No.35-38 High Street.
