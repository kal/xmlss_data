monument_desc St. Raphael's Church, located on the north side of Cumberland Road. The church was built at the expense of Rev. Robert H. Miles as a chapel to the adjacent almshouses for retired sailors and their widows [BUAD 524M]. Miles commissioned the architect Henry Woodyer, an Eton contemporary of his, to design the building (Elliott & Pritchard, 2002 78; 150). The church had thirteen-bays with buttressed aisles on the north and south sides and was constructed of coursed Pennant sandstone rubble with ashlar dressings. Buttresses projected from either side of the west front, which contained a large tracery window. The east window was also of tracery and contained stained glass by John Hardman & Co. of Birmingham. The roof was pitched and there was an elaborate timber fleche containing four bells slightly to the east of the centre of the ridge of the roof, marking the division between the chancel and the nave. The chapel was 100 feet from east to west and 51 feet wide and could accommodate 350 people (BRO P/StR/1/2; Elliott & Pritchard, 2002 150).
Internally there was a reredos of Derbyshire alabaster with a cross in the centre panel and an oak rood screen with carved trefoil-headed tracery. The pulpit was also of Derbyshire alabaster (Elliott & Pritchard, 2002 150).
Construction cost £10000 (Ralph & Cobb, 1991 30).
The church opened in 1859. It developed a reputation for ritualism and the Bishop of Bristol revoked the chaplain's licence to preach in 1879. It was re-consecrated and re-opened in 1893 (Ralph & Cobb, 1991).
The church was damaged by bombing in 1940 and was declared redundant in 1946. It was converted to use as a granary during the late 1940s and early 1950s. The church was demolished in 1954 and its site now lies beneath the western half of the housing development known as The Quays.
