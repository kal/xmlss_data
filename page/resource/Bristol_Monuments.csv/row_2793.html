monument_desc Bristol Castle. The castle replaced a motte and bailey castle (BUAD 940M) which was constructed after the Norman conquest in 1066. The motte and bailey was removed and the castle largely reconstructed in the early-twelfth century. The bailey ditch appears to have been realigned on the western side of the site and a barbican gate created. The castle was enclosed by a tall curtain wall constructed of Pennant sandstone with ashlar detailing to the openings. Internally the castle was divided into two wards, the outer ward on the western side in which the keep, the Constable's quarters and the Chapel of St. Martin were located, while the Great Hall, another chapel and the kitchens stood in the eastern, inner, ward (Neale 2000, 240-241). To the south, in the area between Queen Street and the River Avon was the Kings Orchard.
During the Anarchy, the fighting for the Crown which followed the death of Henry I in 1135, Bristol castle was the centre of power of the Angevin party supporting Henry's daughter Matilda (Fleming 2004, 4-5). In the mid-twelfth century Henry II took control of the castle and made it a Royal fortress. The maintenance of the Castle seems to have been a drain on Royal resources and the accounts of the Constables of the Castle record numerous repairs in the thirteenth and fourteenth century. Particularly in the later middle ages, a level of disrepair seems to have been tolerated.
The castle was sold to the Bristol Corporation by the Crown in the 1630s. It was reinforced during the First Civil War (1642-1646) and acted as the headquarters for the parliamentarian governor of Bristol, Nathaniel Fiennes. A total of 33 cannon were sited at different locations in the Castle at the time of its capture in July 1643 (Roy 1975, 262-263).
The castle was demolished relatively rapidly in the late 1650s to make way for the development of housing on the site, and the stone was apparently recovered for use in the construction of the houses. However, some elements seem to have escaped immediate removal. James Millerd's 1673 map of Bristol suggests that the west curtain wall was still standing at that date. Part of the south curtain wall also survived, as did the building known as the Vaulted Chambers - thought to be part of the entrance to the Great Hall.
Archaeological investigation of the site has been relatively limited but there have been two campaigns of excavation, one in the late 1940s and early 1950s and one in he late 1960s which was directed by M. W. Ponsford of the Department of Archaeology of the City of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery (Ponsford 1979).
The only elements now visible above ground are the Vaulted Chambers, a rock-cut sally port and a section of the south curtain wall.
