monument_desc Brandon Hill, reputed to be the oldest public park in Bristol being originally granted to the Bristol Corporation by Robert, Earl of Gloucester in 1174. Four acres at the summit of the hill were granted to the Abbey of Tewkesbury and a small chapel dedicated to St Brandon was built there so time prior to 1193 (1028M).
In 1581 the Corporation paid the Crown £30 for these remaining four acres at the summit of the hill.
Brandon Hill became known for the traditional rights for people to dry their clothes there and beat out their rugs (only between the hours of 6am and 9pm).
During the Civil War (1642-46) substantial defences were erected on the hill and where these earthworks and fortifications survive these are protected as Scheduled Monuments.
Following the continual encroachment of residential developments around the hill a subscription fund was started to pay for landscaping works in the park including walks and walls to enable the preservation of the landscape. Eight hundred pounds were raised for these works.
In 1857 some captured Russian guns were placed at the top of the hill before being replaced in 1897-8 by the erection of Cabot Tower, designed by W.V. Gough.
In 1936-7 further landscaping was undertaken to create water gardens with pools and cascades on the slopes below the tower.
In 1949-50 a heather garden was created at the base of the tower.
