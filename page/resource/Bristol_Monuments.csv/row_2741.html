monument_desc Civil War Defensive Wall to the north of Bristol. A line of defences around the north side of Bristol was constructed in 1642 to defend the city when it declared its support for Parliament. The form of the original line is certain but it probably comprised a stone wall with a ditch in front. The precise line of the defences is largely still to be established. However, it ran from a Water Fort (BUAD 1043M) close to the River Avon at the foot of Brandon Hill, north across Brandon Hill to a fort at the summit of the hill. The line then passed north-west to the Windmill Fort in the area of modern Tyndall Avenue. From there it ran to Colston's Fort (BUAD 943M) on the north-west side of modern Montague Place, then to Prior's Hill Fort (BUAD 121M) near Fremantle Square (with Back of Kingsdown Parade likely to follow the line of the ditch). The line then turned downhill to Stokes Croft which was barricaded by a wooden gate (still extant in the 1740s). On the east side of the gate there was a small bastion or hornwork. The line then ran south-east toward the river Frome, passing though the area of Portland Square to a cannon position on Newfoundland Road.
After Bristol was captured by Royalist forces in 1643 the line was subject to significant works to enhance its strength. The Windmill Fort was rebuilt and renamed The Royal Fort and there also appears to have been some movement of the line of the defences on Brandon Hill.
After the Civil War the line appears to have been removed relatively quickly. Some features were still extant in the mid-eighteenth century and were recorded by Jean Rocque's 1742 map of the city. The antiquarian Samuel Seyer also noted the remains of Colston Fort and earthworks on Brandon Hill in the 1820s.
The surviving fort and earthworks on Brandon Hill are a Scheduled Ancient Monument (AV 118).
