monument_desc Price's Pottery, No.39-46 St. Thomas Street, located on the east side of St. Thomas Street. The factory was in existence by the early 1820s. At that date the factory building was a three-storey structure, apparently constructed of brick.
The elevation of the building was recorded in a drawing by T. L. Rowbotham in 1826 (BRSMG M2092) and at that date the main, east-facing, elevation on St. Thomas Street had a three-window range. At ground floor level there was a large entrance with double doors on its south side and a pair of sashes to the north. At first-floor level there was a similar arrangement with a further large opening with double doors on the south side and two windows (possibly two-light casements) to the north. The second floor had three windows (possibly two-light casements). There was a sign on front of the building reading "CHARLES PRICE & SONS / STONE WARE & PATENT WATER PIPE / MANUFACTORY."
By 1887 there were six kilns, two against the northern boundary of the site, three just to the south of its centre and the last against the southern boundary. The factory continued to operate into the early twentieth century and part of the interior was photographed in 1925 (Winstone 1971, pl.164).
