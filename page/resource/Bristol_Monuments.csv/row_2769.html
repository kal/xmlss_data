monument_desc Headford House, located on the north side of Jacob Street, about 135 feet below David Street. Faced the old malthouse, and almost adjoined the back premises of the 'Stag and Hounds'. Had a frontage of 32 feet, with a long depth. Known as Headford or Headfort House, and described as such in a will of 1804. In September of that year it was sold, the lessee at that time being a Robert Headford. One document refers to "two lead soft-water pumps in the Brew-House". It had a gable on each of its four sides. The front windows were "somewhat small", and the ground-floor was six inches below the pavement. The large room on entering was almost devoid of interest, the only original work being the "moulded oak breast of the open fireplace - a timber 7 feet long by 10 inches in depth. The oak staircase at the back of this room possessed a good newel, and light from the rear of the property to the staircase came through an old leaded casement". The latter, with the moulded 'head-piece', was presented to the City Museum (995A). In the principal apartment on the first-floor was a carved fireplace or chimney-piece (994A), also later given to the Museum. On a shield in the centre were the letters
"C. / N. B." [i.e. denoting the initials N. C. and B. C.] with the date 1664 divided on the left and right cartouches. There was a "handsome" moulded ceiling in the same room, in the same manner as the chimney-piece. Above was another floor, but with no features of note.
