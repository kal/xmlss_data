monument_desc Blaise Castle House, Henbury Road. The building replaced an earlier, a house was constructed between 1796 and 1799 for John Scandrett Harford, a Bristol banker to a design by the local architect William Paty (1757-1800).

(Priest 2003, 117-121).

Limestone ashlar and render with a slate roof. Double-depth plan around a central hall. Neoclassical style. 2 storeys; 5-window range. 1:3:1 fenestration with a pedimented and slightly projecting centre.
A central semicircular Ionic portico on a low platform, banded square-cut rustication to the ground floor, first-floor plat band, modillion cornice and balustrade. An exedra behind the portico has 2 niches and a swag frieze with bucrania, a half-glazed door with a fanlight; six-over-six pane sashes with thick glazing bars, with moulded architraves below sunken panels on the first floor.

John Nash (1752-1835) added the Conservatory in 1804.
Extended 1831-1832 by Charles R. Cockerell (1788-1863) for John Harford junior. Garden front remodelled c1832

Interior, a complete Neoclassical decorative scheme by Charles Cockerell, with statuary collected by John Harford junior on his 1832 Italian tour.

The house is set in a landscape planned designed in c.1796 by Humphry Repton (1752-1818). Repton visited the site in August and October 1795. The Red Book is preserved in the House.

The Harford family left the house in 1926, selling the estate to Bristol Corporation.
The first visits by the public to the estate took place on 22 to 25 May 1926. The park was then closed until the purchase was finalised in June 1926. Blaise Castle House became a branch of the Bristol City Museum and Art Gallery in 1949.

The building has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/17/1370).
