monument_desc Newgate, located at the western end of Broad Weir. The town gate was constructed as part of the second circuit of the town wall (BUAD 1040M) in the thirteenth century and stood in Broad Weir, at its junction with Peter Street and Wine Street. It appears to have directly replaced the earlier Aldgate or Old Gate (BUAD 34M), which was 82 metres to the south-west along Wine Street.
The form of the gate in medieval period is currently unclear. William Worcestre, writing in the 1480s, records the gate as 9 yards long by 4 yards wide (Neale 2000, 250-251) and notes that there was a large cellar beneath it (Neale 2000, 12-13). It appears to have had a drum towers on at least the eastern sides (BRSMG M2718) and was likely to have been of two storeys, with a room above the gate. At first-floor on the north-east facing elevation there was a cross-window at the north-western side with a trefoil-headed window under a gable top towards the centre of the elevation. The jambs of this window comprised clasped shafts.
From c1426 until the middle of the century a grammar school was held in the room over the gate. The master was Robert Londe and the curriculum of the school, indicated by a manuscript of teaching materials copied "at Bristol, above the New Gate" by Thomas Short (Oxford, Lincoln College MS lat.129) who may have been a senior pupil, derived from Oxford (Orme 1976, 38-40). Londe died in February 1462 but it is not clear whether the school continued to be held after his death. However, it had disappeared by 1480 (Neale 2000, 28-29).
The gate was still extant seventeenth century and formed part of Bristol's defences in the civil war of 1642-1646 when a falcon, a cannon which fired a shot weighing 2 pounds, was sited on it (Roy 1975, 261). Subsequently the gate appears to have been incorporated into the structure of Newgate prison (BUAD 439M) and a second storey was added, possibly around the time of the construction of the prison. Elements of the two round towers on the north, or River Frome, side of the gate survived into the early nineteenth century.
George Weare Braikenridge notes that contractors began to demolish Newgate and the prison in January 1821 and Hugh O'Neill made several drawings during the demolition (BRSMG M2713-M2717).
