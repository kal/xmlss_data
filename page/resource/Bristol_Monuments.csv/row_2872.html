monument_desc The Black Hourse public house, facing on to the south side of College Square. The Dean and Chapter leased a plot at the south-east corner of the square to James Greene in 1611 (BRO DC/E/40/28/1) and the building was constructed on this plot in the seventeenth century. It was a single-bay jettied, gabled house of three storeys and the front (north-facing) and side (west-facing) elevations of the building were rendered. The ground floor contained a central entrance doorway and the jetty above was supported on four equally-spaced columns. The ground floor was divided from first floor by a narrow full-width pentice. The fenestration at first floor comprised a single five- by three-pane timber casement window. On the east side of the building there was a projecting element with a hipped pantile roof which extended only up to second floor. This was apparently square and was in front of the façade of the two-storey building attached to the east side. The function of this projecting element is unclear and it was divided from the rest of the façade by a timber pilaster, although it would appear to have been part of the original design of the building. The pediment dividing first- from second-floor did not extend across the full-width of the main elevation at this level, terminating at the eaves of the hipped element of the roof. The second floor fenestration was a wide five- by two-pane casement. There was no window in the attic gable. The roof was pantiled with a three-flue brick stack in the ridge of the roof at the rear of the building. On the west side of the building there was a gateway of coursed squared rubble stone, the main, north-facing,elevation was rendered with what appear to have been ashlar facings. This led to a path around the east and south sides of a walled garden belonging to the building to the west.
By 1825 the building had become the Black Horse public house. The building was demolished not long after 1890 along with the other structures on the south side of College Square. The site of the building now lies mainly beneath Anchor Road.
