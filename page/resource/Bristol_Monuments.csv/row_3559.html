monument_desc Mere Bank, Avonmouth. The monument is a straight linear earth bank with flanking ditches which ran north-west from Lawrence Weston towards the river Severn as least as far as Hoar Gout (at ST 52468017). The bank is approximately 2.6 metres wide and stands around 0.25 metres high. Mere Bank Rhine forms the ditch along the north-east side of the Mere Bank while that on its south-west side is a smaller field boundary ditch. In 1992 an archaeological field evaluation trench was excavated across the monument to establish its nature and date as part of the evaluation programme preceding development of the Second Severn Crossing (SMR 20130). The bank was was found to have been formed from a series of clay loam and silty clay layers. No palaeosol was observed beneath the bank but it sealed an earlier ditch and a layer of very silty clay which produced a sherd of Cotswold Tripod Pitcher ware dating to the twelfth- or early thirteenth-century.
The Mere Bank is likely to be part of a late-Saxon or medieval scheme of reclamation of the Henbury salt marsh, which formed part of the estates of the Bishops of Worcester from the eleventh century. It enclosed the open fields of Great Madam and Little Madam, common land until the early-nineteenth century.
A section of the Mere Bank approximately 800 metres long (between ST 5307179491 and ST 5359578915) is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM No. AV27988).
