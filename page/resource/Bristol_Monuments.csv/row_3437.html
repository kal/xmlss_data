monument_desc Bristol Bridge, re-constructed in 1768. In the eighteenth century the narrow highway of the medieval bridge was causing an impediment to the flow of the increasing volume of vehicular traffic through the city. In October 1758, as a reponse to public concern, the Corporation formed a committee to consider the options for improvements. Public disagreement over the various schemes prevented any final decision being reached. It was however resolved to construct a temporary wooden bridge alongside the medieval bridge which opened in early 1762 (Ison 1952, 115-117). In 1760 an Act of Parliament was obtained to allow works to be undertaken (Marcy 1968, 152).
The design ultimately used was produced by James Bridges. The bridge was built under the supervision of Thomas Paty between 1764 (the foundation stone being laid on 9 April) and 1768, reusing at least some of the piers of the medieval bridge (Barrett 1789, 77). The classical design is in limestone ashlar, the stone used being Courtfield stone from Monmouthshire (Ison 1952, 122) and comprises a central segmental arch flanked by two semi-circular arches, with rusticated voussoirs, soffits and pilaster strips. There were Portland stone balustrades, divided into three bays above each arch, with semi-circular iron lamp brackets above the apex of the central span. At either end of the bridge a pair of square, domed toll houses were built. The tolls imposed to cross the bridge were unpopular and ultimately provoked a serious riot in 1793 (Jones 1980; Manson 1997). When the practice of charging tolls was ended the four toll houses were converted to use as small shops.
The bridge was widened in 1873-74 by the addition of a cast-iron deck supported by paired cast-iron Doric columns on either side of the bridge. The toll houses were removed as part of this work. Steel pavement extensions and handrails were added in the 1930s.
Currently (01/02/2000) there is some damage to the ashlar, particularly the voussoirs and the Victorian elements of the bridge are badly corroded and unstable.
An archaeological analysis of the bridge was undertaken in early 2000 by Roger Leech. This identified the survival of ashlar foundations of three of the four eighteenth-century toll houses, as well as foundations of two shops and a blocked arch interpreted as the outlet for the necessary house associated with those shops (Leech 2000).
The bridge was a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM Number AV 158) but was descheduled in 2004. It has a Grade II listing (Listed Building Number 901-1/42/514).
