monument_desc Lawford's Gate House of Correction. A decision was taken by the Gloucestershire Commissioners to develop a new prison close to the site of the old bridewell in October 1783. The prison, on the south side of Lawford's Gate, was built in the late 1780s to a design by William Blackburn. Blackburn was replaced as architect during the course of construction by William Hobson.
The prisoners were transferred to the new building on 1 June 1791. In its finished form the house of correction was of two storeys and was constructed of rubble stone with a slate roof. A central, octagonal element contained the chapel at ground floor with the infirmary and dark (punishment) cells on either side at first-floor level. There twenty cells on each floor and these were arranged in two wings off the octagonal core. Each floor of each wing had a central corridor with five cells on either side of it. The cells all measured 7 feet 4 inches (2.24 metres) by 6 feet 7 inches (2.01 metres). Within the prison there was also a petty sessions room approximately 6 metres square and a two room keeper's house (each room of which was 3.6 metres square), plus a wash-house (Whiting 1975, 138-141). According to a late eighteenth-century report quoted by Whiting (1975, 141) there were on average between ten and twelve prisoners who were emploed cutting logs, making shoemakers pegs or making shoes. By 1832 the work given to the prisoners was picking oakum. The prisoners used the one yard and one day-room where they could fraternise and receive visitors.
In the nineteenth century an extension was added to the south-east side of the prison.
The building remained in use until 1860 but stood empty after that date until its demolition in 1907.
