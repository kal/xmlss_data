monument_desc The Merchants Arms, No.1 Prince Street, located on the corner of Prince Street and King Street. The building was of later seventeenth or early eighteenth-century date and was of four-storeys.
The main, north-facing, elevation in King Street was of six bays and was plainer. There was a jetty between first and second floor. The two western sashes at second floor level were missing. The west-facing, Prince Street, frontage was of five bays. There was a central doorway in the west frontage with a broken pediment above it. A large window occupied most of the north side of the ground floor while there was only a single window to the south of the doorway. The sashes at first and second floor were evenly spaced, and at the first floor the centre window was occupied by a plaque. The two gables at attic level each had a sash with drip moulds above. The roof was pantiled and had traceried barge boards. A string course divided the second and attic storeys.
By 1882 the building was a public house known as the Merchants Arms and the street directory of that year gives the licensee as Edward Price.
The inn was demolished to make way for the construction of the inner circuit road in 1936 the Prince Street roundabout (removed in 2006) was constructed over its site.
