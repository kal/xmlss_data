monument_desc The Preceptory of the Knights Templar, Temple Fee. The preceptory was founded c.1129 after a gift to the order by Robert, Earl of Gloucester of the eastern part of the marsh south of the Avon. The site appears initially to have been the centre for the administration of the Templar order in the south-west but later lost this function.
The extent of the preceptory is unknown but a ditch and rubble wall observed alongside Water Lane during laying of services in 1972 (BUAD 98) may indicate that the lane marks the north side and Temple Street the western. The southern and eastern boundaries are unknown.
Little of the internal layout of the precinct has so far been determined. A round-nave church, 18.5 metres in diameter (the same size as their church of St. Mary the Virgin in the City of London), was built and its site lies within Temple Church and excavation recorded evidence for a hall to the north of the church which, although not dated to the twelfth century, was believed by the excavator to be attributable to the Templar occupation of the site. This arrangement suggests a plan similar to that of preceptories elsewhere, the buildings being arranged informally around a central space.
The Templars also built dwellings, probably on Temple Street, which were rented out; by 1185 there were twenty-eight messuages producing, together with their other property in the area, an annual rental of just over £2 9s (Lees 1935, cxxi; 58). With the suppression of the order in 1312 the preceptory was given to the Knights Hospitaller.
