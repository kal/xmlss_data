monument_desc The Exchange, Corn Street. Consideration of the need for a new gathering place for the city's merchants to replace the existing Tolzey began in 1717. An Act of Parliament was obtained in early 1722 permitting construction of the Exchange. William Halfpenny (the professional name used by Michael Hoare) submitted several designs for the building but John Wood the Elder was ultimately selected to produce the design. The foundation stone was laid by the Lord Mayor in March 1741 and the building completed in 1743. The decorative carving was undertaken by Thomas Paty. The opening on 21st September 1743 was attended by the Mayor (Sir Abraham Elton) and Aldermen.
The Corn Street elevation is of limestone ashlar, with lateral stacks, a hipped slate and leaded roof. It has a central square courtyard with rooms ranged around it. The principal elevation is symmetrical with a pedimented 3-window centre set forward, a rusticated ground floor, pedestal course to four Corinthian columns to the centre and pilasters to the sides. The main entrance in the Corn Street facade is a semi-circular arched doorway with rusticated reveals and heavy arched double doors with studs and cast-iron lion-head knockers. The ground-floor windows have incised voussoirs, and the first-floor windows Corinthian pilaster jambs with entablature and pediments. They are alternately triangular and segmental, with a central Venetian window, and square second-floor windows. Below the frieze is a band of well-detailed swag and festoon to human and animal heads symbolising trade, and a Royal Coat of Arms in the tympanum. The majority of the original fabric is Pennant sandstone rubble.
Wood's design included a coffee house and a tavern at the north-east and north-west corners of the building, respectively. Along the east and west sides of the building there were individual houses, each of which consisted of a single room on each floor with a cellar, a kitchen at semi-basement level and other accommodation above, including a counting house. Two of the kitchens on the eastern side of the building retain many of their fittings as does one of the counting houses on the west side.
There have been significant campaigns of alteration to the building throughout its life. The floors of the cellars in the centre of the building were lowered c.1811-1813 when the Exchange became the Corn Exchange. Another major scheme was carried through in 1872 by the architect E. M. Barry. The central courtyard was roofed and a new suite of offices was created on the first floor, above the colonnades around the court. There were further changes after the Second World War. The clock tower over the south range was removed and the four floors of the coffee house and the main entrance were rebuilt internally. The current roof over the central courtyard dates to 1949.
Wood published a short description of the building and an account of its building (Wood, 1745) and a study of its building was made by Priest in 1980. The most complete examination, however, has been a full measured survey and interpretation made in 1998 by Dr. Roger Leech (BUAD 3374) which identified the extent of the surviving original fabric and phased the various alterations (Leech, 1998). The original inner gates to The Exchange are currently located in the Industrial Museum.
The building has a Grade I listing (Listed Building number 901-1/11/578).
