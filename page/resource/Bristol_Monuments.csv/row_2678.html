monument_desc Bristol Old Station, located on the north-western side of Temple Gate. A railway terminus built between 1839 and 1841, to a design by Isambard Kingdom Brunel, for the Great Western Railway Company. Limestone ashlar, squared Pennant Sandstone rubble with a hipped slate roof.
Axially-planned with offices across the end facing the road, the engine shed behind and the passenger shed beyond again (now linked with Temple Meads Station). In Tudor Revival style.
The offices (1010A) are three storeys; 7-window range. A symmetrical front missing the balancing right-hand Departures gateway. Tudor windows and a Tudor-arched doorway, with later shop fronts. To left, the 2-storey Arrivals gateway (1011A) with flanking turrets and a wide Tudor arch; a lower connecting block has a smaller gateway. The Pennant Sandstone rubble engine shed (1012A) behind has a tunnel crossing beneath it at the end. The passenger shed, or train shed (1013A), has a central projecting Arrivals entrance facing north.
The offices include a large open-well staircase. The sheds are built above a semicircular-vaulted undercroft: the engine shed has cast-iron stanchions; the aisled passenger shed has a cantilevered timber roof with false hammer beams above an arcade of Tudor arches on cast-iron columns.
The GWR line to London was completed in 1841, at which time the Bristol terminus opened. Arrivals and Departures were on opposite sides, passengers passing through the undercroft, which held the waiting rooms. The engines were turned on turntables and moved from line to line in the engine shed. The functional space of the station as designed by Brunel is almost entirely intact. The building is the earliest surviving large railway terminus in the world.
