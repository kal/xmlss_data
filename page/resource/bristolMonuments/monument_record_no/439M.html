monument_desc Newgate Gaol or Prison, located at the corner of Castle Mill Street and Narrow Wine Street, with frontages to both. Presumably partly built into the former gate. Attached to the west.
Hugh O'Neill's 1820 drawing (BRSMG Mb13) of the Castle Mill Street elevation shows an austere 3-storey building with at least 2 bays. Diagonal buttress left, to full height; central buttress, again full height; right-hand bay at an angle with a right-hand end buttress with battered base. In the left bay, a right-hand entrance with two-centred head arch, otherwise no openings. At ground-floor of the right bay, only a slit where donations for the prisoners (who were debtors) could be deposited. At first-floor, right, a cross-window, to its left a trefoil-headed window under a gable top, the window jambs comprising clasped shafts. Sash window to the second-floor. Also illustrated by him in 1821 (BRSMG M2711), from the south-east.
The elevation drawn also by O'Neill in 1820 (BRSMG M2708) depicts the Narrow Wine Street facade. On the corner, an L-shaped three-storey building, with an entrance in the eastern side of the south wing, a porch above, and a small first-floor window. On the south end of the same wing, a single ground-floor window; at first-floor, one window either side of a plaque above which a scrolled pediment; 2-light second-floor window. Hipped roof behind a parapet. Next to the west, a three-storey facade, in C18 military style, consisting of three elements, all with a common parapet, with hipped roof behind. A pilaster, then a bay with one window segmental-arched each to first and second floors. Next a bay standing slightly forward of its neighbours, a 1-window range with a semicircular-arched doorway with projecting keystone and capitals; above, a plat band running to the far western end of the facade; a segmental-arched window to the first and second floors. The left-hand element a 3-window range with plinth, one right-hand ground-floor segmental-arched window; to first and second floors, each with 3 segmental-arched openings, only the right-hand open as windows. Pilaster at left-hand end; lamp-iron above plat band near same end. Brick chimney stacks.
Braikenridge notes (19) that in January 1821 contractors began to take down the prison. O'Neill made several drawings during the demolition, showing exteriors and interiors, including the governor's house, the chapel (1047A), etc. He mentions the 'Tennis Court', also "the Bath" (1046A). There were a number of semicircular or segmental-arched openings containing cross-windows or narrow windows with one light top and bottom. All windows were heavily barred. In the yard in front of the governor's house were two pumps, originally separated by a tall wall, and probably using the one well. Off one yard was a 'room for Refractory debtors'. The chapel was galleried on 3 sides; during demolition an unsuspected cell was discovered behind the pulpit and reading desk.
On the end of the prison facing east, O'Neill's view from the north (BRSMG M2718) shows what appear to be further remains of the medieval gatehouse, with a round tower against the road, and the base of a similar structure at the rear.
The National Monuments Record holds a file on the Newgate Prison (NMR File No. 90991).
