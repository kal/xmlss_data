monument_desc Located on the Castle Precincts, the site of The George was a 24m wide plot of land located at the western end of Castle Street. The site extended backwards from the street to the river. The plot of land was laid by the Corporation, who had bought the land in 1656. The site of the inn was originally a house in the Castle. Latimer quotes the resolution of the Council: "Whereas the Castle is demolished, and a common street and highway made therein. And whereas there was formally a house in the Castle called the George inn. A new house having been built on part of the old site, and it being very commodious for entertaining men and horses, Ordered that the said house be used as a common inn and hostelry." The Merchant Venturers became the owners of The George in 1686. At this time there were three tenants: Walter Lucas, innholder; Edward Hutton, distiller and William Davis, tobacconist.

George Woolham was the tenant from 1716. John Whittock was the occupant from 1722 to 1731. John Dibble and his widow, followed by John Russell and his widow, and then Nathaniel Ridley were successive tenants from 1731 to 1743. Charles Atkinson occuped the premises from 1731 until at least 1749. John Wolf was the occupant in 1753 and was succeeded by his widow. S. Wolf is recorded as the tenant in 1775, followed by George Cooper a few years later. Richard Cox was occupant to Mary Hilsonte, proprietor, in early 1800. In 1806, the premises were occupied by Davis and Company. Ball Davis and Co were the occupiers in 1810.

By 1820, Richard and Charles Vaughen were the proprietors/occupants, with the premises described as a Wool Warehouse. The Coalbrookdale Company used the buildings as an iron warehouse from 1829, the premises being described as "shop and warehouse" in the 1850's and 1860's. The 1883 OS survey at 1:500 scale showed most of the site as a warehouse but with only half of the 24m street frontage. The remainder was occupied by two smaller premises (nos. 83 & 84). Later OS plans (1:2500 scale) show the building with a long covered haulingway running virtually the length of site. By this time Lindrea and Company, leather makers and importers were at this address, remaining here until the construction of the Regent Cinema in the later 1920's.
