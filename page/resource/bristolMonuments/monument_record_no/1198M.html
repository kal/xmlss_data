monument_desc Richmond House or Abbot's House, located on the east side of Stoney Hill. Research by Dr. Roger Leech indicates that the plot was leased from the Dean and Chapter of Bristol Cathedral in 1631 by Thomas Hobson, and a lodge already stood on the site at this time (BRO DC/E/40/57/51706). The lodge was replaced by two banqueting houses which were in the possession of Thomas Wells by 1649. Wells gained a new lease in 1661 (BRO DC/E/40/57/51706) and Richmond House was constructed c.1664.
The building was of rubble construction (in Brandon Hill grit), three storeys with an attic, cellar and cross-gable pantile roof. It appears to have had a substantial chimney stack on the south side with at least three flues. A further stack was positioned at the end of the gable on the north side of the building. There were two principal rooms on each floor, known to have been panelled inside with decorative plasterwork on the ceilings. The principal room on the first-floor was drawn by Samuel Loxton in the early twentieth century and contained a large chimneypiece and the walls had panelling to dado height. The ceiling had decorative platerwork with a large figure of abundance at the centre. Within the building there were also decorated chimneypieces, one with the initials "WTE" and the date 1664 (R. H. Leech pers. comm.; Leech in Lawrence, 2003 79-82). Frederick Jones reports a subterranean passage within the building but this is more likely to be the remains of the cellar (Jones 1946 182).
The building was gutted, apparently as a result of bombing during the Second World War, and was demolished in the 1940s. The Trenchard Street multistorey car park was built over the site in the 1960s.
