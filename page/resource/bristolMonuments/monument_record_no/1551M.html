monument_desc No.77-78 Broad Quay, located at the west corner of Broad Quay and Thunderbolt Street. The site was created by the infilling of Aldsworth's Dock in the mid-seventeenth century and a building is recorded on the site by Millerd's 1673 map of Bristol.
The original form of the building is unknown but it appears to have been refronted in the late-eighteenth or early-nineteenth century. It was by then a three-storey rendered building of four bays with rusticated ashlar quoins and a hipped pantile roof. The main, north-facing, elevation in Thunderbolt Street consisted of a shopfront with the entrance towards the eastern side. There were three large shop windows on either side of the entrance at ground-floor level, with a third at the west end. A string course divided the ground from the first floor and the fenestration above comprised four tall six over nine pane sash window. The third floor was also marked by a string course with four smaller six over six pane sashes. The side, west-facing, elevation in Broad Quay had three entrance doorways, one blocked at the north end of the elevation with a pedimented canopy, another with a recessed door set in an opening with a plain segmental-arch at the centre. There was a similar doorway at the south end of the elevation and immediately to its north there was a small cellar light. Between these doorways, on both the north and south side of the central door, there was a large shop window. A string course divided the ground from the first floor and at first-floor level the fenestration consisted of tall nine over nine pane sash windows, with two in the south half of the elevation and a single sash in the north half. There was a smaller window between the large sashes, within its head well above the others, possibly lighting a stair. At first floor there were two smaller single nine over nine pane sashes. The eaves of the roof were marked by dentils which continued around the façade of No.79 Broad Quay (BUAD 1395M). There was a small brick chimneystack in the north elevation and a larger stack, the base of which was of stone rubble and the upper part brick, projecting from the party wall with No.79 Broad Quay.
The Goad fire insurance plan of January 1887 records the building as a chandlers dealing in ships stores with a sail loft. By the 1890s it was owned by Tratman Brothers who advertised themselves as canvas and rope merchants, a general ship stores and sail lofts on the front of the building (Winstone, 1960 pls.62-63). The building was apparently demolished soon after the closure of Alderskey Lane in 1900 (Pritchard, 1904 128-129) and the site was subsequently redeveloped for the Co-operative Wholesale building.
