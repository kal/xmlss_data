monument_desc No.92 St,. Thomas Street, located on the west side of St. Thomas Street, to the south of Three Queens Lane. The building was a timber-framed, jettied and gabled house of probable later seventeenth- or early eighteenth-century date. It was of two storeys and attic and had a single bay. The building appears to have had a pantiled cross-gable roof. By the end of the nineteenth century at ground-floor level the main, east-facing, elevation on St. Thomas Street had a main entrance door on its northern side and a small window at the centre of the elevation. There was a timber stall riser with painted lettering fixed to the exterior. The first-floor was jettied and had a single small four by three pane sash window at the centre. There was another sash at the centre of the attic gable and the eaves were protected by plain wooden barge boards. The main entrance door off St. Thomas Street gave access to the passageway which led to a rear yard. A rainwater downpipe ran from the north side of the roof across the main elevation immediately below the attic sash and into another downpipe on the south side of the projecting element of the jetty. The elevation was rendered.
Internally the plan consisted of three adjacent rooms with a narrow passageway against the north party wall with No.93 St. Thomas Stret (BUAD 851M). There was a single room against St. Thomas Street. Another room to the west was connected by a single doorway and the rear, western, room had a connecting doorway and a doorway in its north wall to the passageway and yard.
The exact date of construction of the building has not yet been established. The 1800 Land Tax assessment records that the property was owned by William Hopkins (Jackson 2001, 5). At the end of the nineteenth century the building was occupied by Mary Francis, manufacturer, who was producing textile sheeting for awnings, tarpaulins etc. Along with the three properties to the north, No.92 St. Thomas Street was bought by Samuel Hannam for use as a timber yard (Jackson 2001, 5).
According to notes by Philip E. W. Street the building was demolished in about 1934, although the Ordnance Survey 1:1250 plan indicates that a building stood on the same footprint until the early 1950s. The site was cleared for road widening in the late 1950s and much of the site of No.92 St. Thomas Street now lies beneath the highway.
