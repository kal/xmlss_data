monument_desc No.146 Temple Street, located on the western side of Temple Street. The building was a jettied, timber-framed and gabled house of three storeys and attic. It dated to the seventeenth-century.
The main entrance was on the southern side and there was another entrance on the north side. The full width of the first-floor was glazed, with bays at the northern and southern ends of the elevation. At second-floor there was a single bay at the centre of the elevation, containing two eight-light mullion and transom windows at the front, with two-light windows at the sides. The attic storey was jettied and the eaves of the roof are supported by brackets. There was a six-light mullion and transom window in the gable end.
The building is reported to have been the residence of Sir John Knight, Mayor of Bristol in 1663. In the eighteenth-century the building was used as a Synagogue. This use continued until 1786. Features and plate from the Synagogue were retained and have been reused in the Synagogue in Lower Park Row [BUAD 1033M]. The building was painted by Hugh O'Neill in 1819 (BRSMG M2153) but appears to have been substantially altered later in the nineteenth century. The Ordnance Survey first edition 1:500 scale Town plan surveyed in 1883 (Bristol Sheets LXXV.4.5 and LXXVI.1.1) suggests that the entire plot had been developed and the original building extended. The Goad fire insurance plan of January 1887 confirms that all of the internal walls had been demolished, although the party walls probably survived. At that date the building was part of the provision warehouse of W. Clark & Sons. The building was still in use as a provision warehouse in the late 1930s but was badly damaged by bombing during the Second World War and had been demolished by the late 1940s.
