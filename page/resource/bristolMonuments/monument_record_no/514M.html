monument_desc The Society of Friends or Quaker burial ground at Redcliff Pit. During the middle ages the site belonged to the Hospital of St. John the Baptist and was known as Redrock Garden. A hermitage was established at the south end of the garden (BUAD 627M). The hospital was dissolved in March 1544 and its lands were surrendered to the Crown. The site, and much of the associated property, was sold to Dr. George Owen, physician to Henry VIII and a Fellow of Merton College, Oxford. In 1665 the freehold of the site was acquired by the Society of Friends and a burial ground was established. By the nineteenth century the burial ground was set out in rows with stone markers for each. An Order in Council of 18 February 1854 made under the Burial Act of 1852 imposed the restriction that "only one body [was] to be buried in each grave" on the burial ground.
In the later-nineteenth or early-twentieth century a uniform series of markers was created, each of sawn slate approximately 100 millimetres thick with a name carved into the face. The burial ground continued to be used until 1923. In 1927 the "gravestones were removed and placed behind a fence in front of the Hermit's Cell" (WRO 1699(28A) quoted in Stock 1996, 6).
The north end of the burial ground, including the entrance gate at that end, was lost when the inner circuit road was created in the late 1930s. A large water main was also laid across the site which uncovered lead coffins of probable late eighteenth- or early nineteenth-century date (BRO 40804(1)). These were left in situ. The site was given to the City of Bristol in 1950 and the graves were subject to clearance in 1956, the human remains being reinterred at Avon View Cemetery in grave numbers 1357-1358 and 1449-1450 Purple AA (Stock 1996, 7).
