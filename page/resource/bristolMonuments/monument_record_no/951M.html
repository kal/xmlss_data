monument_desc The Bishop's Park, located on the south-east side of modern St. George's Road. Elements of the park may be medieval in origin, since this was part of the estate of the adjacent Abbey of St. Augustine. At the creation of the diocese of Bristol in 1542 the lands of the former abbey were divided between the Bishop and the Dean and Chapter. The Abbot's Lodging and the Abbot's Park were granted to the Bishop. The park is recorded by James Millerd's 1673 map while Jean Rocque's 1742 'A Plan of the City of Bristol' shows the "BISHOP'S PARK" bounded by Cow or Limekiln Lane, the Rope Walk at Canons Marsh, and the rears of buildings fronting College Green and Lower College Green. Towards the south-western end of the park the plan shows three ponds (BUAD 1060A-1062A), the northern (a fishpond?) being the largest, triangular in shape, perhaps dammed at its southern end, while the others were irregular, one of them connected with the large drainage ditch that ran along the northern edge of Canons Marsh. No evidence of the largest pond was found during archaeological excavation on its putative site on the south side of Deanery Road.
In March 1770 the then Bishop of Bristol, Thomas Gooch, leased the Park to Samuel Worrall who was to develop it with housing: "The said close of ground should be improved by buildings to be erected theron and whereas the said Samuel Worrall hath formed a plan for covering the said close of ground with buildings to which plan there is a street called College Street of the breadth of forty feet from front to front of the second story of the intended houses out of which is taken three feet for areas or bow windows on the first story or ground floor and five feet for a paved foot-way which leaves twenty four feet carriage. " (BRO DC/E./40/28/2). The main sewer to serve the housing was reported to have been completed in September 1770 (Ison 1952, 210) and 1775 eight houses in College Street were occupied according to Sketchley's directory of Bristol. The leases on the individual properties reverted to the Bishop and in 1853 he issued a new lease to William Tanner, a property speculator (BRO 21054(2)). The Bishop sold his interest in the Park to Tanner in May 1871 (BRO 21054(7)). The land remained in a single ownership until June 1950 when the individual plots composing the estate were sold at auction (BRO 21054 (17)).
