monument_desc The Custom House, located on the north side of Queen Square (at the east corner of King William Avenue). Construction commenced in 1709 and the original building was burnt down during the 1831 Bristol Riots. It was rebuilt in 1836 to a design by Sidney Smirke.
The original building was of three storeys with a full-width portico carried on six columns; rusticated quoins; sashes to all openings, with tall first-floor windows with 12/12 panes, otherwise 8/8 panes to windows; aprons below all windows.
Limestone ashlar, with ridge and party wall stacks.
Double-depth plan. Neoclassical style. 2 storeys, attic and basement; 5-window range; symmetrical front. A large doorway has paired pilasters to an entablature, with a plinth above with raised ends, and a carving of the Royal Coat of Arms; 9-pane overlight and 2-leaf 4-panel door; tall semicircular-arched first-floor windows. Left-hand return has 3-window range, with 3/3-pane attic sashes set below the cornice, and basement windows with grille to pavement lights. Rear has projecting right-hand section, and gabled left-hand with semicircular-arched windows. There are traces in basement of the early 18th.century house destroyed in the riots, with tooled Pennant walls and timbers, and security doors.
