monument_desc The "Spunging House", located on the west side of Pithay, on the north side of the junction with Tower Lane. The building was a three-storey, two-bay house of probable mid seventeenth-century date and occupied a plot measuring 14.25 metres east-west by 5 metres north to south. The main elevation faced south-east on to Pithay. The ground floor was apparently a shopfront with a central entrance doorway. A full-width pentice roof, apparently covered with slates, divided ground from first-floor. The upper floors of the east end of the building extended over Tower Lane, forming a frontage 7.5 metres wide at first-floor level on Pithay. The fenestration at first and second floors consisted of continuous twin bays with panelling in the base and Ipswich windows above. The bays were apparently roofed with lead. The divison between the floors was marked by a moulded string course and between the second-floor windows is the arms of the Brewers' Company moulded in plasterwork. Immediately above there was a sunburst decoration. At attic level in the centre of the elevation there was a smaller bay which rested on the corners of the second-floor bays and contained an Ipswich window. Above the bay, immediately beneath the apex of the eaves there was a sunburst decoration. The side, south-west facing, elevation on Tower Lane had a window opening on Tower Lane and there was a rainwater downpipe immediately to the east of this. The building has a gabled pantile roof with a cross-gable on the southern part of the roof only. There was apparently only a single purlin on each side of the main roof.
The early history of the building is currently unknown but presumably during the late eighteenth- and early-nineteenth centuries the building was used as a Spunging House. In 1829 G. W. Braikenridge noted that the building was "formerly used to lock up Persons when first arrested for Debt & [was] called the Spunging House" (Notes, M82). A watercolour painting of that year in the Braikenridge Collection of Bristol Museum and Art Gallery executed by T. L. Rowbotham (BRSMG M2373) records the building and shows a painted signboard bearing the legend "CUMMINS/UPHOLSTERER" over the entry to Tower Lane. Cummins is listed as an upholsterer at No.1 Pithay in contemporary street directories. The painting also shows many trunks and coats displayed outside the building, suggesting that the ground-floor shop may have been used for the trade in ready-made or second-hand clothes.
The building was demolished in March 1898 according to John Pritchard (Pritchard 1900-03, 259) but a photograph by T. Protheroe at the end of the 1890s (BUAD 2651) shows the remains of the northern party wall. The site of the building now lies beneath the entrance to the car park at the rear of The Pithay office building.
