monument_desc Church of St. Werburgh. The church stood on the south-west corner of Small Street and Corn Street. The date of foundation of the church is unknown; the dedication to St. Werburgh has traditionally been taken to imply a Saxon origin. However, the cult of St. Werburga apparently underwent a revival after the translation of the saint's remains to Ely in 1095 (Ridyard 1988, 186n) and the dedication may therefore post-date the Norman Conquest. The church is first directly recorded in a charter of 1148-1183.
The four-stage tower (with stair turret in north-west corner) was on the south, or Corn Street, side. On the west side at second-stage level was one two-light arched window. Upper half with two-light traceried panels or blind arches, three to each stage, the central ones with some openings.
The east end was taken down, and the church truncated, in 1759 to improve access into Small Street (Evans, 1824 278). At this time some of the monuments were removed to William Reeves house at Arnos Court.
When the church re-opened in 1762, the north elevation comprised five bays, with a short sixth bay at the east end. Large windows with geometrical tracery; no window in eastern bay. Porch in second bay from west, ogee-headed arch over entrance, possible room above porch; walls with cinquefoil-headed panels. 3-stage buttresses to church. A small plain single-storey building against east end of the elevation.
Arcades of two-centred arches marked the division of the two aisles, which were either side of nave. 4-light window high in west end of nave.
The interior, looking west, was recorded by Johnson in 1828 (M2458) when there were box pews in nave. Other details, including the Barker monument (recorded by Cashin and Manning (M2461)). Part of a monument of Elizabethan or Jacobean date was drawn by O'Neill, 1820 (M3450) A blank end wall to Small Street, at least at its northern end where one gable end is recorded.
The church was demolished in 1878 to enable Small Street to be widened. A public meeting chaired by the Lord Mayor in March 1876 argued to retain at least the tower (Anon. 1876, 250) but the Bristol Corporation resolved by 30 votes to 23 to demolish the entire structure in December 1876 (Anon. 1876, 1270). The site was sold to South Western Bank for £15130 and subsequently redeveloped as a bank. Elements of the building were re-used in a new church at Mina Road (Listed Grade II*). The medieval font, which had been stored in the tower was reported, at the end of the nineteenth century, to have been removed to the garden of a house on the Bath Road.
