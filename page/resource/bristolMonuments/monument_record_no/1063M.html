monument_desc A stone culvert of ninetenth-century date running parallel to the northern side of Wellington Road, which is entered via manhole cover in the garden of The Phoenix public house.
The culvert runs from the public house west, beneath the site of the Broadweir Baths (now demolished). The only section so far archaeologically inspected is approximately 75 metres long and is built of Pennant Sandstone bonded in a black mortar containing fragments of clay tobacco pipe and rare brick fragments. The culvert is 3 metres wide and a maximum of 2.5 metres high (estimated). The arch of the culvert springs from different levels, with the base of the arch being approximately 0.3 metres lower on the south side than on the north. The floor of the culvert is concave and appears to be constructed of Pennant Sandstone at the edges and brick in the centre, although this is not confirmed as the floor of the culvert is covered with several centimetres of sediment. Overall the section of culvert available for inspection follows a sinuous course, curving slightly north and then turning southward again.
At the east end of the culvert excavation work, probably undertaken in the 1970s and associated with the construction of the car park, had broken through the arch of the culvert. It has been infilled with soil, completely blocking it, but no attempt has been made to repair the arch. Part of the floor of the culvert at the east end has been grouted with concrete. A manhole has been inserted in the centre of the length, and below this again is another manhole serving a further drain beneath the culvert. To the west of the manhole the roof has been replaced, at some time during the twentieth-century, with the insertion of reinforced concrete sections. At the far western end the culvert has been cut off by a brick wall with a concrete pipe through it at ground level. There is no direct water flow through the culvert.
The culvert is clearly not the main channel of the river Frome, which is further to the south, but its function is unknown. The two channels are shown on Millerd's 1673 map but may originally be earlier in date.
