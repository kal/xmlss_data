monument_desc St. Augustine's Abbey, College Green. The abbey may stand upon land with an earlier religious use, the late-Saxon Harrowing of Hell carving having been found (reused as a grave slab) in the vestibule of the Chapter House in the early nineteenth-century.
The abbey was founded by Robert Fitzharding in 1140. The house was Augustinian, part of the Victorine section of the order, and the first abbot came from the abbey of St. Victor in Paris after which the section was named. The Romanesque abbey gateway, the gateway in the Registrar's House and the Chapter House are the surviving elements which date to the inital phase of building.
The precinct appears to have occupied the area between College Green and Canon's Marsh. The area to the south and west of the cloister appears to have had a more agricultural function, with a possible barn (BUAD 1456M) and dovecote (BUAD 1457M) lying to the western side of College Square and another dovecote (BUAD 1215M) close to modern Trinity Steps.
The abbey was dissolved on 9 December 1539 when the abbot and eleven canons were present. There were also forty-six paid servants. The abbey church was taken over as the Cathedral for the city and the administrative centre of the new diocese of Bristol which was created on 4 June 1542.
