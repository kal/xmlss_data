monument_desc Ashley Court or Ashley Barnes, located on the western side of Ashley Hill. The date of origin of the building is currently unknown but it was apparently in existence by the seventeenth century since a drawing of the main, south-east facing, elevation of April 1746 by James Stewart (Bodleian Library, MS Gough Somerset 2 & 8) shows the northern part of the building with a gabled roof typical of local houses at that date. The house was apparently part of the Ashley estate which was purchased by Andrew Hooke, a Bristol brewer, in the 1660s and remained in the Hooke family until November 1731 when Hooke's grandson, also named Andrew, sold the whole estate to the distiller George Bridges. Bridges apparently lived in the house and at his death in 1733 he bequeathed the estate to his son William. William Bridges died in 1741 and the estate passed to his sisters Ann, married to William Champion and Mary. George Bridges widow Mary remarried (to Jacob Elton) in the year of his death but continued to live at Ashley Court until her death in 1775.
The house was refronted in a Classical style in the first half of the eighteenth century. It was recorded on a plan of Ashley by William Lewellin of 1759 (BRO 4964(25)). This indicated that there were ranges at the northern and southern corners of the house which extended north-west to create an enclosed rear courtyard. There was a formal parterre garden in front of the house, enclosed by a stone wall with tall central piers. The plot behind was an orchard. To the east of the house was an orchard known as "Lower Rookery" with a building towards its northern end. A tree-lined avenue (now part of Ashley Hill) ran north-east from the house.
The house had been bought by Edward Ash in the early years of the nineteenth century and his son Richard sold it to John Evans Lunell, a West India Merchant, in 1825 (BRO 4964(66-67)). William Player lived at Ashley Court from 1852 and continued to occupy the house until 1877 when it was sold to James Derham, a property developer. The house was demolished soon afterwards and much of the area of the formal gardens was developed for housing, including the rectory of St. Werburgh's.
The large rusticated limestone ashlar piers on the north side of Ashley Court Road are extant and have a Grade II listing.
