monument_desc The grounds of Brentry House, Brentry Hill. William Payne commissioned Humphry Repton to create a landscape park for his new house at Brentry Hill in 1802. The work was one of Repton's later commissions, relatively small in scale and taking the main house as the focus for the landscape. The Reptons placed the house carefully to exploit the topography of the site, emphasising the views of the Bristol Channel to the west and screening those of Westbury-on-Trym to the south by planting evergreen trees (Daniels 1999, 236). Repton created a grotto which is now lost, a view of the house from inside the grotto being published in 1803 (reproduced in Harding and Lambert 1994, 64). A mound with evergreen planting close to the drive has been suggested as its likely site (Land Use Consultants 1999, 7). When the work had been completed Repton wrote that "few houses have been built with more attention to the situation and circumstances of the place than the villa at Brentry" (Harding and Lambert, 1994 64). Repton's son John Adey Repton designed Brentry House at the same time, one of only two occasions where father and son produced complimentary designs. The house had passed into the hands of John Cave, a member of the Bristol banking family, by the early 1820s and Cave expanded both the house (by adding new ranges) and its grounds. This work took land from the fields on the south side of the original line of Charlton Road (part of which survives as a lane within the hospital) and created the new, existing, line to the south. An entry in the Gloucestershire Quarter Sessions records for 1825 confirms authorisation of this work and another, in the same year, its subsequent inspection and acceptance (Brett 1999). It has been argued that the sinuous carriage drive was created during the 1825 (Land Use Consultants 1999, 7) and the 1825 work appears to have cut the grotto off from the house, both physically and in terms of the landscape design. It may be for this reason that it was taken down. On the north side of the site is a quarried edge. The origin, date and purpose of this is uncertain but the suggestion has been made that it may represents diversion of a public path to enhance the privacy of the house and was undertaken either by Repton or by Cave (Land Use Consultants 1999, 7).
The landscape is included in the English Heritage Register of Historic Parks and Gardens where it has a Grade II listing although it has subsequently been altered by planting, building for the retreat and subsequently the hospital which occupied the site and the widening of Passage Road and the junction with Charlton Road which removed land from the park after the Second World War. Generally "the early 19th century landscape has been respected. Demand for built space has clearly impinged on the landscape and the land use, but it is fortunate that the essential elements of the designed landscape have been relatively unaffected" (Land Use Consultants 1999, 9).
Relatively little of the pre-Repton landscape can be identified, but a possible trackway of unknown purpose runs north-east from the drive leading to Charlton Road (Brett 1999). In the area now occupied by St. Peter's Hospice an area of ridge and furrow was identified by Bryant (Bryant 1995).
