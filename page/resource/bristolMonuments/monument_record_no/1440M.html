monument_desc The White Hart public house, No.95 St. Thomas Street, located on the west side of St. Thomas Street. The building was a rendered timber-framed, two-bay house of two storeys and attic and probably dated to the later seventeenth- or early eighteenth-century.
The main, east-facing, elevation on St. Thomas Street had a main entrance on is south side with an original narrow window immediately adjacent to it. On the north side of the building was a pair of large wooden gates and this gave access to a haulingway which led to a rear yard. The first floor was jettied and contained two six over six pane sash windows. A projecting feature which extended across the full width of the frontages of Nos.93-95 divided second from attic storey. The first-floor was jettied and had a window opening at the centre. The opening in the attic gable was a slightly smaller and contained a central casement window. The building had a pantiled roof which was half-hipped to the front.
The exact date of construction of the building has not yet been established. The Land Tax assessment of 1800 records that the property was owned by William Hopkins. By 1807 the stoneware pottery of Morgan, Walker and Company had been established at No.95 according to Mathew's Bristol Directory for 1807. The company became W. W. Walker and Company between 1808 and 1815 but it was taken over by William and Thomas Powell in 1816. Powells continued to occupy the site until the operation was moved to the company's site at Temple Gate (BUAD 660M) in 1830 (Jackson, 2001 5). By 1870 the building had become the White Hart public house and an opening had been created at the centre of the party wall to connect the building with No.94 (BUAD 1439M) by 1887, although it is not clear whether this alteration was associated with the use as a public house or as a pottery. Along with the three properties to the south, the White Hart was bought by Samuel Hannam for use as a timber yard and, along with No.94, was subsequently demolished to make way for a new brick warehouse (Jackson, 2001). The site was still being used as a timber yard by the early 1950s but the eastern part was was taken for widening of St. Thomas Street in the late 1950s. Much of the site of No.95 St. Thomas Street now lies beneath the highway.
