monument_desc The Merchants or Merchant Seamen's Almshouse, located on the north side of King Street. An almshouse was established on, or close to, the site in the very late-fifteenth or early-sixteenth century by the Guild of Mariners, which also founded the adjacent Chapel of St. Clement in 1493. The form of the almshouse is currently unknown - whether a separate building or accommodation within the chapel - but a kitchen for the almshouse is referred to in a seventeenth-century document, suggesting that it may have been a separate structure close to the chapel building. The almshouse was presumably also served by the priest for the chapel and was funded by a levy on goods and a deduction from the wages of Bristol's mariners which continued to be levied until at least 1595. The Guild of Mariners was dissolved in the mid-sixteenth century and the chapel of St. Clement and the Mariner's Almshouse were taken over by the Society of Merchant Venturers. The almshouse then had twelve residents and subsequently the funds for its maintenance were provided out of the Society's own resources (McGrath 1975, 81).
The present almshouse building was established in 1696 by the Society of Merchant Venturers. The building enclosed a quadrangle, with full ranges on the north, east and west sides and the southern range split by a main entrance at the centre of that side. There was a uniform for those accepted into the almshouse consisting of a long dark blue overcoat with a blue-green lining and a hat (BRSMG M2502).
The almshouse was damaged by blast in an air raid on 2 December 1940. Another raid on 14 May 1941 badly damaged the western range, and south-western range and the western end of the north range.
The almshouse has a Grade II* listing (Listed Building number 901-1/16/595).
