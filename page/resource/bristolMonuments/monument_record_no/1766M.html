monument_desc Southmead barrow, a Bronze Age round barrow located on the northern side of Bowness Gardens, Southmead, adjacent to the playing fields of Doncaster Road Primary School. The earliest reference to the monument is probably by the antiquarian Samuel Seyer (1821, 102). The monument was partially excavated, in December 1873, by Montgomery (SMR 20757) who excavated a section four feet (1.2 metres) wide "from the south side to the centre" (Anon. 1875). An archaeological evaluation trench was excavated on the east side of the monument by the University of Bristol Spelaeological Society in 1922 or 1923 (Tratman 1946, 169-171). Grinsell visited the monument on 9 May 1952 measuring it as 20 paces across and standing 7 feet [just over 2 metres] high (O'Neil & Grinsell 1960, 100).
The lack of a formal report either by Montgomery or by Tratman (the archive for the latter work reportedly having been destroyed by bombing during the Second World War) means that detailed interpretation of the monument is currently difficult. However, it appears to have consisted of a central earth mound, possibly of turf, with a surrounding outer ring of stone. Montgomery recovered human remains, including fragments of a skull, thereby confirming the funerary nature of the monument. No firm dating evidence has yet been recovered, although worked flint was recovered by both Montgomery and Tratman. Tratman suggested a Middle Bronze Age date for the monument by analogy with a similar barrow excavated in Glamorgan (Tratman 1946, 170-171). It has been suggested, apparently on the basis of the name of the field within which the monument lies - "Mill Tut field" - that the barrow was later reused as the mound for a windmill.
The monument is suffering some wear, with an erosion track probably formed by children's bicycles running from east to west across the top of the monument. The barrow is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM No. AV 27).
