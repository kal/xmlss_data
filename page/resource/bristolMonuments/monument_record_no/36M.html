monument_desc The Hartshorn Inn, later known as the Swan Inn, Nos.26 and 27 Mary le Port Street, located on the south side of Mary le Port Street, close to the corner with Dolphin Street. The building was erected in 1434
By 1423 these belonged to Bernard Brewer (Leech 1997, 105). In 1451 John Wylly, a brewer, occupied the southern house and by 1475 it was occupied by William Bruer. These names imply that brewing was probably taking place. A document of 1495 confirms that a brewhouse was present of the site and that an inn or tavern known as the "herteshorne" had been established (Leech 1997, 106).
The building was refronted (and possibly rebuilt) in the seventeenth-century. It possessed a gabled front to Mary-le-Port Street. Richard Robins was being taxed for the Swan by 1745 and continued there well into the 1750's (Land Tax returns; McGraph & Williams 1947, 47). Widow Preston was at the Swan by about 1760. Sketchley's directory indicates that in 1775 the Swan Inn was run by Henry Selden. Edward and then Amelia Cuddeford succeeded Seldon. By 1786 the property consisted of Nos.26 & 27 Mary-le-port Street. Sarah Applin was the occupant of the "Swan Inn & Tavern" in 1823 (First Rate). William Gray was at the "Swan Inn and Spirit Vault" in 1839, also in 1849 (ibid). Charles Osborne was running the Swan in the 1870's, bu which time it was now being described as a hotel. It was demolished (along with the property adjacent on the east, on the corner of Mary le Port Street and Dolphin Street) in January 1936. It was replaced by a modern concrete, brick and glass structure with metal-framed windows. This new building was dstoyed in the evening of November 24th, 1940 by bombing.
