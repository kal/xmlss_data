monument_desc Druid Stoke burial chamber, on the south side of the garden of No.59 Druid Hill, Stoke Bishop. The monument is a neolithic long barrow (Tratman 1946, 165), probably with transeptal terminal chambers (Darvill & Grinsell 1989, 64), and now consists of a number of stones lying at the edge of the drive to the south of the house set in a small gravel garden. One large (cap?) stone leans against a stone set in the ground and on the north side there is another lying nearly flat. No evidence of any surrounding mound or cairn now survives above ground.
The monument was identified by Rev. John Skinner at the end of 1811, who noted "the Capstone of a demolished Cromlech", and made further visits to the site - one in 1818-1819 and another possibly in 1826 (British Library Add. Mss 33643, fo.79r & v fo.80 (sketch); Grinsell 1979, 119). The monument was also described by Seyer (1821, 103). The area around the stones was excavated in 1913 by Francis Were and Lewis Way of the Bristol and Gloucestershire Archaeological Society, though little information was recovered (Were 1913; SMR 20945).
Grinsell reviewed the evidence for the monument in 1979 (Grinsell 1979). It was subject to geophysical survey (SMR 20445) by the Ancient Monuments Laboratory (Clark in Smith 1989, 37) in December 1982 in advance of work to the surrounding garden, and this survey was followed by an archaeological evaluation (SMR 20444) by the Central Excavation Unit of English Heritage in the same year (Smith 1989). The 1983 evaluation, however, found a spread of limestone rubble around the stones which was thought to be the remains of the original mound. A human to bone was also found to the south of the stones and this produced a calibrated radiocarbon date of 2889 to 2460 BC at a 95% confidence. Analysis of a buried soil beneath the rubble produced molluscan evidence to suggest that the monument had been constructed in a woodland environment. It appears from finds evidence at one side of the monument that the mound which had surrounded the stones was likely to have been removed by Roman-British and later ploughing. However, Darvill and Grinsell (1989, 64) note that an "extensive" mound surrounded the monument until c1907 when it was reduced in height.
The burial chamber is a Scheduled Ancient Monument (SAM AV 28)
